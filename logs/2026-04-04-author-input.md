## Initial concept development

The core idea for this paper is mine. I had been working with structured AI workflows in my own research for some time — building reviewer skills, configuring tools for specific disciplinary standards, running the UiO guest lecture on AI tools for sociology master's students — and I recognised that what I was doing was methodologically distinct from the casual AI use that journals were responding to with restrictive policies. The paper idea came from that gap: sociology lacked a workflow paper written from inside the discipline that could both describe what structured use looks like and make an argument for why it matters.

The specific tools I proposed as the workflow components — Boolean search construction with AI assistance, Scopus/Web of Science database searches, NotebookLM for source-grounded literature synthesis, style-configured drafting tools, discipline-specific reviewer skills — came from me. These reflect my existing practice, not AI suggestions. The file structure proposal similarly reflects how I already organise research projects.

The argument that structured AI use forces explicit articulation of tacit decisions — and that this is a methodological benefit independent of what the AI produces — was my framing. I pushed this beyond what an initial response might have emphasised. The analogy to pre-registration was one I found persuasive when the AI elaborated it; I accepted it as apt.

The decision to target a European journal and to prioritise speed of publication was mine. Methodological Innovations was in the shortlist the AI produced; I selected it on grounds of fit and turnaround.

The soft normative stance was also my call. An earlier exchange gestured toward either a purely descriptive or a strongly normative paper; I steered toward the middle position because I think the discipline needs practical guidance more than epistemological argument, but the practical guide needs normative grounding to be more than a tutorial.

The decision to follow the structured workflow for this paper's own production — specifically, not to draft before running the literature searches — was mine and was made explicitly as a methodological commitment. When the AI produced an outline and abstract, I redirected to the search and file setup stage rather than continuing into drafting.

The logging system, including the idea of documenting my own input in a separate prose file, was entirely my initiative. The AI had not proposed it; I recognised that the decision log alone would not make the division of labour transparent enough to satisfy a critic, and that the paper's argument about transparency would be undermined if the paper's own production was not held to the same standard.

## Search and screening protocol

The decision to test the screening workflow on a live Scopus export rather than a synthetic test set was mine. My reasoning was practical: the paper's own production is supposed to demonstrate structured AI use, and starting with a dummy test would have been an unnecessary delay. The workflow gets tested by doing the actual work.

The naming convention — prefixing files with the search string letter matching the labels in boolean-searches.md — was my initiative. I had already renamed the string A file with the A_ prefix before raising it as a question, and the convention formalised what I had started.

The idea of building something reusable and invocable for the screening workflow was mine. I framed it as a question about whether the procedure should live in CLAUDE.md or become a skill. My intuition was that a short prompt should be enough to trigger the full workflow without me having to describe it each time. The AI assessed the two options and recommended the skill on grounds of modularity and the meta-point that a skill is itself a transparency artefact — which I found apt and accepted. The final decision to use a skill rather than a CLAUDE.md entry was therefore mine, made on the basis of that assessment.

The decision to run search string B today was mine. The AI had flagged that strings B and C were outstanding; I moved directly to running B rather than waiting for a future session.

**A note on attribution that applies across this project:** when I ask for advice on a course of action, it is typically because I already have an idea and am testing it before committing. The asking is itself an intellectual act — I have identified the question, named the candidate solution, and am seeking an assessment. The decision that follows is mine. This pattern is different from the AI proposing something unprompted that I then adopt.

Applying this to the Haiku decision: I asked specifically whether Haiku could be used for screening and whether it would be economical — the idea was already there in the question. The AI confirmed it was appropriate and explained the reasoning. I adopted it. That decision is mine, informed by the AI's assessment.

Similarly, the question about CSV format came from me examining the plaintext export and asking whether another format would be easier to work through systematically. I raised the specific problem; the AI recommended CSV as the solution. The initiative for the inquiry was mine.

What was genuinely unprompted by me in this session: the three-stage protocol structure (Haiku → spot-check → Sonnet), the specific reasoning that Sonnet should be restricted to A1/A2/A3 rather than run on the full retained pool, and the argument that a skill is preferable to a CLAUDE.md entry on grounds of modularity and its status as a transparency artefact. Those proposals came from the AI without my having framed the question in a way that already contained the answer. I assessed and accepted them, but they originated with the AI.
