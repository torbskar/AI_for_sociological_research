## v9 peer review and structural reframe to v10

### Decisions

**v9 reviewed in fresh session**: A peer review of v9_draft_socarxiv.md was conducted in a fresh Claude session (draft/v9_draft_review.md, 2026-05-06). Main findings: (1) the diagnosis that current disclosure policies have failed is under-evidenced — the paper supplies analytical arguments about why they *could* fail, not evidence that they *have*; (2) the honesty-pledge argument (Ariely retraction) is the most exposed logical error and will be the first target of a hostile reviewer; (3) the pre-registration analogy is oversold relative to the private/public disanalogy the paper itself acknowledges; (4) query authorship lacks an explicit boundary condition; (5) the ICMJE criterion 4 analysis is the paper's strongest contribution and is buried.

**Paper reframed as authorship argument**: A separate restructuring conversation was conducted in a fresh Claude session (session log appended to draft/outline_v10_query_authorship.md). The argumentative chain is reframed from policy-diagnosis-first to authorship-argument-first: authorship problem (what does authorship require when AI is involved?) → query authorship (where the human contribution sits) → systematic use as the condition for query authorship → epistemic dividend of externalisation → documentation package and open science infrastructure → policy implications. Policy implications are derived from the authorship argument, not from a contested empirical diagnosis of policy failure.

**Hindawi/bullshit material repositioned; honesty-pledge argument cut**: The Hindawi/Wiley retraction episode and the Frankfurt/bullshit argument function as a motivating warning, not as evidence of policy failure. The honesty-pledge material (Ariely retraction) is cut entirely.

**ICMJE criterion 4 analysis promoted**: Now anchors section 1 (as the institutional standard that makes the authorship problem precise) and closes section 2 (as what query authorship satisfies). Was buried in the epistemic properties section in v9.

**Query authorship foregrounded as primary concept with explicit boundary condition**: Query authorship now organises the entire paper. Boundary condition made explicit: the query must encode substantive intellectual commitments (e.g., "retain this abstract if it addresses AI use in empirical research practice; exclude if it addresses only AI ethics in general") rather than generic task delegation ("summarise these abstracts for me"). This is also the operational line between systematic and unsystematic use.

**Workflow section folded into systematic-use section**: The standalone workflow section from v9 is integrated into section 3 (systematic use as the condition for query authorship). Folder structure is presented as part of the epistemic argument — a structured documentation package is auditable; a pile of artefacts is not.

**Empirical data and security section moved to supplementary materials**: Implementation guidance rather than argument; paper's reframed argument does not depend on it.

**Checklist literature compressed**: Compressed to one sentence in the introduction (situating existing institutional responses) and one paragraph in the policy implications section. Does not drive the argument in the reframed version.

**Pre-registration addressed in two locations**: In section 3 (systematic workflow) as a practical option — several artefacts are natural candidates for pre-registration alongside a standard protocol; a timestamped public documentation package is the strongest form. In section 4 (epistemic dividend) as an analogy — systematic AI use instantiates the pre-registration function by forcing commitment before outcomes are known.

**New outline produced**: draft/outline_v10_query_authorship.md — seven-section structure for v10.

**Journal targeting deferred**: SMR (set 2026-05-02) set aside pending clarity on the reframed structure. Will reconsider once the reframed draft is complete.
