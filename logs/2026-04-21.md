## Scope clarification — human authorship, not AI authorship

### Decisions
- Recorded the author's clarification: the paper does not argue about whether AI should be listed as an author (the post-2023 consensus is settled and the paper does not contest it). The paper addresses the *human* authorship situation when AI executes substantial parts of the work. The human remains the accountable author; the framework's purpose is to produce the evidence that makes that accountability substantive rather than nominal.
- This sharpens revision action G considerably. The move is no longer "ICMJE as motivating analogue" but "criterion 4 applies unchanged; the evidentiary conditions under which a human can substantively satisfy it differ once AI executes the work, and the framework specifies those conditions." The paper and the AI-authorship consensus are on the same side of the criterion-4 distinction; the paper's contribution is downstream.
- The "arguably stronger" line can be repaired without retreat: memory-based reconstruction satisfies criterion 4 for traditional work because the human executed it; it cannot do the same epistemic job when AI executed the work. The framework is not stronger than ICMJE — it is what criterion 4 already requires in a situation its drafters did not face.

### Consequential reasoning
- This also partly discharges N7 (generational re-description) for this specific move: the paper is not reinventing authorship theory; it is making a narrow claim about what an established criterion substantively requires under new conditions.

## Gino retractions as evidence against declaration-based policy

### Decisions
- Incorporated O'Grady (2023), "Honesty papers retracted for data 'discrepancies'," *Science* 381(6655): 255–256 (`literature/pdfs/pdfs_manualy_supplemented/science.adj8305.pdf`), into the framing note.
- The Shu/Mazar/Gino/Ariely 2012 PNAS paper — signing an honesty pledge at the top of a form rather than the bottom produces more honest reporting — was retracted in 2021; two further Gino papers were retracted in 2023. Sanders (King's College London) reports ~$250,000 of field trials built on the 2012 finding, all failed to replicate.
- The empirical foundation for declaration-as-mechanism is not merely thin; its flagship demonstration has been publicly discredited. Disclosure-based AI policy rests on the same underlying causal claim. The paper can use this in the introduction as the most vivid available illustration of why documentation (artefacts generated during the work) is epistemically different in kind from disclosure (statements about the work).
- Placement: one paragraph after the Hindawi anchor, framing the problem at two levels — papermills show disclosure is overwhelmed at scale by bad actors; Gino retractions show the behavioural-science case for declaration-as-mechanism has collapsed among good-faith researchers.

### Consequential reasoning
- Calibration insisted on: the claim is narrow (the specific mechanism by which declarations are supposed to produce compliance has lost its most-cited empirical demonstration), not a dismissal of behavioural economics. State once, cite O'Grady, move on.
