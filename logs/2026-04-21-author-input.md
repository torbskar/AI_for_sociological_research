The introduction, as it stood, risked reading as enthusiasm for a workflow rather than as a methodological response to real problems. I named the problems: AI is already reshaping the scholarly landscape; papermills, unethical use, and bullshit-production from uncritical use are real worries; AI is also here to stay, integrated into everything, and so available that assuming researchers will not use it is naive; there is a continuous slope from editing language to inferring with authorship; declaration of AI use is not enough — we need real documentation and paper trails; sycophancy makes shallow thinking appear deep and reinforces the author's existing views; automated-research projects like APE (https://ape.socialcatalystlab.org/) look like p-hacking at scale; and authorship itself is under practical pressure, not as a philosophical puzzle but as a live problem everyone is struggling with. I asked that this be captured as a note and insisted the introduction must lead with the problem, not the tool.

The scope clarification on ICMJE was my intervention. Claude's first pass on the Elicit authorship summary had treated criterion 4 (personhood-accountability) as a problem for the paper. I pointed out that it is not: the paper does not argue about AI being an author. It argues about the human authorship situation when AI executes a lot of the work. The human is the accountable author; the framework's purpose is to produce the evidence that makes the accountability substantive rather than nominal. This is downstream of the AI-exclusion consensus, not against it. This reframing is, I think, the most important single move of the day — it turns revision action G from a retreat into a constructive specification of what criterion 4 already requires under new conditions.

The irony is available precisely because the field is trust-in-research itself.
