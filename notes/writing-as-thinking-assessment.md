# Writing as thinking — synthesis and assessment for the paper

## Core finding

The NotebookLM responses confirm the initial assessment and sharpen it considerably. The paper's current treatment (lines 151–153 in v7) makes the right claims but lacks the mechanism. The sources provide a well-articulated account of *why* writing forces explicit thinking, and the most important finding from the Q&A is that Sommers (1981) supplies the direct justification for extending the writing-as-thinking claim beyond prose to rubrics and configurations — a move the paper currently makes without support.

---

## What the sources establish and how to use each

### Emig (1977) — the foundational mechanism [3,242 citations]

The most authoritative source and the right foundation. Emig's key concepts:

- **Deliberate semantics**: writing requires "deliberate structuring of the web of meaning" (drawing on Vygotsky). Unlike speech, written language must supply explicit logical relationships — causal, coordinate, subordinate — that speech can leave implicit.
- **Inhibition of synpractical connections**: writing slows cognition and "inhibits immediate synpractical connections," forcing a "much slower, repeated mediating process of analysis and synthesis."
- **Simultaneous self-reviewing structure**: the graphic product allows "immediate, literal re-scanning and review" — a feedback loop that internal monologue and speech cannot produce.

**Use in the paper**: cite for the mechanism. Emig establishes that what makes writing epistemically valuable is precisely that it forces the articulation of what would otherwise remain tacit — connections, relationships, evaluative standards. This is the bridge the paper needs: the same mechanism that operates when a researcher writes a literature review operates when they write an inclusion criterion. Both require deliberate semantics. Both inhibit the lazy shortcut of vague intuition.

### Sommers (1981) — structure as heuristic [44 citations, but conceptually central]

The most direct justification for the extension to configurations and rubrics. Sommers argues that "finding a structure was a strategy for finding meaning — structure was both a heuristic and communicative device." This is not a claim about prose specifically; it is a claim about what structured language tasks do cognitively. The act of designing a structure (a rubric, a search string, a configuration) is itself a way of discovering what one means — not recording meaning that was already formed. Sommers also draws the contrast that matters: writers who write to fill in a pre-given structure (analogous to accepting an AI's draft wholesale) engage in mere polishing, not discovery.

**Use in the paper**: cite alongside Emig to make the extension explicit. Writing configurations is writing-as-thinking in Sommers' sense: the researcher discovers, through the act of writing the criterion, what their prior evaluative standard actually was. This also directly supports the tacit knowledge argument in the epistemic properties section — making the cross-section connection explicit would strengthen the paper's internal coherence.

### Quitadamo & Kurtz (2007) — empirical warrant [434 citations]

Provides empirical evidence that writing improves critical thinking (specifically analysis and inference) on standardised measures, not just in self-report. The formulation most useful for the paper: writing "requires an individual to make his or her ideas explicit" and analysis is "the ability to break a concept or idea into component pieces in order to understand its structure and inherent relationships." A screening rubric is exactly this: breaking the concept of "relevance" into component pieces with explicit relationships.

**Use in the paper**: one citation for empirical backing. Does not need detailed discussion — a parenthetical or an attributive clause is enough.

### Donnelly (2018) — "thinking in action" [3 citations — low, use sparingly]

Directly supplies the phrase "writing is thinking in action." Also contains a useful observation: designing writing activities that require mind engagement takes effort on the part of the instructor. This maps directly onto the cognitive work of writing skill configurations. However, with only 3 citations this should not carry argumentative weight on its own — use the phrase as shorthand, backed by Emig and Sommers.

### Warner (2024) — the objection that can be turned [non-peer-reviewed, YouTube video]

Warner argues that AI "generates syntax" but does not write, because writing is "the apprehension of knowledge that was maybe in here somewhere but was not apparent to ourselves." The cognitive value lies in the "productive struggle." This is the strongest form of the objection that systematic AI use erodes the thinking that writing enables.

The turn: Warner's objection applies precisely to unstructured use. Systematic use locates the productive struggle where it matters — in the specification. Writing a detailed skill configuration involves exactly the "agentic interaction with challenges" Warner identifies as cognitively valuable. What Warner opposes is the researcher who skips the struggle by accepting AI output without engagement. That is the unstructured use case. The systematic user has not skipped the struggle; they have relocated it.

**Use in the paper**: Warner is a credible public intellectual on this topic, but the source is a YouTube video and should not anchor a central claim. Use as the named objection in a concessive move, but carry the rebuttal with Emig and Sommers rather than with Warner against Warner. Do not cite in the reference list without clarifying its non-peer-reviewed status.

### Fry & Villagomez (2012) [109 citations]

The Zinsser quote embedded in this source is vivid: "often the act of writing even the simplest document clarified my half-formed ideas... Writing and thinking and learning were the same process." Useful as illustrative material, but does not add theoretically to what Emig and Sommers already provide. Use if a concrete illustrative note is needed; skip if space is tight.

## The objection and the paper's answer (from Q6)

The strongest objection, confirmed by the NotebookLM analysis: writing instructions is a "singular act of command" that does not require the recursive shuttling (reverting to earlier stages, progressive revision) that Emig identifies as the core mechanism. A shallow prompt is not deliberate semantics.

The paper's answer must be careful here: the claim is not that any AI prompt is writing-as-thinking. It is that writing *detailed, iterated specifications* — rubrics that are revised when they produce unexpected results, configurations that are tested and refined — engages the same recursive mechanism. Sommers' point about revision-as-discovery applies: the researcher who writes an initial rubric, runs it, finds it misclassifies edge cases, and revises it is doing exactly the iterative discovery Sommers describes. The systematic workflow builds this iteration in structurally (human verification checkpoints, documented revision). A one-off prompt to a default-mode AI is not systematic use and does not carry this benefit.

This is actually a useful sharpening of the structured/unstructured distinction: unstructured use produces neither deliberate semantics (criteria are implicit, not written) nor recursive discovery (the output is accepted, not iterated against a specification).

## Recommended draft revision

The current two-paragraph treatment (v7, lines 151–153) should be revised to:

1. Open with the writing-as-thinking claim grounded in mechanism, not just assertion: cite Emig on deliberate semantics and Sommers on structure-as-heuristic (one sentence each, or combined).
2. Make the extension explicit: writing configurations, rubrics, and search strings engages the same mechanism — it forces the researcher to articulate what relevance, quality, or coherence actually means, rather than relying on tacit intuition.
3. Acknowledge the objection briefly: shallow prompting does not carry this benefit. The systematic workflow builds iteration in precisely to ensure it does.
4. Connect to the tacit knowledge argument in Section 5: the same externalisation function operates at both levels — this is not a coincidence but a structural feature of what writing-as-specification does.

The hermeneutic circle sentence (currently the last sentence of the first paragraph) is good and can stay, now backed by Sommers rather than left as an unexplained gesture.
