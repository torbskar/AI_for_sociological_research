# Note: The exploratory work caveat — accountability as evidence, not prescription

**Source:** Author reflection (2026-04-17)

**The concern:** A reader might take the paper's argument as prescriptive — as claiming that all research should be conducted in a fully systematic, documented, criteria-first manner, leaving no room for exploratory work, thinking-while-writing, or ideas that emerge through the process. That reading would be both wrong and off-putting.

**The clarification:** The paper does not argue that everyone should work this way. It argues that the artefacts of systematic work are what enable accountability, and that their absence is evidence of their absence — not a moral failure, but an epistemic fact with consequences.

**Important caveat on self-reference:** This paper should not be described as having developed exploratorily without constraint. The more accurate account is: the core argument and the project configuration were established at the outset; the literature search was completed before drafting began; subsequent developments happened *within* that structure. What evolved was the scope and emphasis — the file-structure and systems aspect emerged as more central than initially anticipated and was developed further; a computer-science angle was added after a targeted search (Gemini); the empirical data analysis section was added partly because of the author's own quantitative practice and partly because the literature-review framing was becoming too dominant. Logging was not in place from the start, but when its importance became clear it was introduced — itself a documented decision. This is development within constraints and orchestration, not free-form emergence. The paper is a demonstration of what it argues: systematic work with iterative refinement of the system itself, and those refinements are traceable in the project history.

**The key argumentative move:** Transparency reveals how you actually worked. If you have no artefacts to show — no search strings, no screening rubrics, no reviewer configurations, no session logs — then you were not doing systematic AI authorship, and any reader or reviewer must assume it was unsystematic. This is not an accusation; it is an inference from evidence.

**The open science parallel:** The same logic governs open science practices more broadly:
- If a hypothesis was not pre-registered, we must assume some degree of forking paths was possible, even if the researcher did not intend to exploit it.
- If code is not available, we must assume the study is not independently replicable, even if the original analysis was sound.
- If AI workflow artefacts are not available, we must assume the process was unsystematic, even if the researcher was careful.

In all three cases the inference is not certain — careful unregistered research exists, sound unreproduced studies exist, careful undocumented AI use exists — but the absence of evidence forces a conservative prior. The open science movement did not eliminate undocumented research; it changed what undocumented research means for how it is assessed.

**The normative point, stated precisely:** The paper does not argue that unsystematic AI use is forbidden or that exploratory work is bad. It argues that if a researcher chooses not to produce accountability artefacts, that choice makes their work unaccountable — and they should accept being assessed accordingly. This is a symmetric and non-moralistic claim: document your process and be assessed on it; do not document it and be assessed as if you did not. The researcher who prefers the latter is free to make that choice, but cannot simultaneously claim accountability without evidence of the process that would support it.

**Where this might fit in the paper:** A short caveat paragraph — probably in §5.4 (what structured use cannot guarantee and what it does not require) or at the end of §3.2 (what systematic use looks like) — acknowledging that systematic AI authorship does not exhaust what good research looks like, that exploratory and iterative work has its place, and that the argument is about accountability evidence rather than prescribed workflow. The open science parallel makes this crisp: the argument is structurally identical to what the replication movement argues for statistical code, and nobody takes that argument to mean that all analysis must be algorithmic.

**One concrete framing:**
> The argument here is not that all AI use must be systematic, any more than the case for replication packages implies that all analysis must be automated. Researchers will continue to work exploratorily, to think while writing, and to let the process develop their thinking — this paper did the same. What systematic AI authorship provides is not a constraint on how one works but evidence of how one worked. Absent that evidence, a reader must apply the same conservative prior that open science norms apply to undocumented analysis: if there is no record, one must assume there was no system, with all that entails for how the work is assessed. The choice to work without producing accountability artefacts is a legitimate one; so is the reader's inference from their absence.
