# Writing as thinking — sources and argument development

## What the draft currently says

In the drafting section (v7, around line 151–153), the paper makes two adjacent claims:

1. Prain & Hand (2016) are cited for the view that writing is an epistemic tool — "a resource for enhanced thinking, meaning-making, and conceptual work" — and that the drafting phase should be used as such.
2. The draft then makes a move that is currently underdeveloped: that writing instructions, configurations, and prompts *is also* a form of this cognitive work — "acts of composing the structure and line of argument" — but this is stated rather than argued.

The second claim is the more original one and currently lacks theoretical grounding. The sources below can provide it.

## Source assessments

### Most relevant

**Emig (1977) — Writing as a Mode of Learning**
The most theoretically useful source. Emig argues that writing is a unique mode of learning because it forces "deliberate semantics": unlike speech, writing is artificial and requires the writer to structure meaning rather than relying on "immediate synpractical connections" — the tacit associations that allow speech to feel coherent without being logically explicit. Writing promotes analysis and synthesis precisely because it *slows* cognition and makes connections visible for review.

This maps directly onto the tacit knowledge argument already in the paper (section on epistemic properties). If writing prose forces deliberate semantics, then writing a search rubric, an inclusion criterion, or a reviewer configuration does the same. The act of specifying what "relevant" means in a screening rubric is a form of deliberate semantics — it forces the researcher to articulate what was previously a tacit evaluative standard. Emig gives the theoretical grounding that the paper currently lacks for this claim.

**Sommers (1981) — Intentions and Revisions**
Revision is not polishing but recursive discovery: skilled writers use revision to make text congruent with *changing* intentions, discovering what they mean through the writing process. Writing is explicitly a *heuristic* — a means of discovering meaning through the search for structure. This directly supports the draft's mention of a "hermeneutic circle between drafting, reviewing and revising," giving it theoretical depth rather than casual gesture. Sommers also usefully distinguishes between writers who write to discover and those who write to fill in an abstract template — which maps onto the structured/unstructured distinction the paper makes.

**Donnelly (2018) — Potential Impacts of Academic Writing**
Describes writing as "thinking in action" — a direct, citable phrase. Argues that writing forces clarification of ideas and the logical assembly of reasons. Useful as a secondary citation where the claim is stated directly without needing elaboration.

**Quitadamo & Kurtz (2007) — Learning to Improve**
Empirical evidence that writing significantly improves critical thinking (analysis and inference), not just in subjective perception but on standardised measures. Writing is described as "a cultivator and an enabler" of higher-order thinking; its recursive and reflective character forces students to analyze, review, and clarify. Useful for lending empirical warrant to what otherwise reads as an assertion.

### Moderately relevant

**Warner (2024) — How to think about writing in the age of AI**
Warner argues that AI "generates syntax" but does not write, because writing is "the apprehension of knowledge that was previously non-apparent to the writer." His critique of AI can actually be *turned*: the paper can acknowledge Warner's concern (outsourcing writing to AI foregoes the epistemic benefit of writing-as-thinking) and respond that systematic AI use preserves the relevant cognitive work by locating it in the specification — the query, the configuration, the rubric — rather than in the prose output. Warner himself notes that the cognitive value lies in "productive struggle" and "agentic interaction with challenges," both of which are present when a researcher writes a detailed skill configuration. This is a useful concessive-then-turn structure.

**Fry & Villagomez (2012) — Writing to Learn**
Writing is "making a subject clear to oneself by reasoning through it in sequential steps." Less theoretically developed than Emig or Sommers, but the framing of sequential-step reasoning is useful if the paper wants to argue that writing a structured prompt requires reasoning through the research question sequentially in a way that vague oral or mental engagement does not.

### Less relevant

**McCurrie (2005) — Peer Review Matters**
Primarily about peer feedback and social dimensions of writing. The argument that writing is "internalized social talk made public again" is interesting but does not connect tightly to the paper's needs. Skip.

**Premkumar (2024) — The Impact of AI on Literary Creation**
Focused on authorship and authenticity in literary creation, not the epistemic function of writing. Does not add to the argument. Skip.

## How these sources strengthen the paper

The current draft makes the writing-as-thinking argument too quickly and then pivots to the more interesting claim — that writing configurations *is* writing-as-thinking — without providing a bridge. The strengthening move is two steps:

**Step 1.** Use Emig (1977) and Sommers (1981) to sharpen what writing-as-thinking actually means: not just "expressing ideas" but forcing deliberate semantics (Emig) and recursive discovery of meaning through structure (Sommers). A sentence or two, citing both. This replaces the current brief Prain & Hand citation, which makes the claim but doesn't explain the mechanism.

**Step 2.** Use this sharpened account to argue that the specification work in systematic AI use — writing rubrics, query strings, configurations — engages exactly this mechanism. The researcher who writes an inclusion criterion is forced into deliberate semantics in Emig's sense: the tacit evaluative standard must be articulated as structure. Sommers' heuristic framing applies equally: the researcher often discovers, through the act of writing a configuration, that their prior criterion was ambiguous or inconsistently held. This is not a new claim in the paper — it is already present in the tacit knowledge section — but developing it also in the drafting section would close a current gap.

The Warner concessive turn is optional but potentially elegant: acknowledge the strongest form of the objection (outsourcing writing to AI erodes the thinking) and respond that the objection applies to unstructured use precisely, and that systematic use's answer is to locate the writing-as-thinking effort where it epistemically belongs — in specification, not prose generation.

### Q1: On the mechanism of writing-as-thinking

> The paper argues that writing AI configurations, rubrics, and query strings is a form of writing-as-thinking, not just writing prose. Answer: 
Based on the provided sources, **Janet Emig (1977)** provides the most developed account of the specific **mechanism**—not just the assertion—of why writing forces explicit thinking. She draws on cognitive psychology and the work of Lev Vygotsky and A.R. Luria to explain that writing is a unique cognitive activity because it functions as a "multi-representational mode for learning" that integrates the hand, eye, and brain.

The core mechanism described by Emig involves three key psychological processes:

### 1. Inhibition of Immediate Connections

Writing forces a "remoteness of reference" and inhibits the brain's tendency to rely on immediate, vague, or "synpractical" (action-based) connections. By slowing down the thought process, writing requires the brain to engage in a deliberate "mediating process" that transforms raw ideas into a stable, visible structure.

> "Written speech is bound up with the **inhibition of immediate synpractical connections**. It assumes a much slower, repeated mediating process of analysis and synthesis, which makes it possible not only to develop the required thought, but even to **revert to its earlier stages**, thus transforming the sequential chain of connections into a **simultaneous, self-reviewing structure**."

### 2. Deliberate Semantics and Structuring

Unlike speech, which is often organic and redundant, writing requires the writer to explicitly supply topics, subjects, and logical connections (such as causal or coordinate relationships) that might remain implicit or fuzzy in internal thought.

> "...writing makes a unique demand in that the writer must engage in '**deliberate semantics**'—in Vygotsky's elegant phrase, '**deliberate structuring of the web of meaning**.' ... written speech is a mode which is 'maximally detailed' and which requires explicitly supplied subjects and topics. The medium then of written verbal language requires the **establishment of systematic connections and relationships**."

### 3. Multi-Representational Feedback

The physical act of writing creates a unique feedback loop. Because it involves a graphic product, the writer can visually "re-scan and review" their own evolution of thought, allowing for symbolic transformation and revision.

> "What is striking about writing as a process is that, by its very nature, all three ways of dealing with actuality are **simultaneously or almost simultaneously deployed**. That is, the symbolic transformation of experience... is shaped into an icon (the graphic product) by the enactive hand... writing through its inherent re-inforcing cycle involving **hand, eye, and brain** marks a uniquely powerful multi-representational mode for learning."

### Support from Other Sources

While Emig offers the most psychological detail, **Nancy Sommers (1981)** provides a complementary account of the mechanism through the lens of **structure as a heuristic**. She argues that the struggle to find a structure—such as the configurations or rubrics you mention—is the very act of discovering meaning.

> "**Finding a structure was a strategy for finding meaning**—structure was both a heuristic and communicative device. Walter found his structure by linking the inner elements of his text and, in so doing, selected and shaped his meaning."

Similarly, **Quitadamo and Kurtz (2007)** note that the "recursive and reflective nature" of the writing process "requires an individual to make his or her ideas explicit". This supports your paper's argument that the high-precision "writing" required for AI configurations and query strings forces a level of explicit analysis and inference that characterizes higher-order thinking.

### Q5: On the objection that AI-assisted writing bypasses cognitive work

> Warner (2024) argues that having AI generate text erodes the learning and thinking that writing enables. Are there other sources in this notebook that raise a similar concern, or that frame the cognitive value of writing in a way that implies it cannot be delegated? What is the strongest version of this objection that these sources support?

Several sources in the notebook strongly reinforce John Warner's (2024) argument that writing is a cognitive process that cannot be delegated without losing the learning it enables. These sources frame writing not as the transcription of pre-existing thoughts, but as the **biological and psychological mechanism** through which higher-order thinking is constructed.

### The Strongest Version of the Objection: Writing as an Irreducible Cognitive Act

The strongest objection supported by these sources is that writing is a **multi-representational biological process** that "virtually forces" the brain into states of analysis and synthesis that are otherwise inaccessible. If the act of writing is delegated to an AI, the "shuttling" between past, present, and future thoughts is erased, and the "deliberate structuring of the web of meaning" never occurs. In this view, delegating writing is not just "outsourcing a task"—it is **preventing the cognitive development** of the individual.

### 1. The Multi-Representational Constraint

**Janet Emig (1977)** provides a foundational argument that suggests the cognitive value of writing is rooted in the physical and visual loop that AI cannot replicate for the learner. She argues that writing is a **"unique mode of learning"** because it simultaneously engages the "enactive" (hand), "iconic" (eye), and "symbolic" (brain) systems.

* **Mechanism:** Emig states that writing serves learning uniquely because it "**requires the establishment of systematic connections and relationships**".
* **The Objection to Delegation:** She emphasizes that writing "**virtually forces a remoteness of reference on the language user**" and inhibits "**immediate synpractical connections**". By having AI generate the connections, the student bypasses the "**much slower, repeated mediating process of analysis and synthesis**" required to transform a sequence of thoughts into a "**simultaneous, self-reviewing structure**".

### 2. The Heuristic and Discovery Argument

**Nancy Sommers (1981)** frames writing as a heuristic—a method of discovery—that is fundamentally undermined if the structure is provided by an external agent like AI.

* **Mechanism:** Sommers found that for skilled writers, "**Finding a structure was a strategy for finding meaning—structure was both a heuristic and communicative device**".
* **The Objection to Delegation:** Sommers warns against imposing rigid, pre-set structures (similar to AI templates or outputs), noting that they turn writing into a "**polishing act concerned with taking the linguistic litter out of sentences**" rather than a creative act. If the AI provides the draft, the writer is robbed of the "**discovery of ideas**" that occurs during the recursive process of making a text "**congruent with a writer's changing intentions**".

### 3. The Requirement of Explicit Articulation

**Ian Quitadamo and Martha Kurtz (2007)** provide empirical evidence that the cognitive gains of writing are tied to the requirement that the individual makes their own ideas explicit.

* **Mechanism:** They define critical thinking as a "**process of purposeful self-regulatory judgment**". They argue that writing improves this because it "**requires an individual to make his or her ideas explicit**" and to "**conceptually organize and structure their thoughts**".
* **The Objection to Delegation:** Their research showed that students who did not engage in the writing treatment failed to improve their analysis and inference skills, whereas the writing group showed significant gains. This implies that the **cognitive "hard-wiring"** for critical thinking is only activated when the student is the one doing the work of "**shaping their thoughts at the point of construction**".

### 4. The Loss of Human Agency and Emotional Depth

**Bindu Premkumar (2024)** and **Roisin Donnelly (2018)** address the modern implications of AI and professional identity, highlighting what is lost when the "human curator" takes a back seat.

* **Mechanism:** Premkumar notes that AI text generation Demonstration a reliance on "**predefined models**" and lacks the "**nuanced emotional depth and creativity characteristic of human-authored works**".
* **The Objection to Delegation:** Donnelly cites Menary (2007) to assert that "**writing is thinking in action**". She suggests that designing and engaging in writing activities that require "**mind engagement takes effort**". This reinforces Warner's (2024) view that AI-generated summaries lead to a "**detachment of making words or or or thoughts into a work product that are detached from the actual sense of time sense of experience**".

### Q6: For a counter-argument or reframing

> Given what these sources say about why writing is cognitively valuable — the deliberate semantics, the productive struggle, the metacognitive process — what would these authors likely say about the claim that writing *instructions to an AI* (query strings, screening rubrics, detailed prompts) involves similar cognitive demands? Use the sources to construct both the strongest support for and the strongest objection to this extension.

Constructing the strongest case for and against the claim that writing **AI instructions** (prompts, rubrics, or query strings) involves the same cognitive demands as **prose writing** requires looking at the specific psychological mechanisms identified in the sources.

### Strongest Support for the Extension

The strongest argument for this extension rests on the requirement of **"deliberate semantics"** and the analytical breakdown of thought. Writing for an AI demands a level of precision and explicitness that mirrors the highest cognitive functions of traditional writing.

* **Deliberate Structuring of Meaning:** Janet Emig (1977), drawing on Vygotsky, argues that writing is unique because it requires the **"deliberate structuring of the web of meaning"**. She notes that written speech is **"maximally detailed"** and requires **"explicitly supplied subjects and topics"**. Writing a high-quality prompt or rubric for an AI necessitates this exact "deliberate semantics"; the user must establish **"systematic connections and relationships"**—causal, coordinate, or subordinate—to ensure the AI understands the intent without ambiguity.
* **Analysis of Component Pieces:** Quitadamo and Kurtz (2007) define analysis as the **"ability to break a concept or idea into component pieces in order to understand its structure and inherent relationships"**. They argue that writing improves thinking specifically because it **"requires an individual to make his or her ideas explicit"**. Writing structured instructions like a screening rubric is an act of high-level analysis; it forces the author to deconstruct their implicit standards into explicit, component criteria.
* **Structure as a Heuristic:** Nancy Sommers (1981) posits that **"finding a structure was a strategy for finding meaning—structure was both a heuristic and communicative device"**. When an author writes complex query strings or instructions, the act of "finding the structure" of the command becomes a way to discover and clarify the meaning of their own project.

### Strongest Objection to the Extension

The strongest objection is that writing _for_ an AI delegates the **"productive struggle"** and the recursive discovery process to a machine, thereby **erasing the experience** that constitutes actual learning and deep thinking.

* **Erasure of the Experience:** John Warner (2024) provides a fierce objection, arguing that when we use AI to generate text, the **"experience has been erased"**. He contends that writing is the **"apprehension of knowledge that was maybe in here somewhere but was not apparent to ourselves"**. While the _instruction_ may be structured, the actual realization of thought occurs during the struggle of prose composition. By bypassing the act of writing the prose itself, the learner loses the **"metacognitive process"** and the **"spark"** that occurs when unique intelligence collides with a text.
* **Inhibition vs. Command:** Emig (1977) argues that the cognitive value of writing comes from how it **"inhibits immediate synpractical connections"**. It forces a slow, **"repeated mediating process of analysis and synthesis"** that allows a writer to **"revert to its earlier stages"**. Writing a prompt is often a singular act of command; it does not necessarily require the same recursive "shuttling" between past and future thoughts that occurs when one is physically and mentally immersed in a 1,000-word essay.
* **Lack of Emotional Depth and Nuance:** Bindu Premkumar (2024) notes that AI-generated works often **"lack the nuanced emotional depth and creativity characteristic of human-authored works"**. If the "writing-as-thinking" claim is extended to prompting, opponents would argue that the "thinking" is truncated. The AI relies on **"predefined models"** and formulaic patterns. Therefore, the person writing the prompt is merely acting as a **"curator"** of a limited, algorithmic output rather than engaging in the full, "bi-spheral" functioning of the brain that Emig (1977) identifies as essential to writing-as-learning.
