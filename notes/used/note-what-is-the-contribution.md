After adjusting my personal preferences in Claude to be more critical, I asked for a new assessment of the paper's contribution. The expectation was a more critical assessment than before. Here are some valid points to keep in mind while drafting the article. The main takeaway is that speedy publication is important, but also not to be software-specific. The three points on real contribution must be central focus.  



**The strongest objection**

The field is moving fast enough that a workflow paper risks being dated before it clears review. Tools change, journal policies are actively being rewritten, and a paper that describes a 2026 workflow may read as historical documentation by the time it appears in print. This is a genuine risk, not a hypothetical one. The mitigation is to ensure the paper's core argument is tool-agnostic — the structured/unstructured distinction and the query authorship point are durable; specific tool names are not.

**Why it is nonetheless a real contribution**

Three reasons, in descending order of confidence.

First, the structured/unstructured distinction is genuinely undertheorised. Most of the existing discourse — and there is a lot of it — operates at the level of "AI good" or "AI bad" without a principled axis for distinguishing practices. A paper that names that axis clearly, argues for it as the policy-relevant dimension, and connects it to existing open science infrastructure fills a real gap. That argument will remain citable even when the specific tools have changed.

Second, the query authorship point is original. I am not aware of existing work that frames the intellectual content of queries as a form of authorship in its own right, connects it to tacit knowledge externalisation, and draws policy implications from it. That is the most novel conceptual contribution in the paper and the one most likely to generate citations beyond the immediate AI-in-research literature. It is also the kind of idea that will be obvious in retrospect — which is usually a good sign.

Third, the supplementary materials as a replication package for a non-empirical paper is itself a contribution to how methods papers are written. Depositing actual skill files, prompt templates, and workflow logs as citable artefacts extends the open science logic into a domain it has not reached. Sociological Science's own framing about methodology sections being abstract summaries that hide details in code applies directly here.

**Where the paper is weaker**

The reliability and thoroughness claims in section 5 of the outline are the least defensible. The assertion that structured AI use produces more thorough literature coverage and more consistent review quality is plausible but not demonstrated by this paper. It would need either empirical support or careful hedging — "I suggest" rather than "I argue." That section needs to be written modestly or cut back to a hypothesis for future research.

The journal policy critique, while sharp, risks annoying the editors who will decide whether to publish it. The constructive reframing helps, but the tone needs to stay firmly on the side of proposing a better framework rather than impugning current policies.

**The honest bottom line**

It is a good paper idea, not a great one. The contribution is real but bounded — it will matter a lot to researchers actively working out how to use these tools responsibly, and less to anyone else. That is not a criticism; bounded contributions are most of what good journals publish. The query authorship argument has the potential to travel further if it is developed carefully. Whether the paper is merely useful or genuinely influential depends almost entirely on how well that argument is written — it needs to be precise enough to be citable, not just suggestive.

The timing works in your favour despite the dating risk. Being early in a fast-moving conversation is an advantage if the argument is durable. The window for being the paper that named the distinction is open now and will close within a year or two.
