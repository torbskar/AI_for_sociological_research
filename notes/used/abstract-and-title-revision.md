## Abstract — needs sharpening

The current abstract (v5) reads:

> Current policies on AI use in academic research treat it as a binary — permitted or prohibited, disclosed or not — but this misidentifies the epistemically relevant dimension. What matters is not whether AI was used but whether it was used *systematically*: embedded in explicit criteria, subject to human verification, and producing documented outputs. I introduce the concept of *systematic AI authorship* and contrast it with unsystematic use, which is unaccountable and unreliable at scale. Systematic AI authorship is a necessary condition for epistemic soundness, not a sufficient one: it makes errors visible and correctable rather than eliminating them. It instantiates the properties of open-science practice — externalising tacit commitments, producing verifiable artefacts, consistent with replication package norms. The ICMJE authorship criteria, applied positively rather than merely negatively, give policy a more tractable question than whether AI was used. A documentation-based framework that operationalises this distinction is more epistemically honest than binary approaches and requires no new infrastructure.

**Problems with the current abstract:**

- Opens on what policies do wrong rather than on what the paper offers — the hook is negative and reactive rather than intriguing
- "Epistemically relevant dimension" is technical jargon before the reader is oriented
- The key concepts (systematic AI authorship, query authorship) are introduced without any sense of why they are surprising or consequential
- The abstract reads as a list of moves rather than an argument with stakes
- "Requires no new infrastructure" is a good closer but lands flat because the reader has not been made to care yet
- Query authorship — arguably the most original conceptual contribution — does not appear in the abstract at all
- 150 words is the Sociological Science maximum; the current abstract is within that but could use the space more effectively

**What a sharper abstract should do:**

- Open with a tension, paradox, or counter-intuitive claim — something that makes the reader want to read on
- Name the stakes early: what is at risk when AI use is governed by the wrong instrument?
- Introduce *query authorship* as the novel concept, not just *systematic AI authorship*
- Make the open-science connection feel like a payoff, not a checklist item
- End on a policy implication that feels urgent, not merely reasonable

**Draft direction to explore (not yet written — for author or skardhamar-style skill):**
Consider opening on the paradox that the most conscientious AI users — those who document their process, verify outputs, encode explicit criteria — are currently treated identically by policy to those who accept AI outputs without scrutiny. Or open on the query authorship idea directly: the intellectual contribution in AI-assisted research is not in the text the AI produces but in the instructions that shaped it. Either framing is more arresting than the current policy-critique opener.

## Title — scope decision needed

**Current title:** *Systematic AI Authorship in Sociological Research*

**The decision:** Is the paper's argument specifically about sociology, or does it apply to social science more broadly — or even to all empirical humanities and interpretive research?

### Arguments for keeping "sociology"

- The workflow is demonstrated through the author's own sociology practice
- Sociological Science is the target journal; disciplinary specificity fits
- The paper already acknowledges generalisability is uncertain and scopes carefully in §1
- "Sociology" signals methodological contribution to a specific community rather than generic advice
- The paper explicitly says "sociology and adjacent social sciences" — the title could reflect that

### Arguments for broadening to "social science" or "academic research"

- The core argument — systematic vs unsystematic use, query authorship, pre-registration analogy, ICMJE criteria — is not sociology-specific
- The literature review spans psychology, economics, criminology, NLP, and data science
- The workflow (literature search, empirical analysis, drafting, review, documentation) is generic to qualitative and quantitative social science
- A broader title widens the potential readership and citation audience
- Discipline-specific titles can discourage citation from adjacent fields where the argument would apply

### Title options to consider

**Sociology-specific:**

- *Systematic AI Authorship in Sociological Research* (current)
- *Query Authorship: Systematic AI Use as Open Science in Sociology*

**Social science:**

- *Systematic AI Authorship in Social Science Research*
- *Query Authorship: A Framework for Systematic AI Use in Social Science*
- *From Disclosure to Documentation: AI Authorship Standards for Social Science*

**Broader / discipline-neutral:**

- *Systematic AI Authorship: A Documentation Framework for Empirical Research*
- *Query Authorship: How Systematic AI Use Instantiates Open Science Practice*
- *The Systematic/Unsystematic Distinction: What AI Policy in Research Gets Wrong*

## Journal scope decision

If the title and framing shift to social science broadly, the journal choice should be revisited.

| Journal                                | Scope fit                                                   | Notes                                                                                                                                  |
| -------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Sociological Science** (current)     | Good if sociology framing kept; adequate for social science | Diamond OA, no word limit, methodological contributions welcomed, mandatory replication package — strong fit on all structural grounds |
| **Sociological Methods & Research**    | Social science methods broadly                              | Peer-reviewed, high impact in methodology; Davidson & Karell (2025) published here — direct precedent                                  |
| **Social Science Research**            | Broad social science                                        | Quantitative focus; less natural fit for a conceptual/methodological paper                                                             |
| **Science and Engineering Ethics**     | AI ethics + research integrity                              | Relevant theme but different audience and framing requirement                                                                          |
| **Research Integrity and Peer Review** | Research practice and policy                                | Open access; policy-oriented audience; good fit for §6 argument but may undervalue the methodological contribution                     |
| **Big Data & Society**                 | Computational methods, AI in research                       | Good fit if framing tilts toward AI infrastructure; less natural for the open-science and authorship argument                          |
| **Sociologica**                        | Open-access sociology, methodological pluralism             | Törnberg (2024) published here — direct precedent; smaller venue                                                                       |

**Recommendation for author consideration:** If the argument is genuinely for social science rather than specifically sociology, *Sociological Methods & Research* is the strongest alternative — it has published direct precedents (Davidson & Karell 2025), high disciplinary prestige, and an explicit methods focus. Sociological Science remains the better choice if the sociology framing is kept, given its structural fit (replication package requirement, no word limit, open access).

**Decision needed:** Settle the scope question first, then revise the title, then sharpen the abstract to match. The abstract revision and journal choice should be made together, not sequentially.
