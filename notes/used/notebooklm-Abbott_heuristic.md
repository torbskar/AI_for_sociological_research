Your argument that systematic prompting constitutes a form of **"query authorship"** aligns closely with Andrew Abbott’s central thesis that the most vital part of social science is the **heuristic process**—the systematic use of "mental moves" to generate new ideas.

According to the sources, your concept maps onto Abbott's framework in several specific ways:

### 1. The Systematic Nature of Imagination

Abbott explicitly argues that the "discovery side" of science—the imaginative part—is **"more systematic than we think"**. He defines heuristics as **"gambits of imagination"** or **"formulas"** used for opening up and realizing possibilities in an argument. If prompting an LLM is done systematically, it functions exactly like Abbott’s "heuristics": a set of "tricks and schemes" used to hasten discovery and solve the problem of "having nothing new to say".

### 2. Science as a Conversation

Abbott views science as a **"conversation between rigor and imagination"**.

* **The Query as "Proposal":** In your model, the query represents the "imaginative voice" that proposes a new angle or frames a problem.
* **The LLM/Researcher as "Evaluation":** Just as Abbott says one voice proposes and the other evaluates, systematic prompting involves a cycle where the prompt proposes a heuristic move (e.g., a reversal or an analogy), and the resulting output is evaluated for rigor.

### 3. Authorship through Heuristic Framing

Abbott suggests that "authorship" or intellectual "genius" is found in the **framing** of a problem rather than just the final data analysis. He notes that:

* Social reality requires a **"subtler wooing"** that combines rigor with imagination.
* The researcher's primary job is to find **"new ways to solve problems"** using "aids to the social scientific imagination".By "authoring" the query, you are essentially selecting which **heuristic gambit** to deploy—such as "problematizing the obvious," "making a reversal," or "borrowing a method"—which Abbott identifies as the core creative act of the social scientist.

### 4. Mapping to Specific Heuristic Types

The "ideas manifest in the queries" can be seen as the application of Abbott's specific heuristic categories:

* **Search Heuristics:** Queries that ask an LLM to "make an analogy" between two seemingly unrelated fields.
* **Argument Heuristics:** Queries that systematically "reverse" a standard assumption to see what new insights emerge.
* **Descriptive Heuristics:** Queries that "change the context" or "change levels" of an existing description to find hidden layers of meaning.

### 5. The "Mozart" Level of Authorship

Abbott describes a fourth, "master" level of intellectual development where a researcher uses different standard stances to **challenge each other**. He uses **Mozart** as an example of an author who mastered multiple styles and allowed them to "talk to each other" to define characters. **Query authorship** mirrors this: the "author" is not just asking for information but is using the prompt to force different intellectual traditions or "fractal debates" (like positivism vs. interpretivism) to confront one another to generate a complex, novel synthesis.

**Summary:** In Abbott's view, the "genius" of the work is not in the "monologue" of the final report, but in the **"continual interchange between intuition and method"**. Therefore, the systematic design of queries—the "query authorship"—is where the real **social scientific imagination** resides.
