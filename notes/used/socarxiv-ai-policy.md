# SocArXiv AI Policy — Notes

**Source:** SocArXiv. (2026, March 8). *AI policy*. Center for Open Science. https://socopen.org/ai-policy/  
**Policy effective:** 2026-03-08

---

## Key quote — research landscape

> "The fact is that we are getting overrun with machine-generated slop, some of which is manipulative or fraudulent and some of which is very low quality. This is a common phenomenon across many platforms. At the same time, some real researchers are using LLMs and other automation tools to help do research."

SocArXiv is a sociology-specific preprint server: this is the field's own infrastructure registering the volume problem operationally, not theoretically. The quote simultaneously names the fraud/quality problem and acknowledges the legitimate-researcher side — exactly the distinction the paper's argument turns on.

Stated goal of the policy: "protect the epistemic commons by distinguishing research that is part of advancing social science knowledge from that which merely dilutes our work."

---

## Policy structure

**Acceptable uses:** language translation; literature searching and idea organisation *with human verification*; copy-editing and formatting; data analysis assistance; dictation.

**Prohibited:** verbatim AI text generation; fabricated research data; treating LLMs as human collaborators; unreviewed AI outputs; entirely machine-generated papers.

**Detection:** moderators assess minimal scholarly contribution and flag suspicious patterns (unsupported theories, superficial synthesis, fake empirical work, methodologically questionable submissions). Task-based and judgement-based, not automated.

---

## Relevance to the paper

**Urgency evidence.** The "overrun with slop" language is concrete, field-specific evidence that the problem is live. Useful in the introduction or the journal policy section as grounding — not an abstract concern but one that preprint moderators are managing manually.

**Partial move toward task-based policy.** The acceptable-use list distinguishes by what AI was used *for*, not simply whether it was used — closer to the paper's argument than binary disclosure. "Literature searching and idea organisation *with human verification*" is explicitly permitted, which is essentially the paper's structured-use definition.

**The systematic/unsystematic axis remains invisible.** The policy does not ask whether the permitted use was documented, verifiable, or criterion-governed. A researcher with zero documentation and one with a full systematic pipeline both pass on the acceptable side. The distinction the paper argues for is not present.

**"Epistemic commons" framing.** Grounds the policy concern in collective harm rather than individual misconduct — compatible with the paper's framing of unstructured AI use as a structural problem rather than a moral failing.

**Citation placement.** Journal policy section, alongside Mondal et al. and Ganjavi et al., as a sociology-specific concrete case that partially moves in the right direction while leaving the systematic/unsystematic axis unaddressed. Also usable in the introduction for the urgency framing.
