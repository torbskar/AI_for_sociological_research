# Note: The intellectual content of a query
## Date: 2026-04-05
## For: Section on structured AI use / transparency / author contribution discussion

---

The current debate about AI in academic writing focuses almost entirely on outputs —
whether text was AI-generated, whether citations are confabulated, whether the argument
is the author's own. This framing misses something important: in structured AI use,
the primary intellectual work is often located in the query, not the response.

A query to an AI system is not a neutral retrieval act. It presupposes a research
question, a theoretical framing, a sense of what counts as a relevant answer, and
a judgment about what the current state of knowledge is missing. The question "does
the same legal restriction apply whether the model runs locally or remotely?" is not
a simple request for information — it encodes a distinction (data location vs. data
use rights), an awareness that these might come apart, and a correct suspicion that
the standard answer conflates them. The intellectual content is in the question. The
AI's role is to confirm, elaborate, or correct — not to originate.

This has direct implications for how we assess authorship and intellectual
contribution in AI-assisted research. The relevant question is not "did the AI
produce this text?" but "where did the reasoning come from?" In structured workflows,
the reasoning is documented in the query sequence — the decisions about what to ask,
in what order, with what constraints. This is precisely what the logging system
developed for this project is designed to capture.

It follows that query authorship is a form of intellectual authorship. A researcher
who formulates the right questions, recognises when an answer is incomplete, redirects
when a suggestion misses the point, and knows which AI output to accept and which to
reject is doing intellectual work that is not diminished by the fact that another
agent produced the prose. The analogy to research assistance, editorial collaboration,
or statistical consulting is imperfect but instructive: in all these cases, the
intellectual ownership of the work is not determined by who typed the most words.

Current AI disclosure policies do not make this distinction. They treat AI involvement
as a binary — disclosed or not — without asking where in the research process the
human reasoning was doing the work. A documentation-based policy framework, of the
kind argued for in this paper, would make that question answerable rather than leaving
it to assertion.
