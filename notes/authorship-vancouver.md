# Authorship and the Vancouver criteria

## Purpose

This note develops the argument that systematic AI authorship — as defined in the paper — maps directly onto the four ICMJE/Vancouver criteria for authorship, and that this mapping both clarifies why AI cannot be an author and why the systematic/unsystematic distinction is the relevant axis for evaluating human authorship in AI-assisted work.

---

## The Vancouver criteria (ICMJE)

All four criteria must be met for authorship; those not meeting all four should be acknowledged but not listed as authors.

1. **Substantial contributions** to the conception or design of the work; or to the acquisition, analysis, or interpretation of data
2. **Drafting or revising critically** for important intellectual content
3. **Final approval** of the version to be published
4. **Agreement to be accountable** for all aspects of the work — including investigation of accuracy and integrity questions

---

## Why AI cannot be an author under these criteria

This is the easy part, but worth stating precisely. AI fails criteria 3 and 4 categorically:

- Criterion 3 requires approving the version for publication — AI cannot consent, cannot review in the sense of endorsing or taking responsibility for a final version.
- Criterion 4 requires being accountable for the work — AI has no legal or moral standing, cannot be investigated, cannot be sanctioned, cannot "agree" to anything. Accountability is a property of persons, not tools.

AI also fails criteria 1 and 2 in the relevant sense: the AI executes within instructions it did not originate; it does not conceive, design, or critically evaluate in the sense required. It produces text; it does not author. This is precisely what the query authorship concept captures — the intellectual work that meets criteria 1 and 2 is in the design of the query, not in the execution of it.

---

## Systematic AI use and the Vancouver criteria

The interesting move is to show that systematic AI authorship strengthens — not merely preserves — the researcher's claim to authorship on each criterion.

### Criterion 1: Substantial contributions to conception, design, acquisition, analysis, or interpretation

**What systematic use provides:**
- The researcher designs the search strategy: specifying what counts as relevant, which concepts to include or exclude, what the scope of the literature is. This is conception.
- The researcher specifies inclusion/exclusion criteria for screening, the analytical strategy for interpretation, the synthesis questions. These are design decisions that encode the paper's theoretical commitments.
- The researcher interprets outputs at each human verification checkpoint — deciding what to retain, flag, or reject. This is interpretation.
- The researcher's intellectual contributions are documented: in search strings, screening rubrics, skill configurations, session logs. The contribution is auditable.

**What unsystematic use threatens:**
- Without explicit criteria, the researcher's contribution to conception and design is invisible and unverifiable. They may have made substantial contributions — but they cannot show it, and the contributions may have been less substantial than systematic use requires.
- If outputs are accepted without verification, the interpretation step — a named criterion — is not being performed.

**The argument:** Systematic AI use is designed so that the researcher's substantial contributions are externalized, documented, and verifiable. The workflow produces evidence that criteria 1 is met. Unsystematic use produces no such evidence and may not meet the criterion substantively.

### Criterion 2: Drafting or revising critically for important intellectual content

**What systematic use provides:**
- "Drafting" under query authorship: writing the search string, the screening rubric, the skill configuration, and the outline is a form of intellectual drafting. The researcher is not merely accepting AI output; they are authoring the framework within which the AI operates. These documents encode important intellectual content — scope commitments, theoretical priorities, evaluative standards.
- "Revising critically": every stage of the systematic workflow involves the researcher reading AI outputs and deciding what to keep, what to revise, and what to reject. The author-input logs document when the researcher redirected, corrected, or pushed back on AI outputs. This is critical revision with an audit trail.
- The session logs distinguish what the AI produced from what the researcher contributed, modified, or rejected — enabling a transparent account of critical revision.

**What unsystematic use threatens:**
- If the researcher accepts AI-generated text without substantive revision, criterion 2 is not met. The test is not whether the researcher read the output but whether they revised it critically for important intellectual content. Unsystematic use provides no evidence that this happened and may not require it to happen.

**The argument:** Systematic use builds critical revision into the protocol at every stage. The author-input files are specifically designed to document criterion 2 — they record what the researcher contributed intellectually, not merely whether they pressed "accept."

### Criterion 3: Final approval of the version to be published

**What systematic use provides:**
- The researcher reviews and approves outputs at each stage before they pass to the next. The human verification checkpoints are formal approvals of intermediate work.
- The researcher reads, revises, and submits the manuscript. This criterion is met by any researcher who submits a paper.
- The difference systematic use makes here is less about whether criterion 3 is met and more about whether the researcher can honestly claim that the version they approved is actually a version they understand and can stand behind. Systematic use, because it externalises decisions and makes the process traceable, enables informed approval. Unsystematic use can produce approval of outputs the researcher cannot fully account for.

**What unsystematic use threatens:**
- Formally, any researcher who signs off on a submission meets criterion 3. But substantively, approving a version whose production process is opaque to you is a weaker form of approval than approving a version you can trace and explain. The accountability criterion (4) is where this distinction becomes sharp.

### Criterion 4: Agreement to be accountable for all aspects of the work

**What systematic use provides:**
- This is where systematic AI authorship has its clearest advantage. To be genuinely accountable for the work — to be able to investigate questions about accuracy and integrity, to explain why a decision was made, to reproduce the steps that produced the output — the process must be documented and traceable.
- Systematic AI use produces exactly this: a replication package that allows the researcher (and reviewers) to trace every step. If a result is questioned, the researcher can show the search string that produced the literature, the rubric that determined inclusion, the synthesis query that generated the claim.
- The working logs document decisions as they were made — contemporaneously, not reconstructed — which is the kind of evidence that satisfies integrity investigations.

**What unsystematic use threatens:**
- A researcher who used AI unsystematically cannot fully discharge criterion 4. If asked to explain why a specific claim was made, or to verify that the AI-generated synthesis accurately represents the sources, they have no documented process to point to. They agreed to be accountable for work they cannot fully account for. This is not necessarily negligence — it is a structural consequence of undocumented process.

**The argument:** This is the sharpest point. Accountability requires traceability. Systematic AI authorship is designed to produce traceability as a byproduct. Unsystematic use structurally undermines criterion 4 regardless of good faith.

## The synthesis argument

The Vancouver criteria collectively define what it means to be an author rather than merely a contributor: substantial intellectual contribution, critical engagement with the content, endorsement, and accountability. These criteria were designed for human co-authorship contexts but apply to AI-assisted work in a revealing way.

Systematic AI authorship is a workflow designed to ensure that the researcher meets all four criteria in a documentable, verifiable way:
- Criterion 1: documented by search strings, rubrics, configurations, and verification records
- Criterion 2: documented by author-input files, revision logs, and skill specifications
- Criterion 3: enacted through explicit human verification checkpoints at each stage
- Criterion 4: enabled by the replication package and working logs that make the process fully traceable

Unsystematic AI use, by contrast, threatens criteria 1, 2, and 4 — not necessarily because the researcher lacks intellectual contribution, but because the contribution cannot be demonstrated and the accountability cannot be fully discharged. A researcher can use AI unsystematically and still be the genuine intellectual author of their work. But they cannot show it — and under Vancouver criteria, authorship is not merely a matter of having made contributions but of being able to stand behind them.

This framing reframes the paper's central argument in a more pointed way: systematic AI authorship is not just good epistemic practice — it is what responsible authorship looks like in the context of AI-assisted research. The Vancouver criteria, applied rigorously, demand it.

## Implications for the paper

- The authorship framework provides independent grounding for the systematic/unsystematic distinction: this isn't just an epistemic preference, it's what the established standard for scholarly authorship requires.
- The AI-cannot-be-author argument follows directly from criteria 3 and 4, without needing to make philosophical claims about creativity or consciousness.
- The query authorship concept maps cleanly onto criterion 2 — "drafting for important intellectual content" — and criterion 1 — "conception and design." This is probably the cleanest justification for query authorship in the paper.
- Criterion 4/accountability is the strongest argument for requiring documented process: you cannot be accountable for what you cannot trace.
- Potential location in the paper: could anchor §5 (epistemic properties) or appear as an argument in §6 (policy implications) — or both in different registers. The Vancouver connection gives the policy argument normative grounding that doesn't depend on the paper's own framework.
