# Assessment of the v7 → v8 Edit
## "Query Authorship: A Framework for Systematic AI Use in Social Science"

*Assessed: 2026-05-04*

---

## Overall verdict

The edit is competent but uneven. The word reduction was real and necessary, and some cuts are clearly correct. However, three substantive losses are more than cosmetic — they weaken arguments the paper depends on — and one structurally important passage was compressed past the point where it still does its work.

---

## 1. The honesty-pledge passage — cut too hard (moderate importance)

In v7 the retraction of the honesty-pledge research is developed with the pointed observation that the empirical warrant for disclosure-based compliance mechanisms was itself fabricated — a neat epistemic irony with real rhetorical force. In v8 this is reduced to two sentences that report the retraction but drop the "precisely the field that generated it" observation and the explicit connection to the mechanism declaration-based AI policy needs. The rhetorical payoff — that the instrument adopted to ensure compliance lost its best empirical defence — is barely visible in v8. This matters because it is one of only two independent grounds the paper offers for why declaration-based policy is insufficient, and the mechanism ground is the stronger one.

---

## 2. The writing-as-thinking passage — substantially reorganised, with a real loss (high importance)

v7 treats writing-as-thinking as a standalone sub-argument in the systematic/unsystematic section: unsystematic AI use bypasses deliberate semantics at the composition stage, but systematic use *relocates* them to the specification layer rather than eliminating them. This is a non-obvious move that does real work — it pre-empts the objection that even systematic AI use bypasses the cognitive benefits of writing. In v8, this argument is split across two locations: a compressed version appears at the end of the systematic/unsystematic section, and a further compressed version appears in the drafting sub-section. Neither location contains the full argument, and the crucial point — that the specification layer is the new site of deliberate semantics, not a bypass of it — is present but attenuated. This is one of the paper's more original sub-arguments and it is now undercooked.

---

## 3. The cross-model persona review passage — truncated, losing the key methodological point (moderate importance)

In v7 the cross-model review section names the specific six personas (theoretical sociologist, philosopher of mind, applied microeconomist, computational political scientist, regulatory committee perspective, developer perspective) and explains why this is an instance of query authorship: each persona encodes a structural epistemic position likely to resist the argument from a direction the author cannot fully anticipate. v8 replaces this with generic language about "disciplinary and epistemological perspectives" and drops the hostile-persona suggestion entirely, including the note that this "proved surprisingly fruitful." The methodological self-demonstration function of this paper is partly in the detail — a reader who wants to implement this needs to see what persona calibration actually looks like. The truncation here makes the advice abstract where it should be concrete.

---

## 4. The authorship-criteria section — actually improved

This is the cleanest section in v8. The v7 version was discursive; v8 sharpens it considerably. Criterion 4 now gets the sharpest treatment of any criterion, which is correct since that is where the argument is strongest. No real loss here.

---

## 5. The "what systematic use cannot guarantee" section — some sentences lost that deserved to stay (moderate importance)

v7 contains an important sentence in this section: "The argument here is not that all AI use must be systematic, any more than the case for replication packages implies that all analysis must be automated." And the inference from absence: "if AI workflow artefacts are not available, one must assume the process was unsystematic. These inferences are not certain, but the absence of evidence forces a conservative prior." v8 drops both. These are not merely rhetorical gestures — the first forecloses a predictable misreading (that this is a prescriptive framework), and the second makes the policy logic actionable by connecting it to the inference a reader or editor should draw. Their loss weakens the transition into the policy section.

---

## 6. He et al. (2026) cross-disciplinary convergence — lost, minor consequence (low importance)

v7 uses He et al. to make the point that an AI accountability framework developed independently in ML arrives at a similar minimum artefact set. This disciplinary convergence argument is a useful rhetorical move — it shows the framework is not sociology-specific — but it is genuinely minor and its removal does not damage the core argument.

---

## Summary ranking by importance

| Loss | Importance |
|------|-----------|
| Writing-as-thinking relocated but not completed | **High** — one of the paper's more original sub-arguments is now unclear |
| Honesty-pledge mechanistic argument compressed | **Moderate** — weakens the independent grounds for rejecting declaration-based policy |
| Cross-model personas stripped of concrete detail | **Moderate** — undermines the self-demonstration function |
| "Not all AI use must be systematic" and inference-from-absence | **Moderate** — preempts misreadings and makes policy logic actionable |
| He et al. convergence argument | **Low** — rhetorical only |

---

## Recommendation

The edit succeeded in cutting length but saved words in the wrong places. The sections that most needed to stay detailed — the writing-as-thinking argument and the persona review methodology — are the ones that were compressed most aggressively. The sections that were already tight (authorship criteria) were tightened further, which was the right call. A revision pass specifically targeting items 1–4 above, without restoring the overall length, would be the correct next step.
