# Note: Optimism balance in abstract and introduction

**Source:** Colleague feedback (2026-04-17)

**Observation:** The abstract and introduction read as overly optimistic. The paper is well-balanced and thorough in the body (§§3–6 have extensive caveats, §5.4 is explicitly about limits), but the framing material pitches the benefits of systematic AI authorship without sufficient acknowledgement of well-known concerns about AI use.

**The gap:** The abstract says what systematic AI authorship achieves (externalises decisions, produces artefacts, consistent with open-science norms) without mentioning what it cannot do. §1's five announced moves are all affirmative. The concerns about AI appear only later — sycophancy, generalisation bias, non-determinism, potential for faked documentation — and a reader who stops at the abstract or introduction does not encounter them.

**Possible approaches (to evaluate on return):**

1. **Single hedging sentence in the abstract.** Something that acknowledges systematic AI authorship is a necessary condition for epistemic soundness, not a sufficient one — which §5.4 already says but the abstract does not. Could be added without restructuring.

2. **A qualifying clause in §1's first paragraph.** The current opening ("The question is no longer whether but how") reads as fully positive about AI adoption. A clause acknowledging that not all AI use is epistemically sound, and that the paper's argument presupposes human judgement at every stage, would temper this.

3. **Explicit acknowledgement in §1's five moves.** Move 1 currently says systematic and unsystematic use differ "in epistemic standing." Adding a brief acknowledgement that even systematic use involves risks (non-determinism, miscalibrated criteria, superficial verification) before naming the key difference might be enough.

4. **A sixth move or a framing sentence at the top of §1.** The five moves are all constructive. A brief framing sentence before them that acknowledges the legitimate concerns motivating restrictive policies (and names one or two) would position the argument as responsive rather than promotional.

**Consideration:** The paper does address known concerns — §5.4 covers limitations in detail, §6.1 explicitly commends current policies for responding to real concerns. The issue is presentational: those acknowledgements are buried in the body while the framing material is unreservedly positive. The fix is probably light — a sentence or two in the abstract and/or §1, not a structural change.
