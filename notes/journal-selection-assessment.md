# Journal selection assessment: Sociological Science vs. Sociological Methods and Research

**Date:** 2026-04-26  
**Status:** Decision pending — author input needed

## The candidates

**Sociological Science (SocSci)**  
Diamond open access; no APC; no word limit (brevity valued); all sociological scholarship types; replication package mandatory since April 2023; no explicit AI policy (noted in the paper as consistent with the argument). Known for rapid turnaround (weeks, not months). Newer journal (est. 2014) but growing in prestige.

**Sociological Methods and Research (SMR)**  
SAGE quarterly; established 1972; high impact factor for a methodology journal (historically 5–7). Devoted to sociology as a cumulative empirical science — collection, measurement, and analysis of data; new techniques and innovative approaches to recurring research challenges. Publishes review articles, original presentations, and articles clarifying methodological problems. Methods scope extends across adjacent disciplines (criminology, political science, economics, etc.). Gold OA available via SAGE Choice (significant APC, typically $3,000+); not open access by default. ASA citation style. Abstract: 150 words maximum. No specific word limit found; quantitative-methods orientation throughout. Turnaround reportedly faster than typical SAGE journals but no specific data obtained.

## Assessment

**The core tension** is between audience breadth and methodological prestige.

**Case for SMR.** The paper is a methodological contribution — a workflow paper with epistemic and policy implications. SMR's scope is an unusually clean fit: it explicitly publishes papers that clarify methodological problems and introduce new approaches to recurring research challenges, which is precisely what this paper does. Methodologists who read SMR are the natural early adopters of systematic AI workflows. The journal's multi-disciplinary scope (criminology, political science, economics) also means the paper could reach beyond sociology if the methods argument lands well. SMR's higher impact factor would increase the paper's visibility in citation databases, which matters if the goal is to establish the systematic/unsystematic distinction as a recognised methodological concept.

**Case for Sociological Science.** The paper's policy argument is directed at journal editors, research funders, and sociologists broadly — not only methodologists. Qualitative sociologists, interpretive researchers, and theoretical sociologists who use AI unsystematically are precisely the readers who most need exposure to the systematic/unsystematic distinction. SMR's readership is disproportionately quantitative, which means the paper would largely be preaching to a sympathetic but already-converted audience: quantitative researchers already have established replication norms and are more likely to use structured workflows by habit. The Diamond OA model also matters: if the paper is paywalled, its practical reach among early-career researchers and those at institutions with limited SAGE access is reduced. Sociological Science's replication package requirement is itself an example of the documentation norm the paper argues for — a fact the paper notes explicitly, and which provides a strong rhetorical fit.

**The author's own framing** of the key tradeoff — SMR is better known but the audience is more quantitative, limiting reach to the qualitative branch — is accurate and important. The paper's argument is most actionable for researchers who do not already have strong replication norms: qualitative sociologists, mixed-methods researchers, and those doing literature-based theoretical work. These are Sociological Science readers, not primarily SMR readers.

**A third option worth considering.** *Sociological Methods and Research* and *Sociological Science* are not the only candidates. *Qualitative Sociology* or *Sociology of Health and Illness* (if the argument is framed for specific audience segments) would reach the qualitative branch directly. *Science and Engineering Ethics* might be a fit if the AI governance angle is emphasised. However, none of these have the combination of methodological prestige, open access, and rapid turnaround that makes Sociological Science compelling.

---

## Recommendation

**Retain Sociological Science as primary target.** The reasons are cumulative: Diamond OA maximises reach; the audience is broader and includes the qualitative researchers who are most in need of the argument; the journal's own replication package requirement is cited in the paper as an instance of the documentation norm, creating a rhetorical coherence between venue and argument; and the rapid turnaround aligns with the time-sensitive nature of the AI use discussion.

**Consider SMR as fallback if SocSci declines.** If Sociological Science passes, SMR is the natural second submission. The scope fit is real, and the methodological audience would engage seriously with the epistemic and workflow arguments. The open access constraint could be addressed by depositing the preprint on SocArXiv (which is already planned), ensuring broad availability regardless of paywall.

**The qualitative-reach concern is real but not decisive.** A paper published in Sociological Science will be read — and can be assigned — across subdisciplinary boundaries in a way that a paper in SMR will not. The prestige gap between the two journals is narrowing; Sociological Science's impact has grown steadily since 2014.
