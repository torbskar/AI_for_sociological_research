This is a sophisticated and timely draft. You’ve done an excellent job anchoring the "systematic AI authorship" argument in the Open Science and "garden of forking paths" literature.

To make this feel more at home in a top-tier sociology journal like *Sociological Science*, you might consider bridging your methodological framework with established sociological theories of **knowledge production, agency, and transparency.** Your argument about "externalizing tacit commitments" is essentially a sociology-of-science claim.

Here are five relevant sociological works/authors that would deepen the article’s disciplinary roots:

### ---

**1\. Bruno Latour & Steve Woolgar: *Laboratory Life* (and the concept of "Inscriptions")**

Your argument hinges on turning an invisible, "black-boxed" AI interaction into a "documented output." Latour and Woolgar’s work on how scientists turn messy observations into "inscriptions" (papers, graphs, logs) to create authority is highly relevant.

* **Why it fits:** You are essentially proposing a new "inscription device" for the age of AI. Using Latour’s terminology of **"opening the black box"** would provide a strong theoretical foundation for why the systematic workflow (the logs and skill files) creates scientific facticity where unsystematic use creates "bullshit."

### **2\. Mario L. Small: "‘How many cases do I need?’ On science and logic in qualitative research" (2009)**

While your paper covers empirical data broadly, its heart is in the "interpretive" and "literature-heavy" work. Mario Small has been a leading voice in arguing that qualitative/interpretive sociology needs a different but equally rigorous "logic of inquiry."

* **Why it fits:** You mention Freese and Peterson (2017) regarding the gap in documenting qualitative decisions. Small’s work provides the **sociological justification** for why formalizing the "logic of selection" (your screening rubrics) is a standard sociological requirement, even when the "data" is text rather than numbers.

### **3\. Andrew Abbott: *Methods of Discovery: Heuristics for the Social Sciences* (2004)**

Your concept of **"Query Authorship"** is, at its core, a discussion of heuristics. Abbott argues that sociological "genius" often lies in the heuristics used to frame a problem or search for an answer.

* **Why it fits:** You could frame your systematic criteria as **"formalized heuristics."** It moves the focus from the AI "writing" the text to the human "designing the discovery." This aligns with Abbott’s view that the "method" is the search strategy itself.

### **4\. Marion Fourcade & Kieran Healy: "Seeing like an Algorithm" (2017) or "The Ordinal Society"**

These authors are the heavyweights in the sociology of algorithms. They discuss how algorithmic classification "sees" and "sorts" the world, often reinforcing existing structures.

* **Why it fits:** In §3.1, you discuss the failure modes of AI (sycophancy and bias). Referencing Fourcade and Healy would connect your "adversarial configuration" (§4.5) to a broader sociological concern: that **un-steered algorithms** will naturally reproduce the "ordinal" biases of their training data. It gives your "Reviewer Persona" method a clear sociological "why."

### **5\. Karin Knorr-Cetina: *Epistemic Cultures: How the Sciences Make Knowledge* (1999)**

Knorr-Cetina explores how different disciplines have different "machineries of knowing." She discusses the role of "liminal space" and "tacit knowledge" in high-energy physics and molecular biology.

* **Why it fits:** Your argument in §5.1 about **externalizing tacit knowledge** is a direct intervention in what Knorr-Cetina calls the "epistemic culture" of sociology. By referencing her, you can argue that systematic AI authorship is not just a "checklist" but a shift in the **sociological machinery of knowing**—moving from the "private craft" of the lone scholar to a "verifiable protocol."
