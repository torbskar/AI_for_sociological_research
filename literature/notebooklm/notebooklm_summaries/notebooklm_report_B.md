## 2. Tacit knowledge in research

*Q2: How do sources treat tacit knowledge in research — as a problem, a
resource, or both? What role does making reasoning explicit play? Are there
accounts of what tacit knowledge consists of in practice?*

Sources attribute several critical epistemic functions to pre-registration, open science, and transparency practices, framing them as essential tools to align scientific practice with scientific ideals such as communality, organized skepticism, and disinterestedness (Grant et al., 2022). These practices are argued to solve systematic failures in research credibility—collectively termed the "replication crisis" or "method crisis"—by increasing the transparency, robustness, and reliability of scientific findings (Nakagawa et al., 2025; Soliman et al., 2025; Toribio-Flórez et al., 2021).

### Regulating the Research Process and Reducing Bias

A primary epistemic function of these practices is the reduction of **researcher degrees of freedom**, which refers to the undisclosed flexibility in data collection and analysis that allows researchers to present almost any result as statistically significant (Grant et al., 2022; Soliman et al., 2025; Sweeten, 2020).

* **Problem:** Sources identify "questionable research practices" (QRPs) such as $p$-hacking (manipulating analyses for significance), HARKing (Hypothesizing After the Results are Known), and selective reporting as the primary culprits behind inflated false-positive rates (Al-Hoorie & Hiver, 2021; Nakagawa et al., 2025; van den Akker et al., 2023). These practices are often driven by a "publish or perish" culture that favors novel and "flashy" findings over rigorous evidence (Lawson et al., 2025; Silverstein et al., 2024; Torka et al., 2023).
* **Mechanism:** **Pre-registration** solves this by forcing researchers to distinguish between **prediction** (confirmatory research) and **postdiction** (exploratory research) (Belardinelli & Zhu, 2025; Nosek et al., 2018). By documenting hypotheses and analysis plans ex-ante in a time-stamped registry, researchers are prevented from pretending that post-hoc results were predicted all along (Mendes-Da-Silva, 2023; van den Akker et al., 2023).
* **Structured Workflows:** For secondary data analysis, where researchers already have access to datasets, the **Explore and Confirm Analysis Workflow (ECAW)** is proposed to achieve similar goals by making access to a full dataset contingent on the prior registration of an executable analysis script based on a data subset (Workflow team, 2023). Additionally, **Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)** and checklists are identified as mechanisms to guide analytic decisions and ensure that methods are not "tortured" to produce desired outcomes (Datta et al., 2024; Mendes-Da-Silva, 2023).

### Mitigating Publication Bias and the "File-Drawer Problem"

Transparency practices are argued to solve the systemic distortion of the scientific record caused by the suppression of null or negative findings (Al-Hoorie & Hiver, 2021; Deressa et al., 2023; Manago, 2023).

* **Problem:** Traditional peer review evaluates research based on its results, creating an incentive for editors and researchers to hide non-significant results in the "file drawer," which leads to exaggerated effect sizes and unreliable meta-analyses (Grant et al., 2022; Soliman et al., 2025; van den Akker et al., 2023).
* **Mechanism:** **Registered Reports** address this by shifting the evaluation focus from results to the quality of the research question and the rigor of the methodology (Soliman et al., 2025; van den Akker et al., 2023). Authors receive "in-principle acceptance" before data collection, guaranteeing publication regardless of the outcome (Chambers, 2013; Silverstein et al., 2023).

### Facilitating Verification, Reuse, and Efficiency

Open science practices are attributed the function of making research artifacts "findable, accessible, interoperable, and reusable" (**FAIR**) to enable cumulative knowledge production (Bijak et al., 2022; Ross-Hellauer et al., 2021).

* **Problem:** Methodological descriptions are often too vague or insufficient to allow independent replication without direct author contact, leading to "research waste" when unreliable findings are pursued or when data is collected redundantly (Botvinik-Nezer et al., 2022; Nakagawa et al., 2025; van den Akker et al., 2023).
* **Mechanism:** Sharing **data, code, and materials** allows independent researchers to verify reported results, check for errors, and conduct sensitivity analyses or meta-analyses (Grant et al., 2022; Silverstein et al., 2024).
* **Documentation and Provenance:** **Formal provenance modeling** (e.g., the PROV standard) and digital notebooks enable a structured audit trail of research decisions, allowing the scientific community to query and update findings as new discoveries are made (Bijak et al., 2022; Grant et al., 2022). Notably, AI tools are explicitly identified as a means to generate detailed documentation of data processing and analysis steps, facilitating this transparency.

### Epistemic Credibility and Accountability

Practices of transparency are argued to reduce **knowledge asymmetry** between research producers and consumers, thereby restoring public trust in science (Adjovi, 2025; Silverstein et al., 2024).

* **Explicit Arguments:** Sources argue that making the "subjective reality" of research visible—through positionality statements and reflexivity—is essential for rigor and trustworthiness, particularly in qualitative traditions where the researcher is an active participant (Cole et al., 2023; Malik & Terzidis, 2025; Silverstein et al., 2024).
* **Nuances and Disagreements:** Leonelli (2023) explicitly critiques the "openness as sharing" model, arguing that indiscriminate sharing ignores the situated nature of knowledge and can cause **epistemic injustice** by privileging resource-rich institutions and flattening epistemic diversity. Instead, she proposes openness as "judicious connection," where transparency is a tool for critical scrutiny rather than an end in itself. Some sources also warn that pre-registration might function as a " symbolic symbol" of legitimacy without actually improving quality if deviations from plans are not transparently reported (Belardinelli & Zhu, 2025; Penders, 2022).

### Connections Between Structured Workflows and Epistemic Quality

Sources draw explicit connections between structured or documented research workflows and the enhancement of epistemic quality, research integrity, and validity. Pre-registration and Registered Reports are characterized as bureaucratic tools designed to safeguard the scientific process by forcing a clear distinction between prediction (confirmatory research) and postdiction (exploratory research) (Belardinelli & Zhu, 2025; Nosek et al., 2018). This structure is argued to increase the reliability of scientific results by effectively "shutting down" tempting researcher degrees of freedom that might otherwise be exploited to achieve statistical significance (Decker et al., 2023; Penders, 2022). By documenting hypotheses and analysis plans in advance, researchers prevent questionable practices such as $p$-hacking and HARKing, which are identified as primary drivers of the "replication crisis" (AlHoorie & Hiver, 2021; van den Akker et al., 2023).

The implementation of "auditable researcher decisions" is presented as a mechanism to transform science from a "trust me" model to one based on verifiable evidence (Grant et al., 2022). For instance, the practice of "showing your work" in its entirety—including intentions and workflows—is argued to facilitate the testing of generalizability and enhance the quality control of published work (Mendes-Da-Silva, 2023). In the context of secondary data analysis, the **Explore and Confirm Analysis Workflow (ECAW)** is proposed as a method that affords protection from researcher bias by making access to a full dataset contingent on the prior registration of an executable analysis script (Workflow team, 2023). Furthermore, formal provenance modeling is argued to provide a structured basis for forming assessments about the quality, reliability, and trustworthiness of research artifacts (Bijak et al., 2022).

### Protocols and Frameworks for Making Research Reasoning Explicit

Sources propose several specific protocols and frameworks designed to force the articulation of research reasoning throughout the inquiry process.

* **Explore and Confirm Analysis Workflow (ECAW):** This five-step protocol is designed for secondary data analysis to ensure reasoning is explicit before the full dataset is observed (Workflow team, 2023). First, a research team submits a paragraph describing their intended analysis and a list of variables to a data management organization (Workflow team, 2023). Second, the organization provides access to a small, random subset of the data (e.g., 1,000 out of 10,000 participants) (Workflow team, 2023). Third, the researchers use this subset to prepare and register an executable analysis script (e.g., in R or Stata) (Workflow team, 2023). Fourth, the organization performs a quality check to ensure the script and reasoning are registered (Workflow team, 2023). Finally, the researchers receive access to the full dataset to run the script, with any subsequent deviations being transparently recorded (Workflow team, 2023).
* **Hybrid AI-Augmented Systematic Literature Review (SLR) Framework:** This framework combines AI’s computational power with human "reflective agency" through five interrelated stages (Malik & Terzidis, 2025). Stage 1 involves "landscaping" and selecting an AI model based on domain specificity and adaptability (Malik & Terzidis, 2025). Stage 2 requires the integration of a curated knowledge base that aligns with the review’s epistemological principles (Malik & Terzidis, 2025). Stage 3 focuses on iterative "prompt engineering," where researchers craft specific instructions to guide the AI’s synthesis (Malik & Terzidis, 2025). Stage 4 involves cyclical validation, using inter-model comparisons and sensitivity testing to ensure reliability (Malik & Terzidis, 2025). Stage 5 is the final deployment of the refined workflow for study selection and communication (Malik & Terzidis, 2025).
* **Formal Provenance Modeling (PROV):** This standard uses a directed acyclic graph to document the research process, explicitly relating "entities" (e.g., theories, data specifications) to "activities" (e.g., calibration, validation) and "agents" (people or software involved) (Bijak et al., 2022). This creates a queryable, structured record that allows others to trace the reasoning and individual steps leading from an initial observation to a final conclusion (Bijak et al., 2022).
* **Registered Reports (RR):** This two-stage publishing format splits the peer-review process to emphasize methodological rigor (Silverstein et al., 2023). In Stage 1, researchers submit their background, hypotheses, and detailed analysis plans for review _before_ data collection begins (Silverstein et al., 2023). If accepted, they receive "in-principle acceptance," and in Stage 2, reviewers only evaluate whether the authors adhered to the pre-specified plan and made accurate conclusions based on the resulting evidence (Silverstein et al., 2023).

### Templates, Checklists, and Worked Examples

Sources provide various tools to implement structured research practices across different domains:

* **Secondary Data Preregistration Template:** A comprehensive 25-question template designed to capture researcher degrees of freedom in existing datasets, including specific prompts for handling outliers and specifying robustness checks (van den Akker et al., 2021).
* **Transparency and Openness Promotion (TOP) Guidelines:** A modular system consisting of eight standards, including citation standards, data transparency, analytic methods transparency, and study/analysis plan pre-registration (Silverstein et al., 2023).
* **ODD (Overview, Design concepts, and Details) Protocol:** A standardized protocol for documenting agent-based models; an extension, **ODD+D**, adds a specific focus on documenting researcher "Decisions" (Bijak et al., 2022).
* **ML Reproducibility Checklist:** A specific checklist for machine learning research that requests information on models, algorithms, theoretical claims, data, and experimental setups (Semmelrock et al., 2025).
* **PRISMA-ScR:** A checklist specifically for scoping reviews to ensure thorough mapping and reporting of heterogeneous evidence (Cole et al., 2023; Ross-Hellauer et al., 2021).
* **Replication Recipe:** A template with two parts—pre-completion and post-completion—for conducting high-quality replication studies (van den Akker et al., 2021).
* **Worked Examples:** van den Akker et al. (2021) provide a full worked example of a secondary data analysis preregistration, including sample answers and guiding comments. Hackett and Strickland (2019) is cited as a worked example of using the "framework approach" to analyze qualitative data through a "decision trail" (Cole et al., 2023).

## 4. Replication crisis and its lessons

*Q4: How do sources characterise the replication crisis — causes, scope,
proposed remedies? Which remedies involve making reasoning more explicit or
decisions more transparent?*

Sources characterize the replication crisis as a systemic "credibility crisis" or "method crisis" rooted in a misalignment between scientific ideals and the institutional incentives of modern academia (Adjovi, 2025; Nakagawa et al., 2025; Soliman et al., 2025). The crisis is defined by the widespread inability to independently recreate the results of published studies, casting doubt on the reliability of the scientific record across multiple disciplines (Baker, 2016; Camerer et al., 2018; Open Science Collaboration, 2015).

### Causes and Scope of the Replication Crisis

**Causes:** The crisis is attributed to several interrelated factors:

* **Questionable Research Practices (QRPs):** Researchers identify $p$-hacking (manipulating data to reach significance), HARKing (hypothesizing after results are known), and selective reporting as primary drivers of inflated false-positive rates (AlHoorie, 2021; Fox et al., 2022; van den Akker et al., 2023).
* **Institutional Incentives:** The "publish or perish" culture prioritizes novel, "flashy," and statistically significant findings over rigorous, reproducible evidence, leading to publication bias and the "file-drawer problem" where null results are suppressed (Adjovi, 2025; Grant et al., 2022; Soliman et al., 2025).
* **Researcher Degrees of Freedom:** The "undisclosed flexibility" in data collection and analysis allows researchers to make ad-hoc decisions that optimize for significance, often without being fully aware of the bias they introduce (Bochynska et al., 2023; Fox et al., 2022; Sweeten, 2020).
* **Theory Crisis:** In some fields, particularly psychology and biology, "vague verbal theories" allow for liberal interpretations, meaning researchers can fit almost any outcome into an underspecified theoretical framework (Nakagawa et al., 2024; Nakagawa et al., 2025).
* **Incomplete Reporting:** Methodological descriptions in journals are often too brief or vague to allow independent verification, a problem described as a lack of "preproducibility" (Botvinik-Nezer et al., 2022; Fišar et al., 2023; Stark, 2018).

**Scope:** While psychology is often cited as the epicenter, sources document replication challenges in biomedicine, economics, social sciences, ecology, evolution, geography, management, and machine learning (AlHoorie, 2021; Bochynska et al., 2023; Fišar et al., 2023; Kedron et al., 2023; Nakagawa et al., 2025; Semmelrock et al., 2025). However, some researchers in qualitative and interpretivist traditions argue that the very concept of a "replication crisis" is a poor fit for their epistemologies, which value situated and co-constructed knowledge over universal generalizability (Cole et al., 2023; Leonelli, 2023; Pownall, 2022).

### Proposed Remedies and the Role of Explicit Reasoning

Sources propose a variety of remedies, many of which explicitly focus on making research reasoning more transparent and auditable.

**Remedies for Explicit Reasoning and Transparency:**

* **Pre-registration:** This functions as a "forcing function" for researchers to articulate their hypotheses and analysis plans before observing data, thereby "shutting down" tempting degrees of freedom (Belardinelli & Zhu, 2025; Decker et al., 2023; Penders, 2022). It forces the distinction between prediction and postdiction to become explicit (Belardinelli & Zhu, 2025; Nosek et al., 2018).
* **Registered Reports (RRs):** RRs move the peer-review process forward, allowing reviewers to evaluate the reasoning and methodology _before_ data collection, which shifts the incentive from finding significant results to conducting rigorous research (Chambers & Tzavella, 2022; Silverstein et al., 2023).
* **Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) and Checklists:** These tools, such as the ML Reproducibility Checklist or PRISMA, require researchers to explicitly record and justify criteria for data cleaning, model selection, and reporting (Datta et al., 2024; Malik & Terzidis, 2025; Semmelrock et al., 2025).
* **Explore and Confirm Analysis Workflow (ECAW):** For secondary data analysis, this protocol makes full data access contingent on registering an executable analysis script based on a data subset, ensuring researcher reasoning is documented before the full results are known (Workflow team, 2023).
* **Adversarial Collaboration:** This involves researchers with opposing views working together on a single project, which forces the articulation and negotiation of tacit assumptions and "arbitrary" methodological choices (Isch et al., 2025).
* **Formal Provenance Modeling (PROV):** This standard uses structured, queryable documentation to record the "entities, activities, and people" involved in producing a finding, creating an auditable "decision trail" (Bijak et al., 2022).
* **Reflexivity and Positionality Statements:** In qualitative work, these practices make the researcher’s subjective reality and background visible, transforming tacit bias into an explicit component of the evidence (Cole et al., 2023; Malik & Terzidis, 2025; Silverstein et al., 2024).

**Other Remedies:**

* **Open Artifact Sharing:** Mandating the public sharing of raw data, code, and materials to enable independent computational reproduction (Fišar et al., 2023; Silverstein et al., 2023).
* **Statistical Reform:** Proposals to lower alpha thresholds (e.g., from 0.05 to 0.005) or adopt Bayesian statistics to more accurately calibrate evidence (Benjamin et al., 2018; Romero & Sprenger, 2021).
* **Multi-lab Collaborations:** Large-scale, crowdsourced projects that aggregate data across many sites to increase statistical power and test boundary conditions (AlHoorie, 2021; Soliman et al., 2025).

## 5. Synthesis

*Q5: Across sources, what is the core argument for why explicit, transparent,
and structured research practice is epistemically valuable? Where do sources
agree and where do they disagree?*

Across the sources, the core argument for why explicit, transparent, and structured research practice is epistemically valuable centers on the transition from a "trust me" model of science to an "auditable" model based on verifiable evidence (AlHoorie & Hiver, 2021; Grant et al., 2022; Mendes-Da-Silva, 2023). This transition is argued to be necessary because the traditional, less structured approach has allowed "practically unlimited researcher degrees of freedom," leading to a "hidden universe of uncertainty" that undermines the reliability of the scientific record (Botvinik-Nezer et al., 2022; Sweeten, 2020).

### The Core Epistemic Argument for Structure and Transparency

The primary epistemic value attributed to these practices is their ability to **protect the integrity of inference** by making the research process public and auditable (Grant et al., 2022; Silverstein et al., 2024).

* **Distinguishing Prediction from Postdiction:** Structured practices like pre-registration force researchers to distinguish between _confirmatory_ research (testing a priori hypotheses) and _exploratory_ research (finding patterns in data after the fact) (Belardinelli & Zhu, 2025; Nosek et al., 2018). This prevents the "garden of forking paths," where researchers selectively report only those analytic paths that yielded significant results (Nosek et al., 2022; Simmons et al., 2011).
* **Mitigating Cognitive and Systemic Biases:** By specifying decisions in advance, structured workflows act as a "forcing function" or "bureaucratic etiquette" that shuts down the temptation to engage in questionable research practices (QRPs) such as $p$-hacking and HARKing (Decker et al., 2023; Penders, 2022). This moves the focus of evaluation from "novelty" or "flashy results" to the rigor of the methodology and the importance of the research question (Silverstein et al., 2024; Soliman et al., 2025).
* **Reducing Knowledge Asymmetry:** Transparency reduces the gap between research producers and consumers, allowing the latter to evaluate "preproducibility"—whether the reported methods contain sufficient detail to be repeated at all—and to verify that tables and figures genuinely result from the claimed methods (Botvinik-Nezer et al., 2022; Silverstein et al., 2024; Stark, 2018).
* **Enabling Cumulative Knowledge:** Making research artifacts (data, code, materials) "findable, accessible, interoperable, and reusable" (FAIR) allows for independent verification, error detection, and the synthesis of evidence through meta-analysis, thereby preventing "research waste" (Bijak et al., 2022; Nakagawa et al., 2025; Ross-Hellauer et al., 2021).

### Points of Agreement Across Sources

* **Science as Self-Correction:** There is broad consensus that science's power lies in its self-correcting nature, but that this requires formal safeguards to ensure that errors are actually exposed and corrected (AlHoorie & Hiver, 2021; Romero, 2021; van den Akker et al., 2023).
* **The Problem of Publication Bias:** Sources agree that the systemic pressure to publish "positive" results has distorted the scientific literature, creating a "file-drawer problem" that necessitates structural changes like Registered Reports (Grant et al., 2022; Soliman et al., 2025; van den Akker et al., 2023).
* **The Value of Sharing Artifacts:** There is general agreement that sharing data and code is a "fundamental building block" for increasing transparency and facilitating reproducibility (Mendes-Da-Silva, 2023; Soliman et al., 2025).

### Points of Disagreement and Epistemic Nuance

* **Universal Applicability vs. Epistemic Pluralism:** A significant point of disagreement exists between those advocating for universal adoption of open science practices and qualitative researchers who argue that these practices are rooted in "quantitative, positivist standpoints" (Cole et al., 2023). Critics argue that universalizing norms like "reproducibility" can perpetuate "epistemic injustice" by marginalizing interpretivist and constructivist traditions that value situated, co-constructed knowledge over universal generalizability (Leonelli, 2023; Pownall et al., 2025; Ross-Hellauer et al., 2021).
* **"Openness as Sharing" vs. "Judicious Connection":** Leonelli (2023) explicitly critiques the "object-oriented" model of openness as merely sharing items. She proposes a "process-oriented" view where openness is about establishing "judicious connections" that recognize the history, context, and "baggage" of research artifacts, arguing that indiscriminate sharing can exacerbate inequalities between resource-rich and resource-poor institutions.
* **Bureaucracy as a Tool vs. Surveillance:** While many see structure as a helpful "etiquette," others warn that practices like pre-registration could become a "form of surveillance" or a "symbolic symbol" of legitimacy that adds burdensome paperwork without necessarily improving the actual quality of the science (Belardinelli & Zhu, 2025; Penders, 2022).
* **Actual vs. Perceived Value:** Sources disagree on the effectiveness of implementation. While some evidence shows Registered Reports improve quality (Soderberg et al., 2021), other reviews find that pre-registration often fails to deliver its intended benefits if deviations from plans are not transparently reported (Belardinelli & Zhu, 2025; van den Akker et al., 2023).
