## 1. Landscape of current policies

*Q1: What policies have journals and publishers adopted regarding AI use, and
how do they vary — across journals, publishers, and disciplines? Describe the
range from permissive to restrictive. Draw on all sources from Q0.*

Journal and publisher policies regarding the use of Generative AI (GAI) in scholarly publishing are characterized by a **rapidly evolving and highly heterogeneous landscape**. While a universal consensus has emerged that **AI cannot be listed as an author**, the allowable scope of AI assistance and the specific requirements for disclosure vary significantly across publishers, individual journals, and academic disciplines.

### The Range of Policies: Restrictive to Permissive

Policies regarding GAI usage currently span a wide spectrum of permissiveness:

* **Restrictive (Bans and Prohibitions):** The **Science** family of journals is frequently cited as the most restrictive, initially implementing a policy that **prohibits AI-generated text, figures, or images** unless the manuscript specifically focuses on AI or machine learning. Violation of this policy is considered scientific misconduct. Other journals, such as **Blood**, allow GAI for graphical outputs but strictly prohibit AI-generated text.
* **Moderate (Assistance with Disclosure):** The majority of major publishers, including **Springer Nature, Elsevier, Wiley, and Taylor & Francis**, have adopted a middle-ground approach. They typically **allow the use of GAI tools for improving the language and readability** of a manuscript (e.g., grammar and copy editing) provided that the use is transparently disclosed. However, they prohibit the use of AI for substantive intellectual tasks like drawing conclusions or interpreting data.
* **Permissive (Broad Support and Citability):** Some entities take a more flexible stance. **PLOS ONE**, for instance, allows AI tools as long as authors disclose the specific tools, the methodology of their application, and how content integrity was ensured. **Sage** is unique among top publishers for allowing researchers to **cite ChatGPT as a source** for written text, whereas others like Elsevier explicitly prohibit this. Some academic perspectives even argue that there should be **no concept of "too much help"** from AI, suggesting that as long as use is transparently reported, the source of valuable content is epistemically irrelevant.

### Variation Across Stakeholders and Disciplines

The adoption and content of these policies vary based on the type of organization and the field of study:

* **Publisher vs. Journal Policies:** There is a significant **lack of standardization** between publishers and their own affiliated journals. A bibliometric analysis found that while 87% of top journals had GAI guidelines, only 24% of the largest publishers provided them. In some cases, **journal policies directly conflict with those of their publishers**, creating confusion for authors.
* **Disciplinary Differences:**
  * **Medicine:** This field has seen the most proactive response, driven by bodies like the **ICMJE and WAME**. Approximately 80% of cardiology journals have established AI policies.
  * **Applied Linguistics:** Adoption has been slower, with **less than half (44.7%)** of high-impact journals in this field providing specific GAI guidelines as of late 2024.
  * **Computer Science:** Groups like the **International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML)** have prohibited papers including LLM-generated text unless that text is part of the experimental analysis.
  * **Social Work:** Journals are increasingly calling for policies based on professional values to manage AI's impact on scholarship.

### Key Ethical Pillars and Disclosure Requirements

Regardless of the level of permissiveness, several core themes dominate the policy landscape:

1. **Human-Exclusive Authorship:** All major publishers and governing bodies (COPE, ICMJE, Nature, Science) agree that **AI tools do not meet the requirements for authorship**. AI lacks the legal personality and accountability needed to take responsibility for submitted work.
2. **Ultimate Human Accountability:** Authors bear full responsibility for the **accuracy and integrity** of the entire manuscript, including any parts generated by AI. This includes being liable for ethical breaches, plagiarism, or "hallucinations" (fabricated information).
3. **Mandatory Transparency:** Disclosure is nearly universally required, though the location varies. Common requirements include documenting AI use in the **Materials and Methods** section, the **Acknowledgments**, or the **Cover Letter**.
4. **Fluidity and Change:** Most publishers explicitly state that their AI policies are **subject to regular review and adaptation** due to the rapid advancement of the technology.

In summary, while the academic community is moving toward standardized frameworks like the **GAIDeT taxonomy**, the current environment remains fragmented, placing a heavy burden on authors to navigate individual journal requirements.

## 2. Binary versus graduated policies

*Q2: Do policies distinguish between types or degrees of AI use, or do they
apply uniform rules? In particular, do any policies distinguish between
undisclosed or unverified AI use and documented, structured use?*

Journal and publisher policies have increasingly moved away from uniform rules, instead adopting **nuanced, tiered frameworks** that distinguish between different types and degrees of AI involvement. These policies prioritize human accountability and specifically contrast the ethical status of **undisclosed or unverified use** with that of **documented, structured use**.

### Distinctions Based on Types and Degrees of Use

Modern policies generally categorize AI applications into levels of "stakes" or "substance" to determine the required level of disclosure and oversight:

* **Substantive or High-Stakes Use:** This includes using AI for core intellectual tasks such as **generating hypotheses, writing primary manuscript sections, or performing data analysis**. Most policies mandate **transparent disclosure** and rigorous human oversight for these tasks, as they directly influence the research results.
* **Supportive or Medium-Stakes Use:** This involves tasks where the author retains primary control, such as **brainstorming, outlining, or summarizing literature**. In these cases, disclosure is often **optional or recommended** rather than mandatory.
* **Routine or Low-Stakes Use:** This category covers **language enhancement** (e.g., grammar and spell checks like Grammarly) and **administrative tasks** (e.g., formatting references). A broad consensus exists that these uses generally **do not require disclosure**, as they do not generate novel intellectual content. However, some publishers, including **IEEE, MDPI, and Sage**, still require disclosure for any AI-assisted tools used in the manuscript preparation.

### Undisclosed vs. Documented and Structured Use

Policies draw a sharp ethical line between **undisclosed AI use** and **structured, transparent documentation**:

* **Undisclosed Use as Misconduct:** Failure to disclose the role of AI in generating text is frequently characterized as **"epistemic dishonesty," "soft plagiarism," or "algiarism"**. Leading journals like those in the **Science** family initially implemented strict, uniform bans on AI-generated content, viewing any violation as scientific misconduct equivalent to image manipulation or plagiarism.
* **Documented and Structured Use for Accountability:** To move beyond simple disclaimers, emerging frameworks like the **GAIDeT (Generative AI Delegation Taxonomy)** provide structured systems for authors to document exactly which tasks (macro and micro) were delegated to AI. This structured approach is designed to make the research process **auditable** and ensure that human authors remain the "site of agency".
* **The Requirement for Verification:** Policies emphasize that **disclosure alone is insufficient**; authors must meticulously **verify the accuracy** of all AI-generated content. Documented use that remains **unverified** is still considered a risk to research integrity, as AI is known to "hallucinate" fabricated data and references.

In summary, while early responses to AI were often uniform bans, the current landscape is defined by **proactive, solution-oriented governance** that uses tiered models to balance the benefits of AI assistance with the non-negotiable requirement for human accountability and transparency.

## 3. Disclosure and documentation requirements

*Q3: What disclosure or documentation requirements do policies impose — what
must authors declare, in what format, and at what stage of submission? What
rationale is given for these requirements?*

Journal and publisher policies regarding the use of generative AI (GAI) emphasize a transition from simple acknowledgment to **structured, transparent documentation**. While requirements vary across stakeholders, a clear consensus has emerged on what needs to be declared, the specific formats for doing so, and the ethical rationale driving these mandates.

### What Authors Must Declare

Authors are generally required to provide a detailed account of the AI’s role in the research and writing process. Specific requirements often include:

* **Technical Details:** The specific **name, version, and model** of the AI tool used (e.g., ChatGPT-4, May 12 Version).
* **Method of Interaction:** The **specific prompts** used to generate content and the date the queries were made.
* **Scope of Use:** A clear description of **how the tool was applied**—whether for literature searching, data analysis, image creation, or linguistic polishing—and exactly **which sections of the manuscript** were affected.
* **Human Oversight:** An attestation that the authors have **critically reviewed and edited** the AI-generated content and take full responsibility for its accuracy.

### Format and Location of Disclosure

Policies specify several locations and formats for these declarations, depending on the nature of the AI's contribution:

* **Materials and Methods Section:** This is the most common location for disclosing AI used in the research process, such as data collection, analysis, or technical drafting.
* **Acknowledgments:** Often used for AI assistance in language editing or brainstorming, though some ethicists argue this is inappropriate as AI cannot consent to being acknowledged.
* **Dedicated AI Statement or Template:** Some publishers, such as **Elsevier and Sage**, provide specific templates or require a new, independent section for AI disclosures.
* **Formal Citations and References:** Many guidelines, including **APA style**, recommend citing the AI tool in-text and including a formal entry in the reference list to improve findability and indexing.
* **Supplementary Material:** Authors are encouraged to record and submit the **full interaction logs (appendices)** with the LLM to allow for external verification.

### Stage of Submission

Documentation requirements typically begin at the **initial stage of submission**. Authors are often required to disclose AI use in their **cover letter** to the editor or via mandatory **self-attestation checkboxes** within the journal's submission portal.

### Rationale for Requirements

The enforcement of these documentation standards is built on several key ethical and scientific pillars:

* **Non-Negotiable Accountability:** Because AI lacks legal personality and cannot be held responsible for errors or "hallucinations," human authors must be clearly identified as the sole site of agency and responsibility.
* **Fair Allocation of Credit:** Disclosure prevents "epistemic dishonesty" or "soft plagiarism" by ensuring human authors do not receive undue credit for work performed by a machine.
* **Reproducibility and Auditability:** Transparent reporting of prompts and models allows other researchers to understand exactly how results were derived, which is essential for the **validity and replication** of the research.
* **Preservation of Integrity:** Comprehensive disclosure allows editors and reviewers to scrutinize AI-assisted work for potential biases, fabricated data, or copyright infringements that the AI might have introduced.
* **Trust and Transparency:** Maintaining a clear record of AI involvement protects the **trust relationship** between authors and their audience, ensuring that readers are not misled about the origin of the intellectual work.

## 4. Authorship and integrity

*Q4: How do sources treat AI and authorship — can AI be listed as an author,
and on what grounds? What research integrity concerns are raised in relation
to AI use, and how are they proposed to be addressed?*

The sources generally treat **authorship as a uniquely human endeavor** based on the core principles of agency and legal standing. There is a broad consensus among major editorial bodies—such as COPE, ICMJE, and WAME—and global publishers that **AI tools cannot be listed as authors**. The primary grounds for this prohibition are that AI **cannot assume legal or ethical responsibility** for the work, nor can it manage copyright, license agreements, or assert the absence of conflicts of interest. While some early publications controversially included ChatGPT as a co-author, these cases were met with immediate scrutiny and often resulted in formal corrections to remove the AI from the byline. Some scholars argue that if AI makes a "substantial contribution," it should receive credit, but most sources maintain that **credit and accountability are inseparable**, and since AI cannot be held accountable, it cannot be an author.

Significant **research integrity concerns** are raised regarding the use of AI in scholarly publishing:

* **Hallucination:** AI systems frequently generate factually incorrect information or fabricate "ghost" references that sound scientifically plausible.
* **Plagiarism and "Algiarism":** There is a risk of authors presenting machine-generated text as their own original work without attribution, a phenomenon sometimes called "soft plagiarism".
* **Algorithmic Bias:** AI outputs can perpetuate or amplify biases present in their training data, leading to unfair or discriminatory research outcomes.
* **Erosion of Rigor:** Over-reliance on AI may lead to "cognitive offloading," where essential academic skills like critical thinking, deep analysis, and authorial voice are degraded.
* **Data Privacy:** Uploading sensitive research, patient data, or confidential manuscripts into public AI platforms creates serious confidentiality risks.
* **Fraud at Scale:** AI facilitates the rise of "junk science" and paper mills, enabling the mass production of fraudulent research.

These integrity concerns are proposed to be addressed through several **structured frameworks and editorial strategies**:

* **Mandatory and Transparent Disclosure:** Authors must clearly declare the specific AI tools used, the version, the nature of the assistance, and exactly which sections were affected.
* **Tiered Models of Use:** Frameworks like the **GAIDeT taxonomy** help distinguish between "substantive" high-stakes use (requiring mandatory disclosure) and "routine" low-stakes use like grammar checking.
* **Rigorous Human Oversight:** Human authors must remain the "site of agency," meaning they are **fully responsible for verifying the accuracy** of every claim and reference generated by the AI.
* **Enhanced Editorial Diligence:** Journals are encouraged to provide comprehensive training for editors and peer reviewers to identify AI-generated anomalies and detect "junk" content.
* **Embracing Open Science:** Practices such as sharing raw data, code, and transparent peer review are viewed as essential safeguards for validating results and detecting fraudulent AI-generated papers.

## 5. Synthesis

*Q5: Across sources, what is the state of journal policy on AI — is there
emerging consensus, ongoing divergence, or evidence of convergence toward
particular models? What are the main unresolved tensions?*

The current state of journal policy on AI is characterized by a **solidifying consensus on core principles**, alongside **significant ongoing divergence** in practical implementation and **emerging convergence** toward structured, tiered governance models.

### 1. Emerging Consensus: Human Primacy

A powerful cross-disciplinary consensus has crystallized around three foundational pillars:

* **Human-Exclusive Authorship:** Almost all major publishers and editorial bodies (COPE, ICMJE, WAME) explicitly prohibit listing AI as an author. The rationale is that AI lacks the legal personality and moral agency required to take responsibility for research, manage copyright, or declare conflicts of interest.
* **Ultimate Human Accountability:** Authors bear full and non-transferable responsibility for the entire manuscript. This includes being liable for inaccuracies, ethical breaches, or "hallucinations" introduced by AI tools.
* **Mandatory Transparency:** There is broad agreement that AI involvement must be disclosed. Failure to do so is increasingly labeled as "soft plagiarism" or "algiarism".

### 2. Ongoing Divergence: Fragmentation and Disciplinary Variance

Despite shared principles, the operational landscape remains highly heterogeneous:

* **Restrictive vs. Permissive Stances:** Policies range from the **Science** family's strict requirement for editorial consent for any AI-generated text or images to more flexible approaches like **PLOS ONE**, which allows AI use provided the methodology and integrity checks are fully detailed.
* **Disciplinary Adoption Rates:** Adoption is uneven across fields. While approximately 80% of cardiology journals have established AI policies, less than half (44.7%) of applied linguistics journals had specific guidelines as of late 2024.
* **Internal Policy Conflicts:** Significant inconsistencies exist between large publishers and their affiliated journals. In some cases, journal guidelines directly contradict publisher mandates, placing a heavy navigation burden on authors.

### 3. Evidence of Convergence: The Shift to Tiered Governance

Sources indicate a move away from simple disclaimers toward **sophisticated, task-based taxonomies**:

* **The GAIDeT Model:** There is a growing trend toward using frameworks like the **Generative AI Delegation Taxonomy (GAIDeT)**, which categorizes AI use into levels such as "substantive" (requiring disclosure) and "routine" (e.g., grammar checking, which often does not).
* **Solution-Oriented Governance:** The scholarly discourse is shifting from "ethical panic" or problem-framing to explicitly normative, "solution-oriented" governance that seeks to integrate AI into workflows while preserving human oversight.

### 4. Main Unresolved Tensions

Several critical tensions continue to divide the scientific community:

* **The Threshold of "Too Much Help":** Some argue that the originator's identity (human or machine) is epistemically irrelevant if the content is valuable and peer-reviewed, while others believe AI use undermines the essential "intellectual struggle" of scholarship.
* **Credit vs. Accountability:** A minority perspective maintains that if AI makes a substantial contribution, denying it credit is a form of misattribution, even if it cannot take responsibility.
* **AI in Peer Review:** While some see AI as a way to enhance reviewer selection and efficiency, major journals like **JAMA** strictly prohibit inputting confidential manuscripts into public AI tools due to privacy and confidentiality risks.
* **Disclosure and Bias:** Mandatory disclosure may inadvertently introduce bias against non-native English speakers who use AI for linguistic polishing. This has led some researchers to shift from supporting mandatory to advocating for voluntary disclosure to protect the integrity of blind peer review.
* **Detection Unreliability:** The continued failure of AI detection tools to reliably distinguish human from machine text creates an enforceability crisis for restrictive policies.
