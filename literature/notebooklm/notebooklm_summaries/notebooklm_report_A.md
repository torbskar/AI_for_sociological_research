The overall stance toward AI use in research is predominantly **conditional**, characterized by a transition from initial "ethical panic" to a proactive, nuanced framework focused on responsible integration (Raitskaya & Tikhonova, 2025). While some authors maintain an encouraging "how-to" perspective for increasing productivity (Lin, 2023), others are deeply skeptical, describing the situation as a "paradox" where the effort required for ethical oversight negates the benefits of using AI for primary research tasks (Mezzadri, 2025; Lin, 2023).

**Reasons for Caution:** The primary reason for caution is the **unreliability of AI-generated content**, particularly the propensity for "hallucinations" where models fabricate facts, references, or data (Mezzadri, 2025; Gallifant et al., 2025; Sebastian et al., 2025; Sharma et al., 2025). This is exacerbated by the **opacity and "black-box" nature** of proprietary systems, making it difficult to audit decision processes or training data (Chakravorti et al., 2025; Raitskaya & Tikhonova, 2025; Leonelli, 2023). Furthermore, researchers warn of **deskilling and the erosion of critical thinking**, as over-reliance may lead to the "atrophy" of foundational scholarly skills (Mezzadri, 2025; Hamaniuk et al., 2025; Sebastian et al., 2025; Raitskaya & Tikhonova, 2025). Ethical concerns also include **algorithmic bias** (racial, gender, and Western-centric), **intellectual property/copyright issues**, and **environmental costs** (Barman et al., 2025; Raitskaya & Tikhonova, 2025; Chakravorti et al., 2025; Sebastian et al., 2025). There is a consensus that AI must remain **subordinate to human responsibility** (Raitskaya & Tikhonova, 2025; Chakravorti et al., 2025).

**Benefits and Tasks:**

* **Literature Reviews & Sourcing:** AI speeds up literature search, synthesis, and identifying thematic clusters or research gaps (Malik & Terzidis, 2025; Thurzo & Varga, 2025; Raitskaya & Tikhonova, 2025; Sharma et al., 2025).
* **Administrative & Secondary Tasks:** Significant benefits are identified for proofreading, grammar/style checking, reference formatting, and creating presentations (Mezzadri, 2025; Lin, 2023; Raitskaya & Tikhonova, 2025; Sharma et al., 2025).
* **Technical & Methodological:** LLMs facilitate coding, debugging, statistical analysis, and simulation-based research (Lin, 2023; Fung & Ng, 2025; Chakravorti et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2026).
* **Ideation:** Useful for brainstorming research questions, generating hypotheses, and conceptual mapping (Lin, 2023; Raitskaya & Tikhonova, 2025; Malik & Terzidis, 2025; Thurzo & Varga, 2025).
* **Accessibility:** AI helps level the "linguistic playing field" for non-native English speakers by enhancing readability and fluency (Lin, 2023; Raitskaya & Tikhonova, 2025).

**Author notes:** _There is a striking tension between the "efficiency promise" of AI and the "oversight reality." The most rigorous sources (Mezzadri, 2025; Malik & Terzidis, 2025) emphasize that AI doesn't actually save time if the user is acting as an ethical gatekeeper. _Q4: Do any sources specify conditions for acceptable AI use — disclosure, verification, prompt documentation, human oversight, task restrictions?_

Acceptable AI use is governed by a conditional framework that mandates transparency and maintains human primacy throughout the research process (Raitskaya & Tikhonova, 2025). Disclosure of AI involvement is required by all top academic publishers, with a consensus that authors must state which tools were used and specify the extent of their contribution (da Veiga, 2025). This disclosure should ideally be made in the methods section, acknowledgments, or a dedicated "Declaration of AI Use" (Raitskaya & Tikhonova, 2025; da Veiga, 2025). Some frameworks propose a tiered model where disclosure is mandatory only for "substantial" use—such as generating hypotheses or analyzing data—while remaining optional for routine tasks like minor editing (Resnik & Hosseini, 2025; Raitskaya & Tikhonova, 2025). Reports must provide specific metadata, including the tool name, version, and the date the content was generated (Gallifant et al., 2025; da Veiga, 2025).

Rigorous verification is a non-negotiable condition because human authors bear full and ultimate responsibility for the accuracy and integrity of the entire manuscript (Mezzadri, 2025; Sharma et al., 2025). Oversight cannot be a superficial screening; researchers are required to check AI-generated claims against original source materials to catch "hallucinations" and fabricated citations (Mezzadri, 2025; Sharma et al., 2025; Sebastian et al., 2025). Conversely, "primary" research tasks—such as drawing scientific conclusions, interpreting findings, or reasoning—must be performed solely by human authors (Mezzadri, 2025; da Veiga, 2025). A universal restriction across the sources is the prohibition of listing AI tools as authors or co-authors, as they lack the legal and moral capacity to be accountable for the work (da Veiga, 2025; Sharma et al., 2025; Hosseini & Horbach, 2023). Finally, data stewardship conditions prohibit inputting confidential manuscripts, sensitive patient information, or unpublished data into public AI systems to protect privacy and intellectual property (Hosseini & Horbach, 2023; da Veiga, 2025; Chakravorti et al., 2025).

The sources specify several conditions for acceptable generative AI (GenAI) use in research, emphasizing that while these tools can be "valuable allies," they must be governed by strict principles of **transparency, accountability, and human critical judgment** (Binz et al., 2025; Rodafinos, 2025).

### Disclosure and Transparency

* **Explicit Acknowledgment:** Researchers must acknowledge the reliance on AI so that readers are aware the text is partially machine-generated (Binz et al., 2025). This includes making explicit which specific models were used and describing how they were applied (Binz et al., 2025; Wachinger et al., 2025).
* **Prompt Documentation:** Ideally, authors should publicly release their **original prompts and the corresponding AI responses** as supplementary materials to maintain the spirit of open science (Binz et al., 2025).
* **Citing AI Content:** Users should properly cite any specific content or ideas generated by AI tools, just as they would with any other source (Rodafinos, 2025).

### Verification and Accuracy

* **Independent Fact-Checking:** Because AI models are "incidental truth tellers" and prone to "careless speech," authors have a non-delegable duty to verify all facts, references, and data (Wachter et al., 2024; Binz et al., 2025).
* **Academic Verification:** Content generated by AI must be cross-checked against **reliable academic sources** to ensure accuracy, as models often hallucinate credible-sounding but fake references (Rodafinos, 2025; Wachinger et al., 2025).
* **Benchmarking and Temperature Settings:** For high-stakes tasks, it is recommended to use **conservative temperature settings** (e.g., 0.0) to increase consistency and reduce the risk of overgeneralization bias (Peters & Chin-Yee, 2025).

### Human Oversight and Accountability

* **Humans "In the Driver's Seat":** AI is viewed as an "augmentative tool" or "research assistant," not a standalone analyst or replacement for human critical thinking (Bail, 2024; Rodafinos, 2025; Wachinger et al., 2025). Humans must retain responsibility for determining the **scientific roadmap** and normative goals of research (Binz et al., 2025).
* **No Co-authorship:** The general consensus is that LLMs are **not eligible for co-authorship** because they lack personhood and cannot be held legally or ethically accountable for scientific precision (Binz et al., 2025; Wachter et al., 2024).
* **Reflexivity:** Researchers must provide the **contextual information** (such as non-verbal cues or situated identities) that AI models are incapable of reflecting upon (Wachinger et al., 2025).

### Task Restrictions

* **Data Privacy and Confidentiality:** Sensitive research data or **confidential manuscripts** (such as those under peer review) should not be entered into proprietary LLMs, as this poses significant privacy and legal risks (Binz et al., 2025; Wachinger et al., 2025).
* **Prohibition on Data Fabrication:** Using LLMs as "stand-ins" for human subjects in behavioral research is often viewed as **contrary to scientific norms** and a form of data fabrication (Binz et al., 2025).
* **Restricted Academic Dishonesty:** AI should not be used to write entire papers or reports; doing so is classified as **academic dishonesty** (Rodafinos, 2025).

## Manually added sources:

The sources distinguish between **ad hoc AI use and systematic, workflow-based approaches**, while proposing specific methodologies, prompt templates, and worked examples to guide researchers in the effective application of these tools.

### Systematic and Workflow-Based Approaches

The scientific community is shifting from casual experimentation to incorporating large language models (LLMs) into **structured scientific workflows** (Binz et al., 2025).

* **Iterative Human-in-the-Loop Workflows:** Rather than accepting single outputs, researchers recommend an **iterative process** where the user constantly checks for facts, revisions, and new connections, treating the AI as a collaborator rather than a simple software tool (Binz et al., 2025; Wachinger et al., 2025).
* **The "Two-Lane" Assessment Approach:** To ensure educational integrity, the University of Sydney utilizes a **two-lane approach**: Lane 1 restricts AI use to ensure core knowledge acquisition through traditional exams, while Lane 2 embraces AI as a **collaborative learning tool** for brainstorming and data analysis (Rodafinos, 2025).
* **API and Coding Workflows:** For large-scale tasks like content analysis, experts recommend setting up **automated coding workflows using APIs**, which allow for more precise control over model parameters and the systematic processing of entire corpora (Bail, 2024).
* **Contextual Isolation:** In qualitative research, it is recommended to use **separate conversations** for independent analyses to prevent the LLM's "learning" from previous prompts from biasing subsequent results (Wachinger et al., 2025).

### Named Methods and Frameworks

Several sources propose specific, named procedures for using or evaluating AI in research contexts:

* **Three-Step Benchmarking Framework for Generalization Accuracy:** This method systematically measures how much an AI exaggerates scientific claims (Peters & Chin-Yee, 2025). The steps include:
  1. **Prompting:** Instructing an LLM to summarize a scientific text (Peters & Chin-Yee, 2025).
  2. **Classification:** Coding both the original text and the AI summary for three features: **generic generalizations** (present tense without quantifiers), **present tense generalizations** (extending past findings to the present), and **action-guiding generalizations** (turning descriptions into recommendations) (Peters & Chin-Yee, 2025).
  3. **Comparison:** Calculating an "overgeneralization score" by detecting instances where the AI broadened the original scope (Peters & Chin-Yee, 2025).
* **Transmission Chain Methodology:** Adapted from psychological "telephone game" experiments, this method involves presenting an LLM with its own previous output iteratively to track how **content biases** (such as gender stereotypes or negativity) are magnified over time (Acerbi & Stubbersfield, 2023).
* **Silicon Sampling:** This framework uses LLMs to create "silicon samples" by prompting the model with specific demographic traits to **impersonate human respondents** (Bail, 2024). This is used for pretesting survey instruments or imputing missing data in real human samples (Bail, 2024).
* **Five-Step Framework Approach for Qualitative Data:** Originally a human methodology, this has been adapted for AI-assisted thematic analysis (Wachinger et al., 2025). The steps performed by the AI include:
  1. **Getting Started:** Defining the transcript context (Wachinger et al., 2025).
  2. **Familiarization:** Summarizing major interview themes (Wachinger et al., 2025).
  3. **Identifying Framework:** Proposing a thematic structure (Wachinger et al., 2025).
  4. **Indexing:** Linking specific segments of text to themes (Wachinger et al., 2025).
  5. **Mapping and Interpretation:** Synthesizing the patterns to draw conclusions (Wachinger et al., 2025).

### Core Principles of Responsible AI Use

The sources converge on several foundational principles for the ethical integration of AI into research:

* **Transparency and Disclosure:** Researchers must explicitly acknowledge the use of AI tools, identifying the specific models and describing how they were applied (Binz et al., 2025). This extends to the "spirit of open science," where authors are encouraged to publicly release their prompts and the corresponding AI responses as supplementary materials (Binz et al., 2025; Wachinger et al., 2025).
* **Human Accountability:** Large language models (LLMs) lack "communicative intent" and legal personhood, meaning they cannot be held accountable for errors or fabrications (Binz et al., 2025; Wachter et al., 2024). Consequently, the ultimate responsibility for the accuracy, validity, and ethical conduct of research rests solely with the human author (Binz et al., 2025; Rodafinos, 2025).
* **Mandatory Verification:** Because models are "incidental truth tellers" prone to **"careless speech"** (Wachter et al., 2024) and **"generalization bias"** (Peters & Chin-Yee, 2025), all AI-generated content—especially citations and data interpretations—must be independently verified against original academic sources (Rodafinos, 2025; Wachinger et al., 2025).
* **AI Literacy:** Universities and research institutions must prioritize AI literacy, training students and faculty to critically evaluate model outputs, understand model limitations, and navigate the "double-edged sword" of efficiency versus academic integrity (Rodafinos, 2025; Wachinger et al., 2025).
* **Fairness and Bias Mitigation:** Responsible use requires an awareness that LLMs reflect and magnify human-like content biases, such as gender stereotypes and negativity (Acerbi & Stubbersfield, 2023). Researchers must take active steps to ensure AI use does not marginalize non-Western (non-WEIRD) perspectives or reinforce systemic inequities in research (Binz et al., 2025; Bail, 2024).

### Areas of Disagreement

Despite these shared principles, significant debate exists regarding the long-term role of AI in the scientific enterprise:

* **Human Collaborator vs. Software Tool:** One faction argues that LLMs function like "knowledgeable research assistants" or "collaborators" whose work should be iterated upon just like that of a human colleague (Schulz et al. in Binz et al., 2025). Others vehemently disagree, asserting that LLMs are merely "synthetic text-extruding machines" that should be treated as tools, not fit participants in the "social process" of science (Bender et al. in Binz et al., 2025).
* **Universal vs. Bespoke Models:** Some researchers believe that large, universal LLMs are adopted because they eliminate the need to learn specialized software (Binz et al., 2025). Conversely, others advocate for a shift toward "bespoke and often lightweight models" designed for specific, interpretable tasks to avoid the opacity and brittleness of general-purpose LLMs (Bender et al. in Binz et al., 2025; Binz et al., 2025).
* **The Scope of Delegation:** There is a conflict over whether certain aspects of science should be permanently reserved for humans. Some argue that "normative" (deciding what problems to solve) and "epistemic" (human understanding) aspects must stay human (Botvinick & Gershman in Binz et al., 2025). Others contend that if an AI can effectively identify fruitful research topics, scientists should "take any help we can get" (Schulz et al. in Binz et al., 2025).
* **Infrastructure and Control:** While some rely on proprietary models, others argue that scientific integrity requires the development of **open-source infrastructure** (Bail, 2024). Open-source models are seen as essential for ensuring reproducibility and preventing the "academic caste system" from being reinforced by corporate paywalls (Bail, 2024; Binz et al., 2025).
* **Legal Duty to Truth:** There is debate over whether LLM providers should have a **legal duty to tell the truth** (Wachter et al., 2024). Proponents argue this is necessary to protect "shared social truth," while others suggest that established scientific norms and existing liability frameworks may already address the most egregious cases of data fabrication (Binz et al., 2025; Wachter et al., 2024).

## 3. Structured and systematic use — including practical methods

Sources in this notebook clearly distinguish between ad hoc AI use and systematic, structured approaches, identifying a major shift in research practice from initial experimentation toward formalized, normative frameworks (Raitskaya & Tikhonova, 2025; Malik & Terzidis, 2025).

### Q5a: Structured vs. Ad Hoc AI Use

Sources characterize ad hoc AI use as often being driven by curiosity but resulting in dissatisfaction or "fragmented adhocracy," whereas systematic integration is necessary for scientific rigor (Sebastian et al., 2025; Chakravorti et al., 2025). Hamaniuk et al. (2025) distinguish three distinct engagement patterns: **"recursive collaboration"** (multiple cycles of human-AI interaction yielding the best outcomes), **"task-specific supplementation"** (one-off queries for specific needs), and **"hybrid orchestration"** (varying the approach based on the complexity of the task). Malik and Terzidis (2025) argue that while AI automates labor-intensive tasks, it must be integrated through a hybrid framework that maintains **"reflective agency,"** ensuring AI remains a collaborative partner subordinate to human expertise. Recommendations for use emphasize **"human-in-the-loop"** validation, where researchers treat AI-generated responses as samples of data to be critically evaluated rather than authoritative truths (Chakravorti et al., 2025; Grossmann et al., 2023; Sharma et al., 2025).

### Q5b: Named Methods and Frameworks

Several named frameworks are proposed to formalize AI integration:

* **GAIDeT (Generative AI Delegation Taxonomy):** This classification system allows researchers to delegate macro-level (e.g., literature search) and micro-level tasks (e.g., editing, code generation) to generative AI while maintaining a structured audit trail and preserving human accountability (Suchikova et al., 2025; Raitskaya & Tikhonova, 2025).
* **The Hybrid SLR Framework:** Designed for systematic literature reviews, this method involves three phases—design, study collection, and interpretation—and five specific stages: (1) AI model selection, (2) knowledge base integration, (3) iterative prompt engineering, (4) cyclical validation (inter-model and intra-model), and (5) employment to the research question (Malik & Terzidis, 2025).

## 4. Tool types and disciplinary variation

*Q6–7: Do sources differentiate tool types — conversational, specialised, agentic? Variation across disciplines? What is said about social science or sociology specifically?*

Sources in this notebook differentiate AI tools into several distinct categories, ranging from general-purpose conversational models to specialized agents designed for "deep research" tasks.

### Differentiation of Tool Types

* **Conversational and Generalist LLMs:** Most sources identify Large Language Models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT, Gemini (formerly Bard), Claude, and Bing AI as the primary tools currently used in research. These are often used for text generation, translation, and summarization.
* **Specialized Models:** Sources highlight the existence of domain-specific models like **PubMedBERT** or **ClinicalBERT**, which demonstrate superior performance in niche areas such as medical literature reviews compared to generalist models. Other specialized tools include "Smart Citation" indices like Scite.ai and de-identification pipelines for sharing qualitative data.
* **Agentic "Deep Research" Tools:** Newer "deep research" systems—such as OpenAI’s o3-mini-high, Google Gemini Pro with deep research, and Grok 3 DeepSearch—are distinguished by their ability to autonomously analyze vast datasets, cross-check findings across multiple authoritative sources, and suggest novel hypotheses. These tools are described as active collaborators in knowledge creation rather than mere assistive assistants.
* **Taxonomies of Delegation:** To manage these various tools, sources propose frameworks like the **Generative AI Delegation Taxonomy (GAIDeT)**, which classifies tasks into macro and micro levels to help researchers decide which functions to delegate while maintaining accountability.

### Variation Across Disciplines

Adoption rates and usage patterns vary significantly by field, driven by different epistemological traditions and professional requirements.

* **STEM Fields:** These disciplines show the highest adoption rates (approx. 78%). Students in engineering and computer science value AI's ability to simplify technical jargon and generate functional simulation code rapidly. However, concerns about "hallucinations" in technical claims are high, leading to the development of specific verification protocols.
* **Humanities:** Adoption is more selective (approx. 62%). Researchers in these fields often worry that AI homogenizes authorial voice and flattens the interpretive nuance or "productive ambiguity" essential to humanities scholarship.
* **Architecture:** In this domain, researchers are exploring whether machines can learn "tacit architectural knowledge" and mimic problem-solving skills once attributed solely to human experience.

### Social Science and Sociology Specifically

Social science researchers occupy a "middle ground" in AI adoption (approx. 71%), but their engagement is marked by specific tensions regarding methodology and trust.

* **Trust in ML vs. GenAI:** Social scientists report significantly higher acceptance and trust in traditional **Machine Learning (ML)** compared to generative AI. ML is viewed as statistically grounded and more transparent, whereas genAI is often seen as an unreliable "black box" built on non-transparent training data.
* **Applications:** In social science, LLMs are used for simulating human behavior, testing hypotheses at scale, prototyping new theories, and automating first-pass annotation of qualitative data. However, researchers emphasize that core intellectual tasks—such as theory development, nuanced interpretation, and normative judgment—remain irreplaceable human functions.
* **Sociology and Preregistration:** In sociology specifically, the adoption of **preregistration** and **registered reports** is highlighted as a growing trend to improve research quality and curb questionable practices like p-hacking or motivated reasoning. This is noted as particularly useful for sociologists because it helps formalize the role of **positionality**—how a researcher's characteristics affect data analysis—by requiring explicit defense of design decisions before research begins.
* **Linguistic Diversity and Bias:** Social scientists raise concerns that because LLMs are trained predominantly on Western, English-speaking data (WEIRD populations), they may perpetuate cultural hegemony and marginalize non-Western perspectives in global knowledge production.

_Q8: Emerging consensus on responsible AI use? Core principles across sources? Where do sources disagree?_

### Emerging Consensus

The scholarly community is transitioning from initial "ethical panic" to a proactive, normative framework for responsible AI integration. A clear consensus has emerged that AI should be treated as an **assistive tool rather than an agent**, and it must remain subordinate to human judgment and responsibility. This evolution is marked by a shift from problem-framing to solution-oriented governance that emphasizes auditable accountability.

### Core Principles

Across the sources, five core principles define responsible AI research practice:

1. **Human Primacy and Accountability:** Human authors bear full and ultimate responsibility for the entire manuscript, including any AI-generated content. AI lacks the legal identity or moral standing to assume accountability.
2. **Transparent Disclosure:** Disclosure is now viewed as a "cornerstone" of research integrity. Authors must explicitly state the tool name, version, and the nature and extent of AI's contribution.
3. **Rigorous Human Oversight:** Oversight must involve a critical, thorough assessment rather than a "superficial screening". This is essential to mitigate factual "hallucinations," biased outputs, and fabricated citations.
4. **Task-Based Categorization:** A "tiered model" is increasingly accepted, distinguishing between **secondary/supportive tasks** (e.g., grammar, formatting, and literature search), which are ethically sound, and **primary/substantive tasks** (e.g., drawing scientific conclusions and generating original ideas), which human authors must perform personally.
5. **Data Stewardship and Privacy:** Responsible use requires protecting confidential data, such as unpublished manuscripts or sensitive patient information, by not feeding them into public, cloud-based AI tools.

### Areas of Disagreement and Tension

Despite broad consensus on principles, significant tensions remain regarding implementation:

* **Authorship and Credit:** While major editorial bodies prohibit AI authorship, some researchers argue that if an LLM contributes at a "human-like level," it deserves formal credit to prevent human authors from taking "unearned" credit for AI-generated intellectual work.
* **Mandatory vs. Voluntary Disclosure:** Some sources advocate for mandatory disclosure at submission, while others propose a **voluntary checkbox system** to prevent bias against non-native English speakers who use AI for language policing, suggesting that disclosure should only be visible after publication.
* **The "Efficiency Paradox":** There is disagreement over AI's actual utility. Some argue AI accelerates discovery, but others claim that if AI is used ethically (requiring independent verification of all outputs), the human effort involved negates any net benefits in efficiency or time-saving.
* **Reproducibility Standards:** The "non-deterministic" and rapidly evolving nature of AI makes its outputs difficult to reproduce. Some argue for standardizing model versions and random seeds, while others suggest that rapid system evolution makes reproducible results a "fragile" basis for trust.

**Author notes:** _The community appears to be consolidating around "process transparency" (how the tool was used) rather than "content detection" (trying to prove text is AI-written), largely because detection tools are increasingly viewed as unreliable._
