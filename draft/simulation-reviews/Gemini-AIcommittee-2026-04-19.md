As the Malthe-Sørensen committee, we have assessed this draft through the lens of our 2025 mandate. While our primary focus remains the integrity of education, we recognize that the "cognitive friction" required for a student to learn is identical to the friction required for a researcher to produce original knowledge.

The paper offers a compelling path toward "appropriate use" by shifting the focus from the *fact* of AI assistance to the *architecture* of the research process.

## ---

**1\. Mapping Systematic Use to "Appropriate Use"**

The paper’s distinction between **systematic** and **unsystematic** use maps directly onto our committee’s preference for "appropriate ways" over blanket bans.

* **The Analogy Holds:** In both research and teaching, "unsystematic use" (blind prompting) leads to what we call **cognitive offloading**—delegating the thinking to the machine and accepting the output without friction. "Systematic use" mirrors our recommendation for "process-oriented assessment," where the journey (the log, the prompt, the revision) is as important as the destination.  
* **The Breakdown:** In teaching, the goal is the *internal development* of the student. In research, the goal is the *external validity* of the finding. A student might use a systematic AI workflow and still fail to "learn" the material if the AI does the heavy lifting of synthesis, even if the process is documented.  
* **The Certification Analogue:** The "certification problem" in education (how do we know the student is competent?) has a perfect analogue in the **Accountability Threshold** of research. The paper argues that systematic use provides a "paper trail" for authorship. This addresses the certification problem by providing evidence that the researcher—not the model—is the intellectual architect.

## **2\. The Missing Link: The "Struggle" and Deep Learning**

The concern from our report that the paper fails to fully address is **the risk of superficiality and the loss of "writing-as-thinking."**

Our committee is deeply concerned that the mechanical struggle of drafting—the "grind" of finding the right word or connecting two difficult ideas—is exactly where deep cognitive processing happens. The paper argues that this is "mechanical task" work that can be offloaded. We are not convinced. By delegating the "text generation" to a skill-configured profile, the researcher may miss the **serendipitous insights** that only occur during the manual struggle of composition. To address this seriously, the paper would need to theorize how **deep conceptual immersion** is maintained when the researcher moves from "writer" to "editor-in-chief."

## **3\. Concentration of Effort vs. Intellectual Shallowness**

The paper’s framing of **Query Authorship** is a clever response to our concerns about intellectual shallowness, but it is only partially persuasive.

* **The Strength:** It correctly identifies that high-level design (the "upstream" work) is where the human contribution lies. Configuring an "adversarial reviewer" is a sophisticated way to force the researcher to defend their work.  
* **The Weakness:** It does not fully mitigate the risk of **automated sycophancy**. Even with "human verification checkpoints," humans are notoriously prone to **automation bias**—the tendency to trust an automated suggestion more than our own judgment when tired or under pressure. A researcher might "verify" an AI-generated synthesis and believe they are exercising judgment, while actually just skimming for "plausibility" rather than "truth." The paper needs more on how to ensure the "verification" remains a high-friction activity.

## **4\. Towards Active Endorsement**

To move from cautious openness to active endorsement, our committee would need to see:

* **Empirical Proof of "Cognitive Depth":** Evidence (perhaps from the cited Zeng et al. or future studies) that researchers using systematic AI workflows can defend their work in a "viva voce" or oral defense setting just as well as—or better than—those who used traditional methods.  
* **Standardization of "Author-Input" Files:** We want the "daily session records" described in §4.6 to become a mandatory part of the "Replication Package" in sociology. If this becomes a disciplinary norm, it protects the integrity of the "certificate" (the published paper).  
* **The "Human-in-the-Loop" Stress Test:** A clearer protocol for what "human verification" actually looks like. It cannot be a checkbox; it must be a documented "interrogation" of the AI’s output.
