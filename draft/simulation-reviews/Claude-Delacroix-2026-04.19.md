# Review by Professor Martin Delacroix
*Theoretical Sociologist — Response to "Structured AI Use in Sociological Research" (v1 draft, 2026-04-11)*

---

## 1. The two arguments most in need of sharpening

**First: the query authorship concept is asserted rather than argued.** The paper's central theoretical move — that intellectual contribution "lies in the query" rather than in the generated text — is treated as though it follows naturally from the observation that someone has to write the prompt. It does not. Authorship in the sociological tradition is bound up with questions of judgment, reflexivity, and the recursive relationship between the knower and the known. The researcher who writes an inclusion criterion is not doing the same epistemic work as the researcher who constructs an argument through the process of writing it. The criterion specifies a category; the argument constitutes one. The paper conflates these. What it needs — and conspicuously lacks — is an engagement with the philosophy of authorship and the sociology of knowledge production that would let it claim, rather than merely assert, that front-loading intellectual commitment into a configuration is equivalent to or a recognisable form of the intellectual contribution we have historically credited in academic work. Without this, query authorship is a rhetorical repackaging, not a theoretical advance.

**Second: the pre-registration analogy carries more weight than it can bear.** The paper acknowledges the disanalogy — a pre-registered commitment is public and timestamped; a skill file on a private machine is neither — and then proceeds to treat this as "real but not fatal." That is too quick. Pre-registration works not only because it forces explicit commitment in advance, but because it creates an independent, verifiable, externally held record that prevents post-hoc rationalisation. The "working logs" the paper proposes as functional equivalents are researcher-generated, researcher-held, and retrospectively completable. The paper's own §5.1 admits that "in principle, the working logs... address the cherry-picking risk." *In principle* is doing enormous work there. The analogy would be significantly more credible if the author confronted directly what it would take to make the private log function as a genuine constraint rather than a narrative resource — and whether that is achievable without the publicity condition that makes pre-registration epistemically robust in the first place.

## 2. The one caveat that would most reduce resistance

The paper makes an implicit normative assumption that needs to be stated and defended rather than assumed: that the *externalisation* of tacit knowledge is epistemically desirable in sociology, and that forcing tacit commitments into explicit criteria does not systematically distort the inquiry. This is not obvious. There is a serious argument — from Polanyi, from Kuhn, from Dreyfus, and one could add Bourdieu on the logic of practice — that the tacit, embodied, pre-reflective dimensions of expert judgment are not merely informal residue of knowledge that should be made explicit, but constitutive of it. When you force a literature reviewer to write down what "relevant" means before running the search, you may be preventing the kind of situated, iterative, theory-laden judgment that actually distinguishes good sociological reading from mechanical sorting. The paper treats this as a problem only of quality — explicit criteria might be *wrong* — but not as a problem of *kind*: that what systematic AI authorship produces is structurally different from what it purports to replace. A caveat acknowledging that this objection has genuine force, and specifying the domain conditions under which externalisation is and is not appropriate, would make the argument significantly more honest.

---

## 3. The one argument I find genuinely interesting

The observation in §6.2 that current binary policy inadvertently *penalises* structured use — treating a documented, reproducible AI workflow identically to careless chatbot prompting — is the sharpest point in the paper and the one that is genuinely difficult to dismiss. The institutional incentive structure this creates is perverse: it rewards opacity by making transparent practice indistinguishable from opaque practice under existing compliance frameworks. I find this interesting not because it vindicates the paper's broader programme, but because it identifies a real contradiction in how the field has responded to AI — one that cannot be addressed by stricter prohibition and that disclosure requirements alone do not resolve. Whether the documentation framework the author proposes is the right solution is a different question. But the diagnosis of the problem is accurate, and journals that have rushed to require disclosure without distinguishing *what* was disclosed have, perhaps inadvertently, created exactly the situation described.

---

## 4. Whether the scope limitation is adequate

It is not adequate, and the problem is structural rather than rhetorical. The paper's scope limitation — that it addresses one workflow for one type of research and makes no claim about interpretive or theoretical work — is stated clearly enough. But the concepts it develops do not stay within the scope it declares. *Query authorship* is introduced as a general epistemological claim about where intellectual contribution resides in AI-assisted research. The *pre-registration analogy* is deployed as a general epistemological argument for the value of externalising tacit commitments. These claims do not carry a scope restriction. If they are valid, they apply broadly; if the scope restriction is genuine, the paper needs to explain why query authorship is appropriate for literature screening but not for theoretical synthesis — why forcing commitment in advance is epistemically productive for one kind of work but inapplicable to another.

The deeper problem is this: the paper's scope limitation is a rhetorical move designed to pre-empt the objections of theoretically-oriented readers, but the arguments developed in the body of the paper implicitly reproduce the very model of research the limitation claims to exclude. The workflow described treats research as a pipeline — inputs, processing stages, outputs, verification checkpoints. This is not a neutral description of one possible research design; it is a specific and contestable model that inherits the logic of systematic review and computational reproducibility. The scope limitation says "this is not the only way"; the body of the paper says "this is what good epistemics looks like." That tension is not resolved by a disclaimer in the introduction. To address my concerns adequately, the paper would need to say something substantive about *why* the pipeline model is appropriate for the tasks it covers — and, more importantly, to engage with the possibility that the distinction between "text-heavy literature-based work" and "interpretive or theoretical work" is not a clean boundary but a continuum, and that the criteria for deciding where on that continuum systematic AI authorship becomes epistemically problematic are themselves underdeveloped.
