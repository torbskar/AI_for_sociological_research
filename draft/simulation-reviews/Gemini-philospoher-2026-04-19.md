As a philosopher of the extended mind and embodied cognition, I approach your paper with a mixture of professional respect for its rigor and a deep-seated ontological concern for its conclusions. You have performed a valuable service by moving the conversation from the shallow waters of "disclosure" to the deeper epistemic grounds of "systematicity." Your concept of "query authorship" is a sophisticated attempt to reclaim the human center in a mechanized process.

However, from the perspective that writing is not merely the *report* of thought but the *act* of thinking itself, there are significant gaps in your framework. Here is my assessment:

### **1\. The Point of Underestimation: The Recursive Nature of Drafting**

The point where you most seriously underestimate the cognitive loss is in **Section 4.4 (Drafting)**. You argue that "by the time the drafting stage begins, substantial work has already been done" and that the intellectual work lies in the configuration rather than the text.

This assumes a linear, "Cartesian" model of research: first we think, then we write. In my tradition, writing is a recursive process of discovery. The "productive struggle" of finding the right word or wrestling with a recalcitrant sentence often reveals that the "substantial work" done in the outline was actually flawed. By offloading the initial drafting to a "skill-configured style profile," the researcher skips the very stage where the most subtle contradictions in their logic usually surface. You treat drafting as **execution**; I contend it is **investigation**.

### **2\. The Necessary Caveat: The Atrophy of Tacit Competence**

To honestly acknowledge the cognitive cost, you should add a caveat regarding **the erosion of internalized standards**.

While you argue that "externalizing" tacit commitments makes them "visible and challengeable", you must acknowledge that **externalization often leads to the atrophy of the internal.** When a researcher relies on a "reviewer skill" to check for internal consistency or framing errors, they are no longer training their own "cognitive musculature" to detect those errors. The cost of "systematic AI authorship" may be the long-term degradation of the researcher's ability to think independently of the system—a state of "cognitive parasitism" where the researcher can manage the tool but can no longer inhabit the argument.

### **3\. Relocation vs. Elimination of Effort**

Your framing of "relocating" effort to formulation and verification is a clever defense, but it does not fully resolve the concern.

* **The Problem of the "Checker" Mindset:** Verification is a reactive, evaluative mode of consciousness. Drafting is a generative, synthetic mode.  
* **The Synthesis Gap:** A researcher who "verifies" an AI-generated synthesis against sources is looking for *accuracy*. A researcher who synthesizes manually is looking for *meaning*. The former is a clerical check; the latter is a phenomenological merging with the material.

Your workflow ensures the output is **accountable**, but it does not guarantee it is **profound**. You have made the process "epistemically honest", but you haven't yet shown how it prevents the research from becoming "technically correct but intellectually hollow."

### **4\. Conditions for Optimism and the Path Forward**

I would be more positive about AI-assisted research writing if the tools were used to **increase resistance** rather than decrease it. Cognitive growth requires an "Other" that pushes back.

* **The Adversarial Success:** Your "Adversarial Configuration" (§4.5) is the most promising element of your paper. By instructing the AI to raise objections and "play devil's advocate," you move away from the "AI as servant" model toward an "AI as dialogue partner" model. This forces the researcher back into the struggle of thinking.  
* **Verdict:** Your structured approach moves decisively in the right direction. By treating the AI as a "system to be managed" and documenting the "author-input files", you provide a trail that allows others to see if a human mind was actually present. You have successfully moved AI use from "bullshit" (in Frankfurt's sense) to "accountable assertion."

Does the shift from "doing the work" to "authoring the query" fundamentally change the *kind* of knowledge sociology produces, or is it merely a change in the *labor* of production?
