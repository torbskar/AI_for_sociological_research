1. Arguments that most need sharpening or qualification  

   **(a) “Systematic AI authorship as open science practice”**

   As it stands, the paper slides from “better documented” to “epistemically better” with insufficient argument, and with a very particular, largely positivist, conception of epistemic quality in the background.

   You insist that systematic AI use “instantiates the epistemic properties of open-science practice” and that the core consequence is that “systematic use makes errors visible and correctable; unsystematic use does not.” <ref:index={3493614} firstWord={1} lastWord={60}/> You connect this directly to the pre‑registration analogy and to Freese & Peterson’s concern with missing decision documentation. <ref:index={3493606} firstWord={1} lastWord={80}/>  

   Problems from the perspective of a theoretically oriented sociologist:

- You treat “open science” as if its epistemic virtues were settled and uncontroversial, ignoring the substantive debates about whether pre‑registration and replication practices import a particular experimentalist, hypothesis-testing model of inquiry into domains where questions are emergent, reflexive, and interpretively constituted.  

- You equate “epistemically better” with “more auditable ex post through artefacts that resemble code-and-data packages.” That is an institutional definition of epistemic value (aligned with journals’ current replication fetish), not a theoretical one. It privileges forms of work that are easily decomposable into steps and artefacts, and it marginalises forms of inquiry whose central value lies precisely in the open-ended, path-dependent evolution of the object and the question.  

- You write that systematic AI authorship is a “necessary condition for the kind of quality control that makes errors detectable” <ref:index={3493601} firstWord={45} lastWord={95}/> but you never specify what counts as “error” in interpretive or theoretical work, or how a workflow designed for screening rubrics, boolean strings, and version-controlled scripts would relate to hermeneutic validity, reflexive adequacy, or socio-historical situatedness.
  To be credible in theory-oriented sociology, you need to:  

- Explicitly acknowledge that the “epistemic properties” you valorise are those of a particular *regime* of knowledge production.  

- Clarify that many central objects of sociological inquiry (symbolic forms, fields, habitus, meaning-making, etc.) are not naturally rendered into the kinds of discrete, checkable “decisions” your workflow assumes.  

- Qualify all claims that systematic AI use is “necessary” for epistemic soundness to the specific subfield and style of research where “error” and “quality” are operationalised in ways compatible with your apparatus.
  **(b) “Query authorship” as the locus of intellectual contribution**
  The idea that “the intellectual work is in the query” is interesting but currently overstated and under-theorised. You say:

- “The intellectual work is to a large extent in the query, and the query is authored by the researcher.” <ref:index={3493628} firstWord={40} lastWord={115}/>  

- You then map “query authorship” directly onto ICMJE criterion 1 (“conception or design of the work”) and present systematic AI use as what *demonstrates* that the criterion is met. <ref:index={3493597} firstWord={40} lastWord={110}/>  
  From my perspective:

- You reduce “conception or design of the work” to ex ante *specification* of criteria—search strings, screening rules, reviewer configurations. This is a very thin view of theoretical work, which in practice unfolds through misrecognitions, retroactive reframings, and encounters with recalcitrant material that *change* what the object is.  

- You treat the query as a stable encoding of “the researcher’s theoretical commitments,” but the most interesting theoretical work involves the destabilisation and transformation of those commitments. Your workflow (and your examples) assume that it is obvious what the relevant population of texts is, what counts as inclusion/exclusion, and that these can be frozen into a rubric without loss. That may be adequate for narrow, instrumental questions; it is much less plausible for theoretical work that interrogates categories, problematises boundaries, and works against the grain of established search taxonomies.  

- You do not consider how the infrastructure of your workflow (APIs, databases, “semantic” search tools) already encodes a set of categories, languages and institutional boundaries that structure what can appear as a plausible query. There is no sociology of the tools here: no sense of how power, language, and field structure shape the space of possible “queries.”
  To make this credible, you would need to:  

- Present “query authorship” as one *partial* locus of intellectual contribution, mainly for relatively well-structured empirical or policy questions, not as a general model of what it means to theorise.  

- Engage with the way the very possibility of writing a boolean string or a screening rubric presupposes a prior theoretical settlement of categories—exactly what much critical sociology wants to resist.  

- Acknowledge that the most consequential theoretical moves often happen *after* engagement with materials, not before; your framework at present treats those as post‑hoc “adjustments” to a spec rather than as constitutive of the inquiry.
2. The one caveat that would most reduce my resistance  
   You need a strong, explicit caveat on *epistemic pluralism* and *field effects*. At the moment you occasionally say that you are focusing on “text-heavy and empirical social science research” and “not the specifics of empirical analysis as such” <ref:index={3493618} firstWord={1} lastWord={60}/> and that this is “one way of using AI, not the only way.” But the internal logic of the paper treats your way as the epistemically *proper* way wherever AI is used.
   The caveat that would matter is something like:
   
   - An explicit acknowledgment that your framework is tailored to a particular *style* of sociological work: relatively codifiable empirical projects oriented to producing stable claims that can be audited by third parties.  
   - A recognition that there are other legitimate epistemic cultures in sociology—interpretive, ethnographic, historical, critical theory—in which:  
     - questions evolve during fieldwork or textual engagement;  
     - “replication packages” are a poor proxy for quality;  
     - fundamental categories are themselves the object of critique rather than fixed inputs.  
   - A clear statement that attempting to impose your documentation-heavy, pipeline-style workflow as *the* standard for sociological quality would amount to extending the replication/open‑science agenda into domains where it would be distortive and disciplinary in the Bourdieusian sense.
   
   In short: concede in print that your proposal is normatively appropriate only for certain segments of the field, and that generalising it institutionally would have consequences for the distribution of legitimacy between different forms of sociological practice.

3. One argument I find genuinely interesting, and why  
   The most genuinely interesting move is your analysis of how systematic AI use can expose the “garden of forking paths” created by unsystematic prompting, and how documenting search strings, screening rubrics, and review configurations turns the AI from a “truth-indifferent generator” into something that can be held to account. <ref:index={3493627} firstWord={80} lastWord={165}/>  
   Specifically, the tie between:
   
   - Frankfurt’s notion of bullshit as “indifferent to truth,”  
   - the directional failure modes of LLMs (sycophancy, generalisation bias), and  
   - the way a configured, adversarial reviewer skill can structurally resist confirmation bias <ref:index={3493615} firstWord={60} lastWord={145}/>  
   
   is conceptually sharp. It shows that the problem is not “AI versus humans,” but the *form of coupling* between human judgement and a generative system that does not itself track truth. The idea that explicit configuration of the AI’s evaluative stance (e.g. adversarial review rather than sycophantic assistance) is a new *site of methodological choice* is a genuinely useful insight for sociologists who are already using these tools.
   I still think you over-claim when you treat this as analogous to pre‑registration, but the framing of AI as a site where we can encode and contest our own standards of critique—rather than as a neutral assistant—is analytically productive.

4. Does the explicit scope limitation adequately address my concerns?  
   No. The existing scope limitation is not sufficient, because the paper is not just describing a workflow; it is advancing a *normative epistemology* that, if taken seriously by journals and reviewers, will have field‑level consequences.
   You say that you focus on “sociology and adjacent social sciences where research questions and theoretical framings cannot be delegated to automated agents” and that your workflow is “one way of using AI, not the only way” <ref:index={3493618} firstWord={60} lastWord={135}/>; you also insist that you make “no claim about interpretive or theoretical work.” But:  
   
   - The argument about “systematic versus unsystematic” use as the *epistemically relevant axis* is not written as domain‑limited. You repeatedly state that unsystematic use is epistemically defective *as such*, and that systematic use is a “necessary condition” for epistemic soundness. <ref:index={3493607} firstWord={120} lastWord={200}/> Any editor or reviewer reading this will be encouraged to treat your criteria as baseline standards whenever AI appears, regardless of methodology.  
   - Your documentation‑based policy proposal explicitly addresses “sociology and adjacent social sciences” and is directed at journals as such. <ref:index={3493626} firstWord={25} lastWord={115}/> Once journals adopt a policy logic, it tends to be applied broadly. There is no mechanism in your proposal to prevent the extension of your standards to interpretive, historical, or critical work that does not fit your pipeline.  
   - By grounding your policy argument in ICMJE authorship criteria and replication infrastructures, you further align sociological epistemic standards with biomedical and econometric norms, while explicitly opposing simple use/non‑use binaries. That is not a neutral scope limitation; it is a bid to reshape evaluative criteria in the field.
   
   To address my concerns, you would need to go further than your current caveats and:
   
   - Explicitly differentiate between *domains within sociology* where your standards should and should not be applied as conditions of publication.  
   - Recognise that making systematic AI documentation a de facto requirement would privilege certain forms of research practice—already advantaged by quantification, funding, and institutional legitimacy—at the expense of others. This is a Bourdieu‑style field effect that your paper currently ignores.  
   - Acknowledge that “epistemic auditability” in the sense you mean is not the only, nor always the most important, dimension of epistemic virtue for sociological work, particularly where the aim is critical interrogation of categories and institutions rather than production of stable, replicable findings.
   
   Without such explicit limits and a sociological analysis of how your framework would interact with existing power structures in the field, the scope disclaimer reads more like a tactical reassurance than a substantive constraint.
