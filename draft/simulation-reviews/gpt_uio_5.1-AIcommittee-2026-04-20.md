1. **Systematic vs unsystematic use and “appropriate use for appropriate tasks”**

The paper’s core distinction between *systematic* and *unsystematic* AI use maps closely to our own “appropriate vs inappropriate use” framing, but with an emphasis on *epistemic structure* rather than on user intentions.

- The author defines *systematic AI authorship* as AI use that is:
  - embedded in explicit criteria,
  - subject to human verification at each stage, and
  - documented so that the process is reconstructible and reviewable <ref: index=3492890 firstWord=1 lastWord=70/>.
- Conversely, *unsystematic* use is prompting without explicit criteria, accepting outputs without verification, and leaving no documentation that would allow auditing or reproduction <ref: index=3492875 firstWord=1 lastWord=70/>.

This structure is very close to what we call “appropriate” use: AI is allowed where it supports but does not replace the core human intellectual work, and where that work remains *inspectable*.

Where the analogy is strongest:

- The paper explicitly rejects the simple use/non-use binary and criticises disclosure-only regimes for failing to distinguish epistemically robust from epistemically weak practice <ref: index=3492892 firstWord=20 lastWord=80/>, <ref: index=3492899 firstWord=1 lastWord=70/>.
- In our terms, “systematic AI authorship” is essentially a worked-out proposal for what “appropriate” AI use in research should look like: it is claimably compatible with existing open-science and authorship standards rather than an exception to them <ref: index=3492868 firstWord=1 lastWord=70/>, <ref: index=3492881 firstWord=1 lastWord=80/>.

Where the analogy breaks when moved to teaching and assessment:

- In research, the central integrity problem is *epistemic accountability*: can peers and readers see what was done and evaluate whether the conclusions are warranted?
- In teaching, our central integrity problem is *certification*: does a grade or degree accurately represent an individual student’s competence, as opposed to the competence of the student-plus-tools?

The paper does develop a *research-side* analogue to certification integrity. It argues that systematic AI use, coupled with ICMJE authorship criteria (conception/design, critical drafting, approval, accountability), allows journals and readers to certify that:

- the human researcher has made a substantial intellectual contribution (via “query authorship”),
- has critically engaged with content (documented through author-input logs), and
- can be held accountable because the process is documented and reconstructible <ref: index=3492896 firstWord=1 lastWord=80/>, <ref: index=3492877 firstWord=40 lastWord=130/>.

In other words, the paper offers a *documentation-based certification* for research authorship: the “certificate” here is authorship itself, backed by replication packages and logs <ref: index=3492870 firstWord=60 lastWord=140/>, <ref: index=3492894 firstWord=60 lastWord=150/>.

However, this certification is collective and system-oriented: it tells us that *this article* is traceable to a responsible human process. It does not directly speak to:

- the *individual skill profile* of the author (e.g., can they themselves write or analyse without AI?),
- nor to how AI use might erode or mask those skills over time.

In teaching, by contrast, our concern is that an exam or assignment stands as an index of what the student can do *unaided*. The paper’s framework helps with *process transparency* but not with distinguishing **“this specific person’s unaided competence”** from “this person’s competence in orchestrating tools.” That distinction is central to our certification worries in education and less central in publication and hiring, where collaborative and tool-mediated work has always been the norm.

So:

- **Yes**, the systematic/unsystematic distinction is a strong analogue to “appropriate/inappropriate use” when the goal is *epistemic responsibility*.
- **No**, it does not directly solve the educational certification problem, because it does not aim to separate unaided from tool-augmented competence, but rather to make tool-augmented competence *auditable*.

2. **Least-addressed committee concern and what would be needed**

Among our four core concerns:

1. certification integrity,
2. shallow learning,
3. metacognitive laziness,
4. cognitive offloading that forecloses genuine learning,

the paper is strongest on (1) and partially on (4), and weakest on (2)–(3) as they pertain to *researchers’ own cognitive development*.

- It directly tackles certification-like issues for research by:
  - using ICMJE criteria positively to articulate what human authorship must consist in when AI is used <ref: index=3492896 firstWord=1 lastWord=80/>;
  - arguing that systematic documentation and replication packages provide a stronger form of accountability than traditional undocumented practice <ref: index=3492877 firstWord=80 lastWord=180/>, <ref: index=3492882 firstWord=20 lastWord=120/>.
- It squarely addresses AI-related *cognitive risks* at the level of output reliability: sycophancy, generalization bias, and “bullshit” outputs that do not track truth <ref: index=3492875 firstWord=70 lastWord=160/>, <ref: index=3492876 firstWord=70 lastWord=170/>.

Where it is thinnest relative to our report is **metacognitive laziness and shallow learning among researchers themselves**:

- The paper argues that systematic use *forces* externalisation of tacit decisions and therefore *disciplines* cognition, analogously to pre-registration <ref: index=3492870 firstWord=140 lastWord=220/>, <ref: index=3492888 firstWord=40 lastWord=140/>.

- But it does not seriously engage with the possibility that even within a systematic workflow, researchers may:
  
  - treat configuration and verification as *formalities* rather than as sites of deep engagement,
  - slide into over-trust of AI-generated structure and prose,
  - and gradually lose fluency in tasks they no longer practice (e.g., independent literature synthesis, argument construction without AI scaffolding).

In other words, the paper treats **systematic structure** as sufficient to secure *actual* critical engagement, whereas our concern is precisely that *apparent* engagement can coexist with weak metacognition.

To address this concern seriously, the paper (or the broader field) would need to:

- **Theorise metacognitive risk explicitly**  
  Not just “AI can be sycophantic,” but “AI may reshape the *distribution* of effort such that researchers’ self-monitoring, reflective judgment, and domain knowledge atrophy, even within documented workflows.”
- **Propose safeguards that go beyond logging and documentation**, for example:
  - Requiring *non-AI* checkpoints: e.g., researchers must perform some fraction of literature synthesis or robustness reasoning without AI assistance and reflect on discrepancies.
  - Embedding *metacognitive prompts* in the workflow (e.g., regular, logged prompts that ask the researcher to articulate why they accept or reject an AI recommendation, in their own words, independent of the AI’s framing).
  - Treating “author-input” files not only as evidence of who did what, but as a site for evaluating *quality of reflection*.
- **Call for empirical study** on whether systematic AI use, over time, correlates with:
  - degraded unaided writing and reasoning skills,
  - overestimation of one’s own critical engagement with AI outputs,
  - or changes in epistemic virtues (intellectual humility, curiosity, willingness to revise).

Without a clear account of these issues, our concern about metacognitive laziness remains unaddressed at the level of *human cognitive development*, even if the paper addresses it at the level of *workflow structure*.

3. **Concentrating cognitive effort vs risk of uncritical acceptance**

The paper’s central epistemic claim is that systematic AI use *reallocates* cognitive effort:

- away from mechanical tasks (typing, routine summarisation),
- toward higher-order work: formulating explicit criteria, configuring review behaviours, and critically verifying outputs <ref: index=3492897 firstWord=80 lastWord=170/>, <ref: index=3492903 firstWord=1 lastWord=80/>.

We find this framing *partially persuasive* as a response to worries about intellectual shallowness:

- Requiring explicit inclusion/exclusion criteria, grounded synthesis with traceable citations, and adversarial reviewer configurations does set the stage for deeper engagement than ad hoc prompting <ref: index=3492873 firstWord=60 lastWord=160/>, <ref: index=3492884 firstWord=60 lastWord=160/>, <ref: index=3492894 firstWord=1 lastWord=90/>.
- The analogy to pre-registration and open-science practice is apt: externalisation of decisions and the production of checkable artefacts are well-established ways to improve both transparency and rigor <ref: index=3492868 firstWord=40 lastWord=140/>, <ref: index=3492883 firstWord=60 lastWord=160/>.

However, two gaps remain vis-à-vis our concern that researchers, like students, may *believe* they are exercising judgment while in practice accepting AI guidance too readily.

1. **The framework ensures visibility, not depth, of engagement.**  
   
   - Documentation shows *that* a human touched and edited the process; it does not show *how searching or demanding* that engagement was.
   - A researcher can satisfy all formal requirements—criteria written down, checkpoints ticked, logs recorded—while exercising only superficial scrutiny.
   - The paper itself acknowledges that “systematic use is a necessary condition … not a sufficient one,” and that human verification “may be superficial” <ref: index=3492886 firstWord=1 lastWord=90/>. That concession aligns with our concern.

2. **The sycophancy fix is structural but still assumes active resistance.**  
   
   - The adversarial reviewer configuration is a thoughtful response to sycophancy <ref: index=3492884 firstWord=120 lastWord=200/>, <ref: index=3492894 firstWord=1 lastWord=80/>.
   - But it still presumes that the researcher:
     - will *take up* the critical feedback,
     - will not simply re-prompt until a more agreeable output appears,
     - and possesses enough independent domain understanding to evaluate the *quality* of adversarial critiques.
   - Our experience with students suggests that even well-designed “criticality prompts” can be ritualised—completed in a perfunctory way that *feels* like critical thinking but does not transform underlying understanding.

So, while the paper’s framing is conceptually coherent and *improves upon* unsystematic use, it does not fully allay our worry that:

- researchers may *overestimate* the depth of their own judgment in AI-assisted workflows, and
- the presence of structure and logs may *mask* rather than reveal shallow engagement.

From our perspective, the key missing element is a way of *measuring or testing* actual critical engagement, beyond the existence of a systematic scaffold.

---

4. **What would move us from cautious openness to active endorsement**

We are, as a committee, open to systematic AI use in research under the kind of constraints this paper articulates. To move from *cautious openness* to **active endorsement**, we would need developments in both the argument of this paper and the broader evidence base:

**a) Empirical evidence on quality and reliability**

- Comparative studies in domains close to sociology showing that systematic AI workflows:
  - improve reproducibility and reduce undetected errors,
  - do not systematically worsen overclaiming or generalisation bias,
  - and do not undermine long-term researcher competence.
- Field or lab studies on:
  - whether systematic AI use changes how researchers search, read, and reason about literature;
  - whether the Zeng et al. finding (reproducible prompts correlate with accuracy) generalises to interpretive and theory-building tasks <ref: index=3492883 firstWord=1 lastWord=80/>.

**b) Explicit treatment of cognitive-developmental effects**

We would want to see:

- A theoretical framework for how AI use affects the *development* of research skills over a PhD and early-career trajectory, including:
  - writing, argumentation, and methodological reasoning,
  - metacognitive skills such as self-evaluation and error detection.
- Empirical work comparing cohorts with different AI-use regimes (systematic, unsystematic, minimal) on:
  - unaided performance,
  - calibration of confidence to accuracy,
  - and resilience against sycophantic or biased AI outputs.

**c) Norms and infrastructure that parallel open science**

The paper rightly emphasises that no fundamentally new infrastructure is needed, only an extension of replication-package norms <ref: index=3492878 firstWord=1 lastWord=120/>, <ref: index=3492882 firstWord=40 lastWord=140/>. For active endorsement, we would want to see:

- Widely adopted **journal policies** that:
  - require systematic documentation for AI-assisted work (not merely disclosure),
  - treat AI workflow artefacts as first-class replication materials,
  - and make peer review of those artefacts routine rather than exceptional <ref: index=3492892 firstWord=60 lastWord=140/>, <ref: index=3492885 firstWord=80 lastWord=170/>.
- **Reviewer training and guidance** to assess AI workflows, analogous to how code review capabilities were normalised in quantitative fields <ref: index=3492878 firstWord=60 lastWord=150/>.
- Integration with credit-taxonomy systems (CRediT, etc.) so that “query authorship” and AI-orchestration work are transparently acknowledged without inflating claims about individual unaided ability <ref: index=3492870 firstWord=1 lastWord=80/>, <ref: index=3492881 firstWord=60 lastWord=150/>.

**d) Governance around boundary conditions**

Finally, we would look for:

- Clear, worked-out models for data protection and legal compliance (e.g., the three-zone data model and PII screening hooks described here) that can be transplanted to Norwegian and European contexts <ref: index=3492898 firstWord=80 lastWord=170/>, <ref: index=3492889 firstWord=80 lastWord=190/>, <ref: index=3492869 firstWord=1 lastWord=110/>.
- Explicit discussion of *limits* to delegation — which research tasks should remain essentially human (e.g., normative judgment, framing of research questions) and why, not only which tasks can be safely automated.

---

**Summary**

We see this paper as an important and generally well-aligned contribution to thinking about *responsible* AI use in research. Its systematic/unsystematic distinction and documentation-based proposal give concrete shape to our own preference for “appropriate use for appropriate tasks in appropriate ways.” It addresses research-side certification and accountability more convincingly than it addresses our concerns about shallow learning and metacognitive laziness, and its optimism about cognitive concentration rests more on structural features than on evidence about actual human engagement.

To move us toward *active endorsement*, we would want to see systematic AI workflows accompanied by:

- empirical demonstrations of epistemic benefits,
- explicit and evidence-based attention to long-term cognitive effects on researchers,
- and mature institutional norms and infrastructures that make systematic practice the default, not the exception.
