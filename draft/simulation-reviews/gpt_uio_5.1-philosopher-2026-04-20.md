
# Comments from a phenomenological / extended-mind perspective

## 1. Where the paper underestimates what is lost

The most serious underestimation of loss lies wherever the paper treats prose generation as a largely *mechanical* task whose cognitive content can be cleanly relocated to “query authorship” and subsequent verification.

Two linchpins:

- The claim that “the intellectual work lies in designing the query, the workflow, and the verification structure, not in the generated text” in the abstract and §5.1–5.5.<ref: index={0} firstWord={23} lastWord={53}/>
- The description in §4.4 of drafting as a stage where a style-configured skill generates text that the researcher then revises, with the “intellectual contribution” framed as residing in the skill specification and accumulated context rather than in sentence-by-sentence composition.<ref: index={0} firstWord={630} lastWord={676}/>

From the standpoint of writing-as-thinking, this elides where a great deal of cognition actually happens:

- In the *micro-adjustments* of phrasing where you realise that your own previous formulation was confused, vague, or committed to a dubious presupposition.
- In the *temporal structure* of composition, where struggling to find a sentence is how you discover what you think at all.
- In the *felt* process of inhabiting an argument through its rhetoric (not just its propositional outline): pacing, rhythm, and emphasis do cognitive work, they are not mere packaging.

Your framework captures the *macro*-level externalisation of commitments (queries, rubrics, configurations) very well, but underestimates the *micro*-level cognitive texture of composing prose. Treating text generation as something whose epistemic value is exhausted by later human revision presumes that nothing essential happens in the act of first articulation. That is precisely what phenomenological and extended-mind views deny.

## 2. A caveat that should be added

A caveat that would honestly acknowledge the cognitive cost would read, in substance, like this:

> Even under systematic use, where queries and verification structures are fully documented, offloading first-pass prose generation to AI is not cognitively neutral. For many scholars, the act of drafting sentences is part of how they discover and refine their own commitments. Delegating that stage may reduce opportunities for conceptual clarification, for noticing tensions in one’s thinking, and for acquiring the tacit skill of argumentation through writing. Systematic AI authorship therefore secures *epistemic accountability* for what is done, but may still alter or attenuate the *cognitive role* that writing itself plays in the formation of ideas.

This can be grounded in your own distinction between “inherent” and “implicit” tacit knowledge (§5.1). You recognise that explicit criteria surface implicit tacit knowledge.<ref: index={0} firstWord={840} lastWord={882}/>  
From my tradition’s perspective, some of that tacit understanding is not merely verbalizable knowledge waiting to be elicited; it is *constituted* in the lived practice of writing. Systematisation helps make it inspectable, but cannot fully replace the learning that occurs through composing.

## 3. Does the “relocation of effort” framing suffice?

Your framing that AI “relocates” cognitive effort — concentrating it in formulation, verification, and revision rather than in mechanical tasks — is an advance over narratives that speak as if effort simply disappears.<ref: index={0} firstWord={90} lastWord={132}/> It addresses worries about *labour* and *accountability*.

It does *not* yet address worries about *cognitive ecology*.

From an extended-mind / embodied-cognition standpoint, where thinking is materially and temporally distributed, the location and sequence of effort matter:

- When writing is interleaved with thinking, the resistance of the page or screen shapes the thought itself.  
- When you front-load effort into designing prompts and back-load it into evaluation, you create a different cognitive loop: query → opaque generation → judgment. It is faster and more auditable, but it offers fewer, and differently-timed, opportunities for “being interrupted by one’s own sentences.”

That means:

- “Relocation” is not mere redistribution of the same kind of cognitive work; it *transforms* the kind of work done and the opportunities for self-correction and discovery.
- Verification and revision of AI prose, even when conscientious, are not equivalent to having gone through the slow, error-prone, self-surprising process of composing those sentences oneself.

So: your relocation framing is epistemically sophisticated, but if the question is whether it answers the worry that something is *lost* when writing is ceded to AI, then more is needed. You would need to explicitly recognise that some cognitive tasks are *inseparable* from particular material practices (like drafting) and that, for those, relocation is *partial substitution plus loss*, not clean delegation.

Concretely, the workflow could be modified to acknowledge this by:

- Recommending that key argumentative passages (e.g., the statement of the central thesis and the core inferential moves) be drafted by the researcher *before* any AI-assisted rewriting.
- Treating AI as a reviser, critic, or translator of already-authored prose, rather than as the default first-pass generator for substantial sections.
- Explicitly distinguishing “writing-as-discovery” from “writing-as-presentation” and constraining delegation in the former case.

Without some such recognition, the paper risks sounding as though once the query and configuration are documented, nothing of epistemic significance hinges on who generated the sentences. That is the deepest point of disagreement.

## 4. What would make me more positive about AI-assisted research writing, and whether this approach moves in that direction

There are two kinds of changes that would make me more positive:

1. **Normative constraints on delegation**
   - Clear norms that certain cognitive tasks *ought not* be delegated: initial articulation of the research question, core argumentative spine, key conceptual distinctions.
   - A strong recommendation that scholars do at least one full, unassisted pass of writing in those regions, using AI only afterwards for critique, clarification, or stylistic refinement.
   - Recognition that, especially for students and early-career scholars, the *skill* of thinking-in-writing is still being formed, and that over-delegation risks genuine cognitive deskilling.

2. **Empirical and phenomenological attention to cognitive impact**
   - Empirical studies of how systematic AI drafting affects argument quality *when controlling for documentation* — does the structure of thought change?
   - Qualitative and phenomenological reports from researchers about how their *experience* of thinking and writing shifts under such workflows.
   - Design of tools and protocols that intentionally preserve spaces for slow, reflective writing, rather than optimising everything for throughput.

Your structured approach *partly* moves in this direction:

- It does a lot to secure **epistemic accountability** and **transparency**; from my perspective, that is a genuine achievement and one I would happily endorse.
- The emphasis on author-input logs and explicit configuration is especially promising, because it foregrounds the human’s conceptual role rather than treating the AI as co-author.<ref: index={0} firstWord={1048} lastWord={1093}/>

However, it also:

- Reinforces a conception of writing as the *execution* of prior commitments rather than as a practice in which those commitments are partly formed.
- Treats systematicity and documentation as largely sufficient to quell deeper worries, when from my tradition’s standpoint they address a different axis (auditability, not cognitive integrity).

So I would summarise my stance this way:

- As a proposal about **how to use AI once we have decided that some delegation is acceptable**, your framework is impressive and, within its own premises, careful and honest.
- As an account of **what is at stake in ceding prose generation**, it needs one more layer: an explicit acknowledgement that writing is not only a vehicle for already-formed thought but a site where thinking happens, and that this imposes principled limits on what a responsible researcher should outsource to an AI, even systematically.

That added layer — in the form of a caveat and some modest constraints on delegation in the drafting stage — would go a long way toward making this a framework that even a cautious extended-mind theorist could regard as a serious attempt to *work with* the cognitive role of writing rather than merely routing around it.
