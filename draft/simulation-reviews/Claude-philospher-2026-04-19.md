# A Philosopher's Reading: Systematic AI Authorship and What the Argument Underestimates

*Response to "Systematic AI Use in Sociological Research" (v3, 2026-04-16)*

---

## Preliminary acknowledgment

The paper does genuine work. The systematic/unsystematic distinction is real and epistemically important. The pre-registration analogy is well-handled — the author correctly identifies the analogy's function (externalisation of tacit commitments) and is honest about its disanalogy (privacy of the initial commitment). The ICMJE analysis is the strongest section: criterion 4 is exactly the right place to press the accountability argument, and the author's claim that systematic AI authorship produces *structural* rather than *presumptive* accountability is genuinely interesting. I also note that the paper's awareness of sycophancy as a structural failure mode — and the adversarial configuration as a documented countermeasure — shows more epistemic sophistication than most work in this space.

What follows is not a general critique. It is the identification of a specific lacuna that the paper's own conceptual commitments make visible.

---

## 1. Where the paper most seriously underestimates what is lost

The paper's central epistemic claim is that intellectual contribution lies in the query — in the specification of criteria, rubrics, and configurations — not in the generated text. I accept this for the searchand-screening register. It is substantially weaker, and perhaps false, for the drafting register, and the paper does not register this difference.

Here is the problem. When a researcher writes prose — even rough, reworked, inadequate prose — something happens that is not well described as "executing a specification." The resistance of language to the thought being formed is itself a cognitive event. A sentence that will not close properly is evidence that the argument it was trying to express has not yet been fully formed. A paragraph that keeps expanding is evidence of an unresolved tension in the underlying claim. This is not a metaphor about "craft." It is a claim about the epistemic function of the generative struggle itself: the writer discovers, through the resistance, that the thought is not yet complete.

The paper's workflow bypasses this. The researcher specifies criteria, reviews AI output, revises, redirects. But the generative resistance — the moment of discovering through failed articulation that you do not yet know what you mean — is not part of the loop. The author acknowledges this obliquely: "Drafts produced by a configured skill are inputs to human revision, not outputs for direct use." But revision and generation are epistemically different acts. Revision presupposes that the thought has already been formed somewhere; the editor asks whether it has been expressed correctly. Generation is where thoughts that could not have been specified in advance sometimes come into existence. What is lost in AI-assisted drafting is not "writing" in a generic sense but the specific cognitive events that occur at the generative threshold — events that are not downstream of specification and cannot be captured by more precise specifications.

This is the deepest point my tradition makes against the paper's account. Extended mind theory holds that cognition is sometimes constituted by external scaffolding, not merely supported by it. The paper implicitly relies on this: the documentation structure, the prompts, the session logs are external cognitive supports that extend the researcher's reasoning. I accept this framing entirely. But the argument cuts both ways. If the external scaffolding is constitutive of the thinking, then changing the scaffolding changes the thinking — not merely the efficiency of its expression, but its content and its limits. A researcher who offloads generation to an AI trained on the existing literature will think thoughts that are continuous with that literature in a particular way: they will think thoughts that are well-expressible, conventionally structured, and unlikely to require the kind of linguistic struggle that, historically, sometimes forces a genuinely new formulation. The workflow optimises for articulable knowledge. It may systematically underperform on whatever lies just beyond articulability.

The paper does not address this. It treats prose generation as mechanical execution of a specification and therefore as legitimately delegable. I am arguing that it is sometimes — not always, but sometimes, and more often in theoretical work than in empirical — a constitutive cognitive act, and that its delegation has a cost that cannot be recovered by more careful revision.

---

## 2. The one caveat the author should add

The paper should acknowledge, in §4.4 or §5.1, something like the following:

*"The workflow described here relocates generative effort from prose production to specification and revision. This relocation is epistemically sound for research tasks where the intellectual contribution is in the criteria, rubrics, and verification structure — literature search, systematic screening, code review, adversarial checking. For theoretical argumentation and for sections of a paper where the argument is being developed rather than expressed, the generative act itself may be where conceptual work occurs that cannot be recovered downstream. Researchers using this workflow for drafting should monitor whether their revision process is genuinely critical engagement with an articulated argument, or whether it is editing an already-closed form. The two look similar from the outside; they are epistemically different."*

This is not a refutation of the paper's framework. It is the honest acknowledgment that the framework applies with different force at different stages of the research pipeline, and that the drafting stage is the one where the framework's assumptions are most likely to be false.

---

## 3. Does the relocation framing adequately address the concern?

Partially, but not fully.

The paper's relocation argument is its most sophisticated move, and it is largely correct for the research stages where specification precedes execution. Literature screening, analytical code generation, verification review — these are tasks where it is plausible that the intellectual work was always in the design, and that manual execution was genuinely mechanical. The relocation claim is well-supported for these stages.

For drafting, the relocation framing fails in a specific way. It assumes that cognitive effort is fungible across stages — that effort expended on specification is a fair substitute for effort expended on generation. My objection is that these are not interchangeable: the effort at specification produces one kind of cognitive output (explicit criteria, structured commitments), while the effort at generation sometimes produces another kind (contact with the resistance of thought, discovery of what one does not yet know). These are not the same thing, and relocating from the second to the first is not a neutral redistribution.

There is a further point the paper could press back with: if the outline, synthesis, and iterative argument development are done by the researcher — which the paper's workflow requires — then the generative work has happened, and drafting is indeed expression rather than discovery. This is a reasonable response, and for a well-specified empirical paper with a clear structure it may be largely correct. My concern is that it may not hold for papers that are working something out rather than expressing something already worked out. The paper under discussion may be one of the former — a methodological contribution where the argument is being developed in the writing, not just expressed through it. If so, the relocation claim is self-undermining: the paper arguing that generation can be safely delegated may itself be an example of research where it cannot.

I am not claiming this defeats the paper. I am claiming it reveals a constraint the paper should state explicitly: *the relocation framing holds most firmly for research where the argument precedes the text. It is weakest for research where the text partly constitutes the argument.*

---

## 4. What would make me more positive, and whether the paper moves in that direction

What would genuinely address my concern is a distinction within the drafting stage between *expressive prose* and *generative prose* — a recognition that some writing is giving form to an established thought, and some writing is the act through which the thought becomes established. For the first, the paper's framework is defensible. For the second, I would want the author to recommend that the researcher attempt the generation themselves first, and treat AI assistance as available only after that attempt — after the resistance has been encountered and the thought has been brought to the point of near-articulability. The AI then helps express what the researcher has genuinely struggled toward, rather than bypassing the struggle.

The paper does not make this distinction, and I take that as a real gap. But it moves toward my position in one underappreciated way: the author-input files and the emphasis on the researcher's active redirection of AI outputs are, if taken seriously, exactly the kind of practice I am describing. A researcher who genuinely redirects — who encounters AI output as resistance, notes where it fails to say what needs saying, and formulates the correction — is engaging in something close to the generative cognitive work I am pointing to. The format changes; the function may be partially preserved.

Whether it is fully preserved is an empirical question my tradition cannot settle from the armchair. The honest position is that we do not know how much of the constitutive cognitive function of writing survives when generation is delegated and revision is the primary mode of engagement. The paper treats this as settled in favour of the workflow. It is not settled. That uncertainty should be in the paper.

The structured approach described here is, on balance, better than unsystematic AI use and better than naive prohibition. It takes the right problems seriously — sycophancy, forking paths, tacit commitment — and addresses them more rigorously than competing frameworks. My concern is not with the framework's validity for the tasks it most directly addresses, but with the confidence of its extension to the drafting stage.

The argument needs one more degree of epistemic humility. Not about the policy framework, which is sound. About what systematic AI authorship cannot yet demonstrate: that the researcher who delegates generation and revises carefully is doing the same cognitive work as the researcher who generates and revises. Until that question is answered, the caveat belongs in the paper.
