# Assessment by the Malthe-Sørensen Committee
## Response to: "Systematic AI Use in Sociological Research" (Skardhamar, v3, 2026-04-16)

**Date:** 2026-04-19  
**Status:** Collective assessment — not a full peer review

---

## Prefatory note

We were asked to assess this paper from the vantage point of a government-appointed committee whose mandate covers AI in teaching and assessment, but whose members are active researchers who have formed views on AI in research through that work. We do not here evaluate the paper's contribution to the teaching literature — that is not its purpose. We bring to it the conceptual vocabulary of our report and ask how its argument reads from that vantage point. Our committee is neither hostile to AI nor to this paper; our charge is careful, evidence-based assessment of epistemic responsibility.

---

## 1. Does the systematic/unsystematic distinction map onto our concern about appropriate versus inappropriate use — and where does the analogy hold and where does it break down?

The mapping is real and in important respects instructive. Our committee's preferred framing — *appropriate use for appropriate tasks in appropriate ways* — was developed in the context of assessment integrity, where we argued that the relevant policy question is not whether students use AI but whether that use is epistemically responsible given what the task is designed to develop and certify. The paper's distinction between systematic and unsystematic AI use turns on precisely the same axis: not whether AI was used, but whether its use was structured to make errors visible, decisions explicit, and outputs verifiable. In both cases, the binary (used/not used, permitted/prohibited) misidentifies the relevant dimension.

The analogy holds most clearly at the level of *process structure*. What we call appropriate use in assessment contexts — use that supports the cognitive work the task is designed to require — corresponds to what the paper calls systematic use: use embedded in explicit criteria, subject to human verification, and producing documented outputs. What we worry about in students — accepting AI output without genuine critical engagement, outsourcing the cognitive effort that produces learning — corresponds closely to the paper's characterisation of unsystematic use, in which criteria are implicit, outputs are accepted without verification, and the process that produced them is invisible.

The analogy also holds at the level of *enforcement strategy*. Our committee concluded that AI detection tools are not a viable enforcement mechanism and that the appropriate response is structural: design assessment that makes the relevant cognitive effort legible. The paper's argument for documentation-based journal policy over binary prohibition makes the same move — not detecting or prohibiting, but making the epistemic structure of research practice evaluable.

**Where the analogy breaks down: the certification problem.**

Our committee's sharpest concern is not about learning quality per se but about the *certification function* of higher education. Degrees and grades are social facts — signals to employers, institutions, and society about what the holder is capable of doing. Even if AI-assisted student work were epistemically excellent as a product, it may not certify what it is supposed to certify: that the student can do the relevant cognitive work independently, at the standard the credential implies. This is not a concern about the quality of a particular piece of work; it is a concern about what the credential means.

Does research have an analogue? We think yes — but the analogy requires careful formulation. The certification problem in research is not about degrees; it is about **attributed competence**. A published article in a prestigious journal certifies, among other things, that the named researcher is capable of the intellectual work the article represents: identifying the right question, selecting and interpreting evidence, constructing and defending an argument. If systematic AI authorship *actually* relocates that cognitive work — compresses it into query formulation, verification, and adversarial configuration — then the certification remains valid: those are genuinely intellectual acts. The paper argues this forcefully, and we find it largely persuasive.

However, the paper does not fully engage with the scenario in which the certification breaks down: where a researcher claims systematic authorship through documentation but the documented intellectual acts are thin — rubrics that took minutes to write, configurations copied from templates, author-input files that record compliance rather than intellectual direction. The documentation framework can certify a form; it cannot straightforwardly certify the substance of the intellectual contribution. This is not fatal to the paper's argument — the same gap exists in traditional research, where methods sections report what was done without certifying how deeply the researcher understood it. But in the AI case, the gap is systematically easier to exploit, because the distance between following a workflow and understanding what justifies it is compressible in new ways. The paper acknowledges that documentation can be faked (§5.4), but does not develop what we would consider the more concerning adjacent case: documentation that is genuine but thin, produced by a researcher who followed the workflow without the intellectual substance the framework is designed to certify.

**A direct question the paper can help us answer.**

Our committee has been cautious about endorsing AI use for assessed work precisely because we lack confidence that current frameworks distinguish genuine cognitive engagement from its appearance. The paper's query authorship concept is, in principle, a tool for making that distinction: a well-formed inclusion criterion is evidence of theoretical engagement; a vague one is not. If this concept were developed to specify what counts as a *substantively* adequate query — not just a formally present one — it would give our committee a framework for distinguishing genuine from superficial engagement that we currently lack. The paper gestures toward this but does not deliver it.

## 2. The concern the paper most fails to address: metacognitive laziness and the illusion of judgment

Of the four concerns our report identifies — certification integrity, shallow learning, metacognitive laziness, and cognitive offloading — the paper most conspicuously fails to address **metacognitive laziness**: the failure to recognise the limits of one's own critical engagement with AI output.

We distinguish this from cognitive offloading, which is a deliberate delegation of cognitive labour. Metacognitive laziness is more insidious: it is the condition in which a person *believes* they are exercising critical judgment while in fact they are not. The adversarial configuration (§4.5) addresses confirmation bias at the level of AI output, and we acknowledge this is a structural improvement over default-mode use. But configuring the tool to be critical does not guarantee that the researcher can evaluate the criticism it produces. A researcher who lacks the relevant domain expertise, or who is operating at the edge of their competence in a subfield, may engage with AI-generated critical feedback the way a weak student engages with track-changes comments: accepting what seems plausible, rejecting what creates discomfort, and producing the illusion of a revision cycle without the substance.

The paper is aware of a related risk — sycophancy, the AI producing affirming outputs — and offers a structural countermeasure. But sycophancy is a property of the AI output. Metacognitive laziness is a property of the researcher's response to it, and it is not addressable by configuring the tool. It requires the researcher to have, and exercise, genuine critical capacity relative to the AI's outputs.

This gap matters for our committee's concerns because it is precisely the condition we worry about in students: not that AI does the work *and* they know it, but that AI does the work *and* they believe they have done it. Experienced researchers with deep domain expertise are probably less vulnerable to this failure than students, and the paper is written for that audience. But the paper makes no argument about the conditions under which the verification checkpoints it prescribes are sufficient — which is to say, what level of researcher competence is required for the framework to do what it promises. A junior researcher, or a researcher venturing outside their core expertise using AI to extend their range, is in a structurally different epistemic position from an expert using AI to handle volume. The paper's framework applies to both but is calibrated for the expert case.

**What the paper would need to say.** It would need to acknowledge that human verification is only as good as the human doing the verifying, and that systematic AI authorship creates the conditions for good verification but does not guarantee it. At minimum, it would need to specify what conditions make verification credible: relevant domain expertise, familiarity with the AI's known failure modes, and — crucially — the epistemic attitude of treating verification as a genuine test rather than a formality. The author-input file concept approaches this but does not close the gap: a researcher can document that they read and accepted each section without documenting whether they were in a position to evaluate it.

## 3. Is the cognitive-effort framing persuasive as a response to the concern about intellectual shallowness?

Partly, and more so for experienced researchers than for the population of AI users in general. The paper's core claim here — that systematic AI use *concentrates* cognitive effort at formulation, verification, and revision rather than distributing it across mechanical tasks — is an important corrective to the lazy reading of AI use as straightforwardly displacing intellectual work. We find the argument that query formulation is itself an intellectual act to be well-supported, and the pre-registration analogy illuminating. Forcing explicit specification of inclusion criteria, analytical strategies, and evaluative standards before running the tool is a discipline that many researchers currently avoid through the comfortable ambiguity of undocumented manual judgement.

However, the framing does not adequately address the risk of accepting AI outputs without genuine critical engagement even when the researcher believes they are exercising judgment. Three specific limitations concern us.

**First, the pre-registration analogy has a gap the paper understates.** Pre-registration's epistemic value lies not only in forcing articulation of decisions before outcomes are known, but in the *asymmetry of cost*: articulating a bad design in advance is uncomfortable in a way that quietly changing it post hoc is not. The social and professional cost of visibly deviating from a pre-registration is what gives pre-registration its disciplinary force. Systematic AI authorship imposes the articulation cost but not the asymmetric cost of deviation: a researcher can write an inclusion criterion, run the search, find the results unsatisfying, revise the criterion, and document the revision as a legitimate methodological refinement. The paper acknowledges this (the disanalogy regarding publicity in §3.2) but treats the process log as an adequate substitute. We are less confident. A process log documents that criteria were revised; it does not explain whether the revision was epistemically justified or opportunistic. This is a version of the garden-of-forking-paths problem the paper invokes (§5.1) — the documentation forecloses some paths but does not guarantee that the chosen path was taken for the right reasons.

**Second, verification is described as a human checkpoint but not as an adversarial test.** The paper correctly specifies that human verification involves checking AI outputs against explicit criteria before the output proceeds to the next stage. But checking against criteria is not the same as *testing* those outputs against the possibility of being wrong. Our committee's concern about intellectual shallowness is specifically about the difference between review-as-endorsement and review-as-scrutiny. The paper's adversarial configuration addresses this for the AI reviewer but not for the human verifier. A researcher who reads each synthesised source claim against the cited document, and finds that it is accurately represented, has performed a genuine epistemic act. A researcher who reads each section and finds it "looks right" has not, even if they are operating within a documented systematic workflow. The paper does not distinguish these cases.

**Third, and most fundamentally: the concentration-of-effort argument rests on an assumption about what researchers do with the cognitive capacity that is freed up.** The paper assumes — it has to, for the argument to work — that the effort no longer spent on mechanical execution is redirected toward deeper conceptual and critical engagement. This may often be true of the researchers who would invest in the systematic infrastructure the paper describes. But it is not guaranteed by the workflow itself. The workflow can be faithfully executed by a researcher who is simultaneously less intellectually engaged with the research question than they would be if they had performed the mechanical tasks themselves — because the mechanical tasks, done attentively, generate the granular familiarity with the material that enables the critical judgment the verification checkpoints are supposed to exercise. This is a genuine tension, and the paper does not resolve it.

We want to be precise: we are not arguing that mechanical task performance is intrinsically valuable or that researchers must suffer through it to do good research. We are arguing that the paper's account of what researchers do with released cognitive capacity is asserted rather than examined, and that this is exactly the question our committee's concern about intellectual shallowness requires it to answer.

## 4. What would move our committee from cautious openness toward active endorsement?

Our committee's current position is cautious openness: we acknowledge that systematic AI use as described in this paper is epistemically more responsible than unsystematic use, and that the documentation framework is a genuine advance over binary policies. We are not prepared to move to active endorsement on the basis of this paper alone. What would move us:

**In the paper itself:**

1. *A developed account of verification quality.* The framework needs to specify not only that human verification checkpoints exist but what makes them credible. This means engaging with the conditions — researcher competence, epistemic orientation, domain expertise — that determine whether verification is genuine scrutiny or procedural compliance. Without this, the framework certifies a process but not its substance.

2. *A treatment of the competence threshold.* The framework is calibrated, implicitly, for experienced researchers with deep expertise in the domain they are working in. It should say so explicitly, and it should address what happens when researchers use it to extend their range into subfields where their verification capacity is limited. This is where the metacognitive laziness risk is highest and where the framework's protections are thinnest.

3. *An engagement with the opportunistic-revision problem.* The claim that process logs address the cherry-picking risk associated with undocumented criterion revision needs more development. Either the paper needs to specify what a legitimate criterion revision looks like and how it is distinguished from opportunistic revision, or it needs to acknowledge that this is a limitation the framework cannot fully close.

**In the broader field:**

4. *Empirical evidence that systematic workflows produce epistemically different research outcomes, not just more auditable processes.* The Zeng et al. (2025) finding is suggestive but not transferable. Research in sociology and adjacent social sciences comparing matched projects conducted with and without systematic AI workflows — on dimensions such as literature coverage, argument coherence, and post-publication correction rates — would substantially strengthen the case.

5. *Evidence on verification behaviour in practice.* Do researchers using systematic workflows actually exercise the critical engagement the framework assumes, or do they execute the verification checkpoints as procedural formalities? This is an empirical question, and its answer would determine whether the framework does what the paper says it does or whether it produces the appearance of systematic practice without its substance. This would be difficult research to conduct, but it is the research our committee would most like to see.

6. *A community standard for what counts as a substantively adequate query.* The query authorship concept is valuable but underdeveloped as a policy instrument. A query authorship standard — specifying what level of theoretical elaboration in an inclusion criterion, skill configuration, or analytical specification is required for the framework to certify what it is supposed to certify — would give editors, reviewers, and researchers a tractable evaluative instrument. Without this, documentation-based policy risks certifying presence rather than quality.

---

## Summary

The paper's central distinction is genuinely useful, and the documentation framework it proposes is a significant improvement over both binary prohibition and blanket disclosure. The mapping onto our committee's concern about appropriate use is real, and the query authorship concept provides intellectual tools we have lacked. The paper is most persuasive on the epistemic value of externalising tacit commitments and on the alignment between systematic AI documentation and open-science infrastructure. It is least persuasive — indeed, conspicuously silent — on the conditions under which human verification is credible, and on the risk that researchers, like students, may engage with AI outputs in ways that produce the appearance of critical judgment without its substance. Addressing that gap would move our committee from viewing this paper as a well-argued case for cautious permission toward viewing it as the foundation for an active endorsement framework.
