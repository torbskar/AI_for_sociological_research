## **Critique of Skardhamar (2026): "Systematic AI Use in Sociological Research"**

**From:** Professor Martin Delacroix

**Date:** April 19, 2026

**Subject:** Epistemic limitations and the "Replication" Trap

Torbjørn, you have provided a remarkably clear manual for the bureaucratisation of the sociological imagination. While your technical "workflow" is undoubtedly rigorous, it rests on several epistemological assumptions that I find, at best, naive and, at worst, an abdication of the researcher’s primary duty to the dialectic.

Below are the points that require your immediate and unvarnished attention.

### ---

**1\. Arguments Needing Qualification**

**A. The Pre-registration Analogy (Section 5.1)** You argue that systematic AI use is epistemically virtuous because it mirrors **pre-registration** by "forcing explicit commitment to decisions before outcomes are known." To a theorist, this is not a virtue; it is a **hermeneutic suicide**. You assume that the "externalisation" of tacit knowledge is a neutral act of transparency. It is not.

In the Bourdieusian sense, the *habitus* of the researcher—the "feel for the game"—is precisely what allows for the recognition of a sociological "break" (the *rupture épistémologique*). By forcing a researcher to codify their inclusion criteria *before* they have wrestled with the text, you are mandating **instrumental rationality**. You must qualify this by acknowledging that "systematicity" may actually blind the researcher to the unexpected or the "lack" (in the Lacanian sense) that only emerges through the messy, non-linear process of manual reading and reflection.

**B. The Concept of "Query Authorship" (Section 3.2)** You claim that "the intellectual work lies in designing the query... not in the generated text." This is a dangerously thin definition of authorship. It reduces the sociologist to a **software architect** or a prompt-manager. In the tradition of Habermas, the "validity claims" of a text are established through communicative action—a process of intersubjective struggle.

When you delegate the drafting to a configured "skill," you are not just delegating "execution"; you are delegating the **process of thinking-through-writing**. Writing is not the *report* of a thought; it is the *site* of thought. Your argument needs to address the risk that "query authorship" is merely an intellectual placeholder for a profound alienation from the object of study.

### ---

**2\. The Essential Caveat**

If you wish to reduce my resistance, you must add a caveat stating that **systematic AI use is a method of *administrative accountability*, not a guarantor of *epistemic depth*.** You must concede that a perfectly documented, reproducible, and systematic AI workflow can still produce **profoundly banal sociology**. Your current draft flirts with the "reliability finding" (Section 5.3) as if "accuracy" and "reproducibility" were the primary goals of our discipline. Admit that this framework is designed to satisfy the *auditor* and the *journal editor*, and that it provides no protection against the theoretical impoverishment that comes from delegating synthesis to a machine.

### ---

**3\. The Genuinely Interesting Argument**

I found your discussion of **Adversarial Configuration (Section 4.5)** genuinely compelling. Why? Because it acknowledges the **sycophancy of the machine**.

The observation that LLMs are structurally "truth-indifferent" (the "bullshit" problem) and tend to confirm user priors is the most sociologically astute part of the paper. Your attempt to "code" a critic—to build a "reviewer skill" that plays devil’s advocate—is a fascinating, if somewhat tragic, attempt to simulate the **Lacanian "Other."** It suggests that we know the machine is a mirror of our own narcissism and that we are desperately trying to program a way to break that mirror. This is a real contribution to the sociology of knowledge.

### ---

**4\. Assessment of Scope Limitations**

Your explicit scope limitation—stating this is for "text-heavy and empirical" work and not "theoretical work"—is **entirely inadequate**.

It relies on a false dichotomy between "empirical analysis" and "interpretation." You suggest that "literature search" and "screening" (Section 4.2) are somehow neutral, systematic tasks that can be "delegated" while the "real" theory happens elsewhere. But the act of categorising a text *is* a theoretical act. Choosing what to "exclude" based on a "systematic rubric" *is* an exercise of symbolic power.

You need to go further: you must acknowledge that by making the empirical pipeline "systematic" in the way you describe, you are **pre-defining what counts as sociology** to be only that which can be captured by a boolean string or a screening log. You are not just offering a tool; you are advocating for a specific, restrictive ontology of social research. You must admit that this "systematic" approach may be fundamentally incompatible with any sociology that seeks to uncover deeper structures of meaning that do not "fit" into a pre-registered rubric.
