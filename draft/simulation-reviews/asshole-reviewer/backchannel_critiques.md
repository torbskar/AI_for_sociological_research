# Two Backchannel Critiques

*What one says at lunch, to the right people, when the target is not present and one has no intention of ever putting it in writing. These are not the attacks one would make in a seminar. They are the judgements one would make about whether a colleague is still doing serious work. The register is collegial-confidential, and the damage — if it lands — is to reputation rather than to the paper.*

---

## 1. The sociology-of-knowledge read: Torbjørn has fallen in love with his workflow

*— over coffee, to a sympathetic senior colleague who also does quantitative work —*

You've seen the draft? I read it last week. Look, Torbjørn is one of the most careful quantitative people we have, and I'd never say this to his face, but — I think he has fallen in love with his tools. You know the pattern. A clever person sets up an elaborate infrastructure for something, the infrastructure works, and then the infrastructure becomes the subject of the paper rather than the instrument for one. The whole argument is a philosophical gloss on what is, if you strip it down, a description of his own project folder. The skill files, the CLAUDE.md, the author-input logs, the version-log script — these are the artefacts of a working researcher's setup, and he is now arguing that they constitute a methodological contribution to sociology.

The tell is the self-demonstration move. He acknowledges — honestly, I'll grant him that — that the paper's conceptual moves developed hermeneutically, and that the systematic infrastructure supported rather than produced them. But then the paper goes on to argue that the infrastructure *is* the methodological contribution. Read those two claims next to each other. If the infrastructure did not produce the conceptual content of this paper, what exactly is it producing for anyone else? The most honest reading of his own admission is that the infrastructure is a competent researcher's note-keeping system, dressed up as an epistemological framework. The work is being done by Torbjørn's judgement; the workflow is recording the work, not producing it.

There is something slightly sad about this. Torbjørn has always been good at the thing that cannot be taught — knowing which question to ask. And now he is writing a paper that says the important part is the documentation of how you asked it. It reads as if he is anxious about justifying the AI use to himself, and has built a framework that converts his anxiety into methodology. You see this in senior people sometimes when a new tool arrives and they want to make sure their adoption of it counts as principled rather than faddish. The Freese-and-Peterson citation, the Popperian framing, the ICMJE reach — these are all doing reassurance work. The paper is more about Torbjørn's relationship with his tools than about sociology's.

I wouldn't say this in review, obviously. But between us — I think this is the kind of paper one writes when one has stopped doing the empirical work that made one's reputation. The segregation papers, the trajectory work, the microgeography piece — that is the work. This is the hobbyhorse.

## 2. The generational read: this is what happens when a quant person discovers theory late

*— at a departmental lunch, to two colleagues from qualitative or theoretical backgrounds —*

Have either of you looked at Torbjørn's AI paper? It is being circulated. I find it fascinating in a slightly uncomfortable way. He is making an argument about tacit knowledge, authorship, hermeneutic work, the externalisation of criteria — and the whole conceptual apparatus is being reinvented from scratch, as if none of us have been thinking about this for forty years. Polanyi is not cited. Collins on tacit knowledge is not cited. The entire qualitative-methods tradition on coder training, negotiated coding, and the documentation of interpretive decisions — absent. Krippendorff appears nowhere. He cites Freese and Peterson on replication and thinks that is enough to cover the open-science ground; meanwhile he is using "query authorship" and "externalising tacit commitments" as if these are new concepts rather than the bread and butter of methodological discussions we have been having in the qualitative wing since the 1980s.

What is happening, I think, is that Torbjørn has had a genuine intellectual experience — working with these tools has made him notice that the interesting work lies in specification rather than execution — and he has mistaken the experience for a discovery. It is a discovery, but only for someone who has spent a career inside a paradigm where this was already understood as the received view. A qualitative methodologist reading this paper will feel the way one feels when a very smart undergraduate bursts in to tell you something you teach in the first week. One does not want to be unkind, but one does notice.

The ICMJE move is the clearest example. He takes a biomedical-editorial document about who gets to be on an author list, and he builds an epistemology out of it. The ICMJE criteria exist because clinical trials had a ghost-authorship problem in the 1980s. They are not an epistemology of contribution. Treating them as one — as if the Vancouver criteria are where sociology goes for a theory of the human researcher's role — is a category error that a theoretically-trained sociologist would not make. It is the move of someone who has spent thirty years not needing to read this literature and has now decided to reinvent it.

I don't want to be dismissive. Torbjørn is an excellent methodologist in his domain. But the paper is doing the thing that quantitative people sometimes do when they wander into meta-methodological territory — treating the absence of a systematic literature in their immediate vicinity as evidence that the literature does not exist. It does. He has not read it. And the paper reads, to someone who has, as a confident contribution to a conversation the author has not attended.

It will probably be published somewhere. But it will be read, in the subfields whose intellectual territory he is unknowingly reinventing, as a slightly embarrassing artefact — the paper a senior quantitative sociologist writes when he decides, late in his career, that he has something to say about methodology in general. We have all seen these papers. They are rarely the author's best work.

## Why these hurt more than the five public attacks

The five public attacks target the argument. These target the author. They are not refutations; they are re-descriptions of the paper as the product of a particular kind of person in a particular kind of professional moment — the senior quantitative researcher who has fallen for his tools, the career-quant who has discovered theory late. Neither re-description can be rebutted by textual changes because neither is about the text. They are about what kind of paper this is, and what kind of colleague Torbjørn has become in writing it.

The defence against this form of critique is not pre-emption. It is the engagement with the literatures the second critique names — Polanyi, Collins, Krippendorff, the qualitative methods tradition on interpretive-decision documentation — demonstrated in the text itself. Cite them, engage them, mark where the AI context genuinely changes the terms of the older discussions and where it does not. A paper that has clearly read the prior literature cannot be dismissed as the product of someone who has not read it.

The first critique, the sociology-of-knowledge read, is harder to pre-empt because it is in part true: the paper does emerge from Torbjørn's relationship with his own infrastructure, and the self-demonstration move invites the re-description. The defence here is narrower contribution claims — the revisions proposed in the earlier defence memo do this — plus, possibly, an explicit acknowledgement in the introduction that the paper generalises from a particular practice and a statement of why the generalisation is defensible despite that provenance. This does not eliminate the critique but it removes the element of concealment the critic is implicitly alleging.

Neither critique will be made to Torbjørn. Both will be made about him. The professional cost is diffuse, accumulates across conversations he will not hear, and does not surface in review comments. This is why this register matters and why the discipline's methodological papers are, on the whole, more cautious than its empirical ones.
