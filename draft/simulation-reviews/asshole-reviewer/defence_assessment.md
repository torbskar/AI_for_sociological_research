# Defence Assessment and Draft Pre-emptive Moves

*For the five attacks on the v3 draft. Assessment first, concrete textual moves second. The working principle: the honest version of each attack is partly right, and pre-emption should consist of conceding the partial correctness with precision and then showing why it does not carry the critic's conclusion. Pre-emption is not inoculation; it is the discipline of making your own argument stronger by not over-claiming.*

---

## Priority ordering

**Must defend, and must defend in the paper itself**: Attack 1 (ICMJE category error), Attack 5 (venue fit and replication-package rhetoric).

**Should defend, with a narrowing rather than an expansion**: Attack 2 (query-authorship novelty), Attack 4 (binary-policy straw man).

**Defensible with the existing §5.4 machinery, but the concession needs to be sharper**: Attack 3 (self-demonstration fails on its own terms).

The ordering is set by damage potential, not by how technically satisfying the attack is. Attack 3 is the most internally elegant critique but the one most easily absorbed by existing concessive moves. Attacks 1 and 5 are the ones that function as desk-level objections: Attack 1 because it undermines the paper's most citeable move (the ICMJE positive-application), Attack 5 because it operates before the paper is read on the merits.

## Attack 1 — ICMJE category error

**Assessment**: The attack is substantively correct. Criterion 4 in the ICMJE document is an institutional-accountability commitment, not an evidentiary standard, and the "arguably stronger" line in §5.5 does substitute one concept of accountability for another. This is the single most fixable weakness in the draft, and also the one with the highest public cost if left.

**What changes**: Demote ICMJE from normative source to motivating analogue. The positive application stops being a claim about what the criteria already require and becomes a claim about what a parallel evidentiary standard would look like.

**Draft text (replaces the relevant passages in §5.5 and the abstract)**:

*For the abstract, last sentence* —

> The ICMJE authorship criteria ("Vancouver criteria"), while not an evidentiary standard in their own terms, suggest by analogy a more tractable question for policy than whether AI was used: whether the researcher can demonstrate the contributions the criteria already require them to have made.

*For the §5.5 passage containing "arguably a stronger one"* —

> A clarification is necessary here. The ICMJE criteria are an institutional-accountability document: criterion 4 commits the named author to answer for the work, not to produce a particular form of evidence that the work is answerable-for. A contemporaneous paper trail and a researcher's capacity to respond to questions are different things, and the first does not substitute for the second. What systematic AI authorship provides is not a stronger version of ICMJE accountability but an evidentiary supplement to it — materials that support the kind of investigation criterion 4 envisages without themselves constituting the accountable commitment the criterion names. The policy implication follows in this weaker, and I think more defensible, form: journals seeking to evaluate whether the ICMJE criteria are substantively met in AI-assisted work have, in documentation-based policy, an instrument that the binary alternatives do not provide.

**Why this is enough**: It costs you one rhetorical line ("arguably stronger") and converts the ICMJE move from a contested reading of a source document into an explicitly analogical argument. The analogical version is harder to attack because it no longer claims the criteria's authority for a reading the criteria themselves do not support. The argumentative work the ICMJE framing does in the paper — giving editors a familiar reference point — survives intact.

## Attack 5 — venue fit and replication-package rhetoric

**Assessment**: Partly a genuine objection to the argument, partly a submission-strategy question. The submission-strategy question is outside the paper's control, but the argument-level concern — that §6.3 treats replication-package norms as generating a consistency requirement they do not generate — is real and fixable.

**What changes**: Stop arguing that journals with replication-package requirements "face a consistency challenge" in treating AI workflow documentation differently. Argue instead that replication-package requirements provide infrastructural compatibility — the same deposition systems, reviewer expectations, and editorial practices — that make documentation-based AI policy low-cost to implement. This is a different claim and a defensible one.

**Draft text (replaces the two sentences in §6.3 about the consistency challenge)**:

> Journals that already require replication packages for quantitative work have the infrastructure — deposition systems, reviewer practices, and editorial workflows — that documentation-based AI policy would use. This is not an argument that the existing requirement entails the new one: the quantitative replication-package norm addresses reproducibility of reported estimates, while AI workflow documentation addresses auditability of a research process, and these are distinct purposes. It is an argument that the institutional cost of implementing documentation-based AI policy is lower at journals that have already built the infrastructure for a different but related transparency standard.

**Additional move — address venue fit directly in §1 or in a footnote**:

> The argument developed here is methodological: it proposes a workflow, a conceptual vocabulary, and a policy framework. Readers will observe that it does not report primary data or a replicable estimate; the replication package accompanying this paper consists of workflow artefacts rather than analytical code and data, and is offered as a demonstration of the proposed framework rather than as a reproduction aid in the sense *Sociological Science*'s replication policy ordinarily addresses. I have opted to develop the argument here because the journal's explicit commitment to methodological transparency — and the audience of quantitatively-oriented sociologists that commitment attracts — is the one best positioned to evaluate whether the workflow described would be adopted in their own practice.

**Why this is enough for the argument-level concern**: The consistency-requirement claim was always weak on inspection, and the infrastructural-compatibility claim is both honest and sufficient for the policy move §6.3 needs to make. The explicit note on venue fit pre-empts the objection rather than hoping reviewers will not notice it — "we know this is an unusual submission for the journal and here is why we chose it" is a standard move and reviewers generally respect it. Whether it is enough for the submission-strategy concern is a separate question on which the pre-emption makes no difference: if the editor judges fit is wrong, the paper is desk-rejected regardless of how the footnote is phrased.

**Strategic note outside the paper**: *Sociological Methods & Research* is a stronger venue fit and would eliminate Attack 5 entirely. *The American Sociologist* would eliminate it and reframe the paper for the audience the policy argument actually addresses. Your stated target is *Sociological Science* and I assume you have a reason; the pre-emptive footnote above is the right move if the target stands.

## Attack 2 — query-authorship novelty

**Assessment**: Partly correct and easily repaired by narrowing the novelty claim. The conceptual precedents in Cochrane, Krippendorff, and computational content analysis are real, and the paper loses credibility with any methodologically alert reader by not acknowledging them. But the narrowing does not hurt the paper — it strengthens it.

**What changes**: State the genealogy. The contribution is not the concept but the specific application to AI-executor research, together with a policy implication that the precedent traditions do not draw.

**Draft text (insert as a new paragraph in §5.1 or §3.2, after the query-authorship concept is introduced)**:

> Query authorship as a concept has clear precedents in the methodological literatures that already treat pre-specified criteria as the locus of intellectual contribution. Systematic review methodology (the PRISMA tradition, and before it the Cochrane Handbook) treats the search strategy and inclusion criteria as the primary methodological artefact, independent of which reviewer executed the screening. Quantitative content analysis, in Krippendorff's formulation, treats the coding rubric as the methodological object under evaluation, independent of which coder applied it. Computational text analysis treats the training regime and labelling instructions as the reproducible specification. What I am calling query authorship is the extension of this already-established logic to a new class of executor — one with particular failure modes (sycophancy, generalization bias, non-determinism) that specific systematic practices are designed to counter. The contribution is not the concept but the extension and the policy implication: that the executor's nature changes which transparency artefacts matter and what evaluation standard they support, but does not change the underlying methodological commitment to pre-specified, externally evaluable criteria.

**Why this is enough**: It defuses the novelty attack by stating the genealogy before the critic does, and it gains argumentative weight — situating the paper in a tradition of methodological rigour is more persuasive than claiming to be outside that tradition. The paper's actual contribution (extension plus policy implication) is narrower than the draft implies but entirely defensible.

## Attack 4 — binary-policy straw man

**Assessment**: Partly correct. The field is not as stuck on the binary as §6 implies, and a reviewer who has read GAIDeT or Jones (2025) will notice. The repair is to characterise the field accurately and position the contribution as specifying the axis along which the emerging tiered approaches should be organised.

**What changes**: In §6.1, acknowledge the emerging tiered frameworks explicitly. In §6.2, narrow the target: the critique is not that policy is binary but that the dominant disclosure-based policies do not specify the axis along which tiering should be organised, and that without such specification the tiers are underdetermined.

**Draft text (replaces the opening of §6.2)**:

> Policy is not uniformly binary. The Generative AI Delegation Taxonomy (GAIDeT) and the disclosure heuristic developed by Jones (2025) both move toward graded disclosure, and Davidson and Karell (2025) specify what was done rather than whether AI was involved. The field is in transition. What these emerging approaches share with the binary policies they are beginning to displace is that they do not specify the axis along which the tiering should be organised. GAIDeT tiers by task and oversight level; Jones by disclosure content. The systematic/unsystematic distinction developed in §3 offers a different axis — one grounded in the epistemic property that matters for evaluation, namely whether errors are visible and correctable. The argument here is therefore not that policy must move from a binary to a tiered structure (that move is already underway) but that the tiering should be organised along the axis that captures the relevant epistemic difference.

**Why this is enough**: The narrowed claim is defensible and the precedent-acknowledgement gives the paper credibility rather than taking it away. The policy recommendation survives; its novelty is reduced from "we must move from a binary to a tiered framework" to "the tiering already emerging should be organised along this specific axis" — which is both more honest and more actionable.

## Attack 3 — self-demonstration fails on its own terms

**Assessment**: The sharp version of the attack is right, and §5.4 addresses the soft version only. The question is whether the sharp version requires textual intervention. I think it requires one sentence, no more.

**What changes**: The existing §5.4 concession handles the infrastructure-supports-hermeneutic-work argument. It does not handle the specific claim that query authorship, as defined, requires pre-specified criteria and that the paper's central conceptual moves were not pre-specified. Add one sentence that acknowledges this specific limit.

**Draft text (append to the §5.4 self-demonstration paragraph)**:

> One further clarification. Query authorship, as I have defined it, is the intellectual contribution encoded in pre-specified criteria. The literature-search and screening components of this paper's production satisfy that definition; the conceptual development does not, because the conceptual moves emerged during writing rather than from prior specification. The self-demonstration claim therefore holds for the literature-review component of the paper's production and not for the argumentative contribution — a limit on the self-demonstration, not on the framework, since the framework explicitly permits hermeneutic stages of work to sit on the spectrum at a different point from pre-specifiable ones (see §3.4). A fully pre-registered conceptual paper is a different genre; this is not one.

**Why this is enough**: It concedes the sharp version directly and drains the attack of rhetorical force. The critic cannot accuse you of inconsistency you have acknowledged in your own terms, and the concession costs nothing because the framework in §3.4 already permits this stance — you are simply being explicit that the stance applies to your own paper.

## What not to defend

Some of the attacks will be made in hostile forms that are not worth pre-empting — not because the attacks are wrong but because the hostile form is not the strongest form, and pre-empting the hostile form weakens the text. Specifically:

**Do not pre-empt the motivational version of any attack.** A reviewer who suggests that "query authorship" is a marketing term for selling a repackaged concept is making a motivational critique, not an argumentative one. The genealogy paragraph in Attack 2's defence answers the argumentative critique and makes the motivational one look uncharitable. Do not address the motivational version in text.

**Do not pre-empt venue fit beyond the single footnote proposed for Attack 5.** If the editor judges the paper out of scope, no amount of pre-emption helps. If the editor judges the paper in scope, the single footnote is sufficient.

**Do not over-concede on ICMJE.** The temptation, once you acknowledge the category distinction, is to weaken the whole argument. Don't — the analogical version is sufficient to carry §5.5 and §6.3, and the stronger form ("arguably stronger accountability") is the only part that needs to go.

---

## Overall

The five attacks together do not bring down the paper. They narrow its novelty claims, demote its strongest rhetorical move from a source-reading to an analogy, and require an honest accounting of the self-demonstration limit. None of these require reconceiving the argument. The pre-emptive moves proposed here preserve everything the paper actually contributes: the systematic/unsystematic distinction as the right axis, the extension of pre-specified-method logic to AI-executor research, the documentation-based policy direction, and the workflow specification itself. What goes is the over-claiming that makes those contributions appear larger than they are — and a paper that claims less but defends what it claims is, by the standards you hold yourself to, the better paper.
