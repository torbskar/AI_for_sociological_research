# Review Notes: Systematic AI Use in Sociological Research (v3)
**Reviewer:** Professor Sarah Kowalski (Political Science / Computational Methods)
**Date:** 2026-04-19

---

## 1. Two places of technical imprecision or argumentative gap

**The pre-registration analogy is undersupported at its most vulnerable point.** §3.2 and §5.1 correctly identify the disanalogy — that pre-registration involves a public, timestamped commitment while a systematic AI protocol is initially private — and then dismisses it in three moves. The dismissal is too quick on the second move, the one that matters most: "the working logs that document iterative process... address the cherry-picking risk that pre-registration's public commitment eliminates through publicity." This doesn't follow. Pre-registration's anti-cherry-picking function works because the commitment is *made before the researcher sees outcomes and is publicly locked* — the researcher cannot selectively revise it post hoc without detection. Working logs, however contemporaneously maintained, are controlled by the same researcher throughout. The researcher can still run the AI many times, observe which output is most favourable, and *then* begin logging systematically from that point forward. The logs document what was retained, not the full decision landscape. The paper treats this as an empirical risk covered by "the artefacts produced are evaluable rather than merely asserted" (§5.4), but that only helps if reviewers can assess what *wasn't* retained — which they cannot. The disanalogy here is more serious than the paper admits, and a sophisticated referee will press on exactly this.

**The structured/unstructured distinction is asserted as a binary when the paper's own argument implies a spectrum — and this creates a measurement gap.** The paper's core claim is that systematic use is epistemically distinguishable from unsystematic use, but it never specifies where on the spectrum a workflow must fall to count as systematic. §3.2 gives four defining features (explicit criteria, human verification, documented outputs, project-level configuration), but offers no guidance on partial compliance — a researcher who has explicit search criteria and documentation but inconsistent human verification checkpoints, or who documents some stages but not others. More practically: the quality of explicit criteria varies enormously. "Retain this abstract if it addresses AI use in empirical research practice" is explicit; "retain if relevant" is also technically explicit. The paper's own workflow illustration implicitly operates at the high-quality end, but nothing in the framework prevents a researcher from claiming systematic status for a low-quality instantiation. Without some graded specification — even a rough tiering — the category boundary is doing more work than the paper justifies. This also affects the policy argument in §6.3: "show us your systematic process" is tractable only if reviewers have criteria to evaluate degree of systematicity, and the paper doesn't provide them.

## 2. The post-hoc documentation problem

The paper does not adequately address this, and it's the most obvious attack surface. §5.4 briefly acknowledges that "documentation can still be faked by dishonest researchers," and §4.6 mentions that git commit timestamps make session logs "independently verifiable as contemporaneous records rather than retrospective reconstructions." The git timestamping claim is technically accurate but insufficient: git commit history is not immutable from the researcher's side. A researcher can rewrite commit history (`git rebase`, `git commit --amend`, `git filter-branch`), and unless the repository is pushed to a public or institutionally controlled remote at the time of each commit, timestamps are soft evidence at best. The paper treats the git timestamp as analogous to pre-registration timestamps, but they're not equivalent — pre-registration is controlled by a third-party server the researcher cannot modify.

What the paper should say: there are two distinct problems here, and they require different solutions. The *motivated fabricator* problem (constructing post-hoc documentation to cover dishonest use) is probably no worse under this framework than under any other, and the paper's point that fabricated documentation is at least evaluable is fair. But the *inadvertent retrospective rationalisation* problem is more insidious and more common — this is the researcher who was broadly systematic but whose documentation reflects the cleaned-up version of their process rather than the actual one. This is not dishonesty; it is exactly the Gelman-Loken garden of forking paths problem applied to documentation. The paper invokes Gelman and Loken against unsystematic users (§5.1) but never turns the same critique on the documentation process itself. The honest answer is that working logs help but don't fully solve this, and that some form of third-party timestamp — pushing logs to a remote repository continuously, or registering a workflow on OSF at project outset — is the structural fix. The paper could propose this without undermining the main argument; omitting it leaves the most obvious objection unanswered.

## 3. Where the paper undersells its contribution to heavy AI users

For researchers already using AI extensively for coding and now considering extending to synthesis and writing, the paper's most valuable contribution is §4.2's grounded synthesis design — the principle of submitting synthesis queries to a notebook containing *only* the relevant-theme documents rather than a general-purpose chatbot, so that responses are traceable to specific sources. This is the one concrete workflow decision in the paper that is non-obvious, practically actionable, and directly addresses the Peters and Chin-Yee (2025) generalisation bias problem. A researcher who already uses AI for coding knows to keep prompts specific and to verify outputs; they don't necessarily know that grounded, source-constrained synthesis is epistemically categorically different from general-purpose synthesis, and why.

The paper mentions this in one paragraph (§4.2) and moves on. It deserves more development, because this is where the systematic/unsystematic distinction has the most bite for the target reader. The analogy would be instructive here: just as a code review AI operating on the actual codebase gives different (better-calibrated, more traceable) output than a code review AI operating from a verbal description, a literature synthesis AI operating on the actual source documents gives epistemically different output than one operating from training data plus user-supplied summaries. The paper establishes the general principle but doesn't develop the mechanism for the reader most likely to push back on it.

## 4. Toward a concrete disclosure standard — does the paper move far enough?

The paper's policy recommendation, as stated, is: journals should ask "show us your systematic process" rather than "did you use AI?" That's a direction, not a standard. A concrete, actionable disclosure standard would need to specify:

1. *What materials constitute minimum documentation* — the paper lists skill files, prompt templates, search scripts, screening logs, and author-input files, but doesn't specify which are required versus optional, or which are required for which task types (literature-only vs. empirical vs. drafting-heavy).
2. *What reviewer competence is assumed* — the paper acknowledges that most current reviewers lack the familiarity to evaluate systematic AI workflows, and asserts that documentation norms create incentives to acquire this competence. That's a long-run claim; a near-term standard needs to be evaluable by current reviewers, which means it needs to be simpler than the full workflow specification.
3. *A minimum attestation requirement* — something like: "A systematic AI workflow was used; the following materials accompany this submission as a supplementary replication package: [list]. The corresponding author attests that inclusion/exclusion criteria were specified before the relevant AI tasks were run, and that human verification checkpoints were applied at [specified stages]." This is analogous to the structured ethics statement many journals now require: it doesn't require reviewers to verify everything, but it creates a specific, falsifiable attestation that can be acted on if challenged.

The paper moves to the threshold of this but stops there. The ICMJE criteria discussion in §5.5 is the strongest foundation for a positive standard — the argument that query authorship maps onto criterion 1, author-input files onto criterion 2, and contemporaneous logs onto criterion 4 is genuinely useful. But the paper doesn't convert that mapping into journal-facing language. One concrete paragraph — even a draft attestation template — would let the policy section do the work the rest of the paper builds toward. As it stands, the conclusion tells journals what question to ask but doesn't give them the instrument to ask it with.

---

*The pre-registration disanalogy and the post-hoc documentation problem are the ones a hostile referee will hit hardest. The grounded synthesis underselling is a missed opportunity with the readership most likely to adopt and champion the framework. The disclosure standard gap is fixable in revision and would significantly strengthen the paper's impact.*
