## B — Documentation ≠ verification caveat (§5.4)

**What to add:** A short paragraph distinguishing three tiers:

1. What documentation *certifies* — that criteria were prior and explicit
2. What it *makes possible but does not certify* — that AI output was accurate
3. What remains *unverifiable under any documentation regime* — non-deterministic execution, model version change

The section should frame its claims as **auditability** rather than **replication**. The model-version and git sentences (action F) are now in §5.4 but do not yet frame the whole section this way.

**Current text to look at:** §5.4 "What systematic use cannot guarantee" — the section already gestures at limits but does not make the three-tier distinction explicit.

## H — Query-authorship genealogy paragraph (§5.1 or §3.2) [ARCHIVED]

**What to add:** A paragraph acknowledging that pre-specified criteria as the locus of intellectual contribution is not a new idea. Precedents: Cochrane/PRISMA on search specification in systematic review; Krippendorff on coding rubrics in content analysis; computational text-analysis training regimes. The contribution of this paper is extension to a new executor (LLMs) with distinctive failure modes (sycophancy, generalisation bias, non-determinism) plus a specific policy implication — not a new concept.

**Why this matters:** Without this, the paper is exposed to the "generational re-description" — that the concepts being proposed have been received wisdom in adjacent fields for decades. Engaging the genealogy turns this from a vulnerability into a strength.

**Current text to look at:** §5.1, paragraph beginning "This is what *query authorship* amounts to." The genealogy paragraph should precede or follow this.

**Note:** Krippendorff (2019, *Content Analysis: An Introduction to Its Methodology*, 4th ed.) is the canonical reference. Cochrane Handbook and PRISMA are standard. These citations will need to be added to the reference list.
